Section 2.5 Word problems

¶

Word problems get a bad rap.

  • What goes where?

  • What even matters?

  • Formatting numbers (e.g., is it \(50\%\) or \(0.50\text{?}\))

  • ''I have to think!?!''

  • Show your work.

  • Problems are time-consuming.

  • Interpretation of answer often required.

But, work problems are an idealized version of most real-world problem solving; in this case we know the course or book we are studying, the unit or chapter, and the topic or section. So, it should be the case that we know roughly what to do, even if we do not exactly know how to do it mechanically.

¶

Mathematical modeling is the art, and science, of writing and studying your own word problems about interesting situations. We 1) take real-world knowledge, 2) identify, often with the help of experts, seemingly important facts and details, 3) develop a verbal or visual `story' about what is happening, 4) translate the words and diagrams to mathematical notation and formulas, and 5) study the math problem and see what we have learned. This process is visualized in Fig. \ref{fig::modeling}.

As we generate results, we compare those to the original mathematical problem for consistency, then to the details of the biological problem. If those first two stages are successful, we see if there is anything that can be said about the real-world problem. If not, we revise our assumptions, model, or analysis and try again. Here failure is expected and provides a valuable chance to learn.

Unlike when we find ourselves solving word problems, here we do not know the tool we are supposed to use. It requires a broad training with a diverse set of techniques to pursue mathematical modeling. Almost always one of these tools is a computer and an ability to edit and write computer programs.

Mathematical modeling of infectious disease Nearly every year we are faced with a newly emerged or reemerged infectious pathogen somewhere around the planet. This is a math problem, though one shared with experts from many other disciplines. During the current coronavirus outbreak, caused by the pathogen known as COVID19, mathematicians, some acting as mathematicians and others more as statisticians, have featured prominently. Mathematicians (and specialty-statisticians often referred to as epidemiologists) and others are using the best available knowledge about the virus, its origin, its transmission, and its effect on the infected, to try to understand the pace of the current outbreak and to try to understand its future.

Many tools, most with mathematical and statistical roots, are coming together to help interpret biological data (e.g., virus genetics, patient responses) and population-level `public health' data (e.g., infections across geographic borders). Any time you see a graph of cases plotted against time or deaths plotted against cases, or see the phrase \(R_0\) (the British `naught' for \(0\text{,}\) so ''R-naught''), you are seeing the results of mathematical reasoning or analysis. Specifically the quantity \(R_0\) characterizes how infectious a pathogen is expected to be and gives the expected number of cases that one infectious person might cause in a completely susceptible population.

The simplest example of a mathematical model, represented by the diagram in (((Unresolved xref, reference "fig-sir"; check spelling or use "provisional" attribute))) , tracks the amount of a population that is susceptible to infection and those that have the infection and are infectious. The Greek symbols in the diagram denote parameters and each symbol is acting as placeholder for a particular number that is important to understanding how the possible outbreak unfolds. Of the two numbers, \(\beta\) is slightly more complicated and attempts to summarize how frequently individuals interact and how likely it is that one of those interactions results in transmission; \(\gamma\) is less complicated and reflects how long a person remains infectious (i.e., it describes a `recovery rate').
