Section 2.6 Modeling with functions

¶

...

If we are ultimately interested in modeling using mathematical functions, we need to build up a catalog of mathematical functions. Before we diversify too much, we will spend a but of time looking at quadratic functions. Quadratic functions

\begin{equation*} f(x) = ax^2 + bx + c \end{equation*}

coupled with the quadratic formula

\begin{equation*} x = \dfrac{-b\pm\sqrt{b^2-4ac}}{2a} \end{equation*}

which gives the \(x\) values that solve \(f(x) = 0\text{.}\) What is surprising, perhaps, is just how often and in how many places the quadratic formula shows up as the lynchpin of so many mathematical problems. Partly this is because so many problems are ultimately studied in two dimensions. For example, we are used to visualizing so many problems in the \(xy\)-plane.

As an example, consider a new problem in which we try to solve the following system of equations,

\begin{equation*} \begin{aligned}[t] \begin{cases} 2x+3y \amp = 7\\ 5x+4y \amp = 9 \end{cases} \end{aligned} \end{equation*}

Specifically we are looking for the numbers \(x\) and \(y\) that simultaneously satisfy these equations. We could solve for one variable from one equation and substitute into the other equation to solve. Alternatively, we could solve each equation for \(y\text{,}\) graph, and look for the intersection. Proceeding in this direction,

\begin{equation*} \begin{aligned}[t] 2x+3y\amp=7\\ 3y \amp = 7 - 2x\\ y \amp = -\dfrac{2}{3}x+\dfrac{7}{3} \end{aligned} \end{equation*}

Similarly from the second equation,

\begin{equation*} \begin{aligned}[t] 5x+4y\amp=9\\ 4y \amp = 9 - 5x\\ y \amp = -\dfrac{5}{4}x+\dfrac{9}{4} \end{aligned} \end{equation*}

We could start by graphing one of these, perhaps the second, whose graph is a straight line with slope of \(m=-\sfrac{5}{4}\) and \(y\)-intercept of \(b=\sfrac{9}{4}\text{.}\) The graph of the line starts at a value of \(\sfrac{9}{4}\) on the \(y\)-axis, from there we go down \(5\) units for every \(5\) units we move to the right (we could also picture going down \(\sfrac{5}{4}\) units for every \(1\) we move to the right). We could superimpose a graph of the other and identify the intersection, which corresponds to the solution to the system of equations.

Subsection 2.6.1 Another approach

¶

...

Using some very different methods, we could rewrite the system

\begin{equation*} \begin{aligned}[t] \begin{cases} 2x+3y \amp = 7\\ 5x+4y \amp = 9 \end{cases} \end{aligned} \end{equation*}

in matrix-vector form as follows,

\begin{equation*} \left(\begin{array}{cc}2 \amp 3 \\ 5 \amp 4\end{array}\right)\left(\begin{array}{c}x\\y\end{array}\right) = \left(\begin{array}{c}7\\9\end{array}\right) \end{equation*}

To confirm that these are equal we perform matrix-vector multiplication. To recover the first equation in the system we multiply the entries in the first row by \(x\) and \(y\) and add between each step of multiplication. The result is equal to the top entry on the right-hand side, the \(7\text{.}\)

With this square arrangement of numbers, the matrix, we enter the field of linear algebra (note: very closely associated with linear algebra, especially in two dimensions, is the study of differential equations). Linear algebra plays an essential role in differential equations, and by association, so does the quadratic formula.} which is broadly concerned with techniques for solving linear systems of equations. Often we are less concerned with the solutions to linear systems and we are more concerned with properties of the matrix itself. One such property of the matrix actually requires us to calculate solutions to an associated quadratic equation using the quadratic formula. To say it again, there is a direct connection between 2D matrices (2 rows, 2 columns) and the quadratic formula.
