Section 1.4 Number systems and powers

¶

Review of tax problem

Counting we are used to counting by tens, likely using fingers. But it turns out there is potentially much more we can do with a set of hands. The 4 typical fingers on a hand each contain 3 joints which allows us to count in 3's to 12. Keeping track of those sets of 12's one at a time using the other hand allows us to count in sets of 12s from 0 to 12 (1 set of 12) to 24 (2 sets of 12), and so on up to 60 (5 sets of 12).

Subsection 1.4.1 Base-10

¶

Though the base-60 on two hands technique is quite amazing, we are much more familiar with base-10.

Example 1.4.2. \dots.

Consider the number \(46.72\) which we can understand as

\begin{equation*} \underbrace{4}_{\vlab{tens}}\underbrace{6}_{\vlab{ones}}\underbrace{.} _{\vlab{decimal}}\underbrace{7}_{\vlab{tenths}}\underbrace{2}_{\vlab{ hundredths}} \end{equation*}

In terms of powers of 10s we can think of this expanded, perhaps more mathematically, as

\begin{equation*} \begin{aligned}[t] 46.72 \amp= 4(10^1) + 6(10^0) + 7(10^{-1})+2(10^{-2})\\ \amp= 4(10) + 6(1) + 7(0.1) + 2(0.01)\\ \amp= 40 + 6 + 0.7 + 0.02\\ \amp= 46.72 \end{aligned} \end{equation*}
Checkpoint 1.4.3.

Write the number \(304\) in terms of powers of \(10\text{.}\)

Solution
This is three hundreds, zero tens, and four ones, or
\begin{equation*} \begin{aligned}[t] 304 \amp= 300 + 4\\ \amp= 3(100) + 0(10)+4(1)\\ \amp= 3(10^2) + 0(10^1) + 4(10^0) \end{aligned} \end{equation*}

Subsection 1.4.2 Application of unit conversions

¶

...

A common way to reason about numbers is to think of their role in unit conversions. For example, when measuring distance we have that

\begin{equation*} 12 \text{inches} = 1 \text{foot}. \end{equation*}

This seems to violate the idea that two sides of an equation must have an equal unit, but this identity allows us to convert between lengths or distances measured with different units. We might be interested in our pace, calculated from nice numbers, while running a 5k race.

Example 1.4.4. Race pace.

Assume it takes someone 25 minutes to run a 5k (km) race. What is the pace in minutes per mile?

Solution
We have 25 minutes per 5 km or
\begin{equation*} \dfrac{25 \text{minutes}}{5 \text{km}} \end{equation*}
At this point it will be helpful to know or remember that \(1 \text{mile} = 1.609 \text{km}\text{.}\) Combining this we have
\begin{equation*} \dfrac{25 \text{minutes}}{5 \text{km}} = \dfrac{25 \text{minutes}}{5 \cancel{\text{km}}}\times\dfrac{1.609 \ cancel{\text{km}}}{1 \text{mile}} \end{equation*}
Multiplying across numerators and denominators, this gives
\begin{equation*} \dfrac{25 \text{minutes}}{5 \text{km}} = \dfrac{25 \text{minutes}}{5 \cancel{\text{km}}}\times\dfrac{1.609 \ cancel{\text{km}}}{1 \text{mile}} = \dfrac{8.045 \text{minutes}}{1 \text{mile}} \end{equation*}
This corresponds to 8.045 minutes per mile, but taking this one step further, we can notice that \(0.045 \text{minutes}\) is the extra fractional part of a minute it takes us. Converting this to seconds we have
\begin{equation*} \dfrac{0.045 \cancel{\text{minutes}}}{1}\times\dfrac{60 \text{seconds} }{1 \cancel{\text{minute}}} = 2.7 \text{seconds} \end{equation*}
Thus, we could equivalently take our pace as
\begin{equation*} 8\!:\!03 \dfrac{\text{minutes}}{\text{mile}} \end{equation*}
Again it is important to remember that `per' means `divided by' so that minutes per mile is an amount of minutes divided by (a convenient amount, usually one of) miles.

One could very quickly ask about speed. We could calculate the reciprocal

\begin{equation*} \left(\dfrac{8.045 \text{minutes}}{1 \text{mile}}\right)^{-1} = \dfrac{1 \text{mile}}{8.045 \text{minutes}} \end{equation*}

which gives \(0.124 \dfrac{\text{miles}}{\text{minute}}\) or (since there are \(60 \text{minutes per hour}\)) a speed of \(7.458 \text{miles per hour}\text{!}\)

Additionally we can combine information with different units to solve a variety of interesting problems. Consider what use we could make of information on our car's fuel economy (e.g., miles per gallon) and our fuel costs (e.g., dollars per gallon).

\begin{equation*} \dfrac{36 \text{miles}}{1 \cancel{\text{gallon}}}\times\dfrac{1 \ cancel{\text{gallon}}}{\$4} = \dfrac{9 \text{miles}}{\$1} \end{equation*}

This means that in fuel costs alone we could expect to drive 9 miles for every dollar we spend on fuel. The idea of a reciprocal provides some additional information here as well,

\begin{equation*} \left(\dfrac{9 \text{miles}}{\$1}\right)^{-1} = \underbrace{\dfrac{\$1}{9 \text{miles}} = \dfrac{\$\sfrac{1}{9}}{\text{mile}}}_{\text{dollars per mile}} \end{equation*}

In this case it costs us about \(\$0.11 \text{per mile}\)

Subsection 1.4.3 Binary

¶

...

Powers of 2, binary numbers Since the digits in base-10 are zero through nine (but no ten), we might assume that the digits in base-2 are zero and one. Here we think of numbers as being built from positive and negative powers of 2. Positive powers give us whole numbers and negative powers contribute fractional parts. With a bit more subtlety this underlies the entirety of our digital representation of numbers.

To write a number such as \(5\text{,}\) we think of what powers of two add to the number \(5\text{,}\) here

\begin{equation*} 5 = 4 + 1 = 1\times2^2 + 0\times2^1 + 1\times2^0 \end{equation*}

Writing this in binary we have

\begin{equation*} \left(5\right)_{\text{ten}} = \left(101\right)_2 \end{equation*}

where the subscripted number tells the base. We can also use a decimal to write the fractional part in terms of powers of one-half (or negative powers of two). For example, \(\sfrac{5}{8}\) in terms of base-2 is

\begin{equation*} \begin{aligned}[t] \dfrac{5}{8} \amp= 1\times\left(\dfrac{1}{2}\right) + 0\times\left(\dfrac{1}{4}\right)+ 1\times\left(\dfrac{1}{8}\right)\\ \amp = 1\times\left(\dfrac{1}{2}\right) + 0\times\left(\dfrac{1}{2^2}\right)+ 1\times\left(\dfrac{1}{2^3}\right)\\ \amp = 1\times2^{-1} + 0\times2^{-2}+ 1\times2^{-3}\\ \amp = (0.101)_{2} \end{aligned} \end{equation*}

Putting this all together we can write \(5.625\) (or `five and five-eighths') as the binary number

\begin{equation*} (101.101)_2 \end{equation*}
