Specifically we are looking for the numbers \(x\) and \(y\) that \textit{simultaneously} satisfy these equations. We could solve for one variable from one equation and substitute into the other equation to solve. Alternatively, we could solve each equation for \(y\text{,}\) graph, and look for the intersection. Proceeding in this direction,

\begin{equation*} \begin{aligned}[t] 2x+3y\amp=7\\ 3y \amp = 7 - 2x\\ y \amp = -\dfrac{2}{3}x+\dfrac{7}{3} \end{aligned} \end{equation*}

Similarly from the second equation,

\begin{equation*} \begin{aligned}[t] 5x+4y\amp=9\\ 4y \amp = 9 - 5x\\ y \amp = -\dfrac{5}{4}x+\dfrac{9}{4} \end{aligned} \end{equation*}
