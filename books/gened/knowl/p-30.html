Fractions with integers on top and bottom are called rational numbers, or

\begin{equation*} \mathbb{Q} = \left\lbrace\sfrac{m}{n} \text{such that} m, n \in \mathbb{Z}\right\rbrace \end{equation*}

Writing a fraction in the form \(\sfrac{m}{n}\) means the same as writing it in the form \(\frac{m}{n}\text{,}\) the first just looks a bit nicer in sentences. For example,

\begin{equation*} \sfrac{2}{5}, \sfrac{3}{7} ,\sfrac{-1}{6}, \sfrac{3}{1} \end{equation*}

are examples of rational numbers. Yes, \(\sfrac{3}{1} = 3\) which suggests that integers and natural numbers are special cases of rational numbers with denominators of \(1\text{.}\)
