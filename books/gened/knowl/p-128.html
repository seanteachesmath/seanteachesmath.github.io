Using some very different methods, we could rewrite the system

\begin{equation*} \begin{aligned}[t] \begin{cases} 2x+3y \amp = 7\\ 5x+4y \amp = 9 \end{cases} \end{aligned} \end{equation*}

in matrix-vector form as follows,

\begin{equation*} \left(\begin{array}{cc}2 \amp 3 \\ 5 \amp 4\end{array}\right)\left(\begin{array}{c}x\\y\end{array}\right) = \left(\begin{array}{c}7\\9\end{array}\right) \end{equation*}

To confirm that these are equal we perform matrix-vector multiplication. To recover the first equation in the system we multiply the entries in the first row by \(x\) and \(y\) and add between each step of multiplication. The result is equal to the top entry on the right-hand side, the \(7\text{.}\)
