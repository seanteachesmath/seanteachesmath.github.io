Having seen square roots arise in solving certain algebraic problems (quadratic equations) or just certain arithmetic problems (square roots of numbers), we will pause to think about how we might actually calculate square roots of arbitrary numbers. We might know that because

\begin{equation*} 5\cdot5=25 \end{equation*}

that we can say

\begin{equation*} \sqrt{25} = 5 \end{equation*}

this is true and reflects the `principal square root'. But, we also have that

\begin{equation*} (-5)\cdot(-5) = 25 \end{equation*}

and in general say

\begin{equation*} \sqrt{25} = \pm 5 \end{equation*}

to indicate both ways of squaring a number to get \(25\text{.}\) Certain numbers we can take square roots of mentally by thinking about the repeated factors that give rise to that number, but for others we are unable to easily do so.
