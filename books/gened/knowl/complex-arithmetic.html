Consider the real number \(3\) and four successive rounds of multiplication by \(i\text{.}\)

\begin{equation*} \begin{aligned}[t] (3)(i) \amp = 3i\\ (3i)(i) \amp = 3(i^2) = 3(-1) = -3\\ (-3)i \amp = -3i\\ (-3i)(i) \amp = (-3)(i^2) = (-3)(-1) = 3 \end{aligned} \end{equation*}

In this case multiplication by \(i\) flips a the real number \(3\) back and forth between real and imaginary. Four steps of multiplication sends us back to our starting point. Notice, for example, that \(i^4 = (i^2)(i^2) = (-1)(-1) = 1\text{.}\) In fact, no matter how we break up stages of this calculation we see that \(i^4 = 1\text{.}\) So multiplying by \(i\) four times or by \(i^4\) one time both yield the same outcome.
