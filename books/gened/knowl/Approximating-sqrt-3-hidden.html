Assuming that our \(\alpha = 3\) in our hopes of approximating \(\sqrt{3}\text{,}\) we might start by guessing that \(x_0 = 1\) and noticing that the step after step \(n=0\) is step \(n+1 = 0+1 = 1\text{,}\) such that

\begin{equation*} \begin{aligned}[t] x_{0+1} \amp= \frac{1}{2}\left(x_0+\frac{3}{x_0}\right)\\ x_1 \amp= \frac{1}{2}\left(1+\frac{3}{1}\right)\\ \amp= \frac{1}{2}\left(4\right)\\ x_1 \amp = 2 \end{aligned} \end{equation*}

Now starting from \(x_1 = 2\) we take another step to calculate \(x_2\text{.}\)

\begin{equation*} \begin{aligned}[t] x_{1+1} \amp= \frac{1}{2}\left(x_1+\frac{3}{x_1}\right)\\ x_2 \amp= \frac{1}{2}\left(2+\frac{3}{2}\right)\\ \amp= \frac{1}{2}\left(\frac{7}{2}\right)\\ x_2 \amp = \frac{7}{4} = 1.75 \end{aligned} \end{equation*}

Repeating this some more (one last time in greater detail),

\begin{equation*} \begin{aligned}[t] x_3 \amp= \frac{1}{2}\left(\frac{7}{4}+\frac{3}{\sfrac{7}{4}}\right)\\ \amp= \frac{1}{2}\left(\frac{7}{4}+\frac{3}{1}\cdot\frac{4}{7}\right)\\ \amp= \frac{1}{2}\left(\frac{7}{4}+\frac{12}{7}\right)\\ \amp= \frac{1}{2}\left(\frac{7}{7}\cdot\frac{7}{4}+\frac{4}{4}\cdot\frac{12}{7 }\right)\\ \amp= \frac{1}{2}\left(\frac{49 + 48}{28}\right)\\ x_3 \amp= \frac{97}{56}\approx 1.732143 \end{aligned} \end{equation*}

Continuing this it might help to visualize the results in a table. Additionally it will no longer make sense to store exact fractions at any step, since our computers are unable to do so anyway. This does save us from having to do things like find common denominators, but does require us to write down some fairly lengthy decimal numbers at times.

Our approximation of \(\sqrt{3}\) by \(x_5\approx 1.732050807568877\) squares to

\begin{equation*} 1.732050807568877^2=2.999999999999998 \end{equation*}

which is quite impressive.
