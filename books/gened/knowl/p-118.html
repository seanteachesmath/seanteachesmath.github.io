Many tools, most with mathematical and statistical roots, are coming together to help interpret biological data (e.g., virus genetics, patient responses) and population-level `public health' data (e.g., infections across geographic borders). Any time you see a graph of cases plotted against time or deaths plotted against cases, or see the phrase \(R_0\) (the British `naught' for \(0\text{,}\) so ''R-naught''), you are seeing the results of mathematical reasoning or analysis. Specifically the quantity \(R_0\) characterizes how infectious a pathogen is expected to be and gives the expected number of cases that one infectious person might cause in a completely susceptible population.
