Section 2.1 From arithmetic to algebra

¶

Previously we discussed a situation where we know we have run for 25 minutes and covered 5 km or

\begin{equation*} \dfrac{25 \text{minutes}}{5 \text{km}} \end{equation*}

Now, say we ran other races or friends have other times at the same race. Rather than repeat the calculation at each instance, we could think of generalizing this thought process and building a formula that works for any time represented by the value of the symbol \(x\text{.}\) We would begin by

\begin{equation*} \dfrac{x \text{minutes}}{5 \cancel{\text{km}}}\times\dfrac{1.609 \ cancel{\text{km}}}{1 \text{mi}} = 0.3218x \dfrac{\text{minutes}}{\text{mi}} \end{equation*}

...

With this we would say that given a time \(x\) our pace is given by

\begin{equation*} \text{pace}= 0.3218x \dfrac{\text{minutes}}{\text{mi}} \end{equation*}

Then we could consider calculations of \textit{any} reasonable value for \(x\text{,}\) or time in minutes. For example,

">

You might notice that multiplication by \(0.3218\) is a rather specific detail and ask if, as you were running with your eye on the watch, you could do a reasonable job estimating your pace with a simpler calculation. We could argue that

\begin{equation*} 0.3218 \approx 0.333\dots = \sfrac{1}{3} \end{equation*}

noticing this we might consider

\begin{equation*} 0.3218x\approx\left(\sfrac{1}{3}\right)x \end{equation*}

and furthermore that multiplication by \(\sfrac{1}{3}\) is equivalent to division by \(3\text{.}\) We could reasonably approximate our pace by

\begin{equation*} \text{pace} = \sfrac{x}{3} \end{equation*}

which might be a bit easier than multiplying by \(0.3218\) in your tired mind.

">

It is worth pointing out that we spend most of our time in math classes (and other classes) looking at problems that we can solve exactly and to a unique answer. This is not the case in almost any real situation. Approximation is valid and valuable as a strategy, we do just need to be aware of the error we introduce and its consequences. Entire fields of advanced mathematics are devoted to such issues. The limitations of doing arithmetic on base-2 computers? That is a big part of numerical analysis. How important is a specific (and often `small') term in a great big formula? That is a big part of perturbation theory.
