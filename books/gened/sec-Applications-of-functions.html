Subsection 2.8.1 Mathematical-modeling-reprise

¶

...

When modeling spread of something infectious (a pathogen, a rumor, a behavior) we break a population into relevant groups. One of the simplest breakdowns is into groups of

  1. susceptible (these are the `healthy' people)

  2. infectious

  3. recovered

There are some subtleties here that I will mention, but not spend any more time on. The name of the group `infectious' suggests that these individuals are not only infected, but are also capable of transmitting their infection to others. The `recovered' group is sometimes called `removed' to indicate the group of individuals that are removed from the process of infection either by natural recovery or death.

The way infection is modeled is by a system of differential equations which describe how the number of individuals within each population is assumed to change over time, as individuals flow between groups. A diagram of the possible transitions is given in Figure 2.8.1.

When we try to translate these assumptions into a mathematical model we begin with

\begin{equation*} \begin{aligned}[t] \text{change in susceptible} \amp = -\text{(people getting sick)} + \text{(people losing imunity)}\\ \text{change in infectious} \amp = + \text{(people getting sick)} - \text{(people recovering)} - \text{(people dying from infection)}\\ \text{change in recovered} \amp = + \text{(people recovering)} - \text{(people losing immunity)} \end{aligned} \end{equation*}

We translate this to more mathematical language involving some calculus as follows

\begin{equation*} \begin{aligned}[t] \dfrac{dS}{dt}\amp = -\beta S I + \rho R\\ \dfrac{dI}{dt} \amp = \beta S I - \gamma I - \delta I\\ \dfrac{dR}{dt} \amp = \gamma I - \rho R \end{aligned} \end{equation*}

The specific equations are not of great importance here, but you could hopefully imagine that the number of new infections depends on the number that are susceptible to infection (\(S\)) and the number that can transmit infection (\(I\)), so in some way the amount of new infections combines those two values. Similarly, the number of individuals that recover should depend on the amount that are infectious and the number of individuals that lose immunity should depend on the number that have recovered.

The Greek letters are called `parameters'; these are symbols used to represent various numbers like the transmission parameter \(\beta\) or the recovery rate \(\gamma\) (see Table \ref{tab::epi}). Sometimes it is easier to write and study the model generically without having to worry about the precise decimal values that go in these positions, so we use the Greek letters as placeholders. This is similar to how we use the formula \(y=mx+b\) to describe a linear function between variables \(y\) and \(x\text{.}\) Whether or not we have acknowledged this before, \(m\) and \(b\) are parameters of the linear formula (`slope' and `\(y\)-intercept', respectively). Changes to these numbers might change the appearance, but do not change the fundamental nature of the relationship between \(y\) and \(x\text{.}\) ">
