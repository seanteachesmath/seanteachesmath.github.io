Section 2.1 Examples of fractals

There are an endless number of interesting fractals and in many cases a variety of mechanisms of generating a particular fractal.

Subsection 2.1.1 Typical construction

The Sierpinski triangle can be constructed by shading an inverted triangle in the middle of each white triangle.

Hopefully something about the construction strikes you as interesting. If so, what do you notice? If not, what might make this more interesting to you, personally? Connections to a specific application?

Count the numbers of each kind of triangle (black and white) at each step. Does it change predictably? Organizing your results with a table might help.

Example 2.1.3. Calculating Numbers of Triangles.

With a bit of careful guessing, it's possible to explore formulas that support these patterns and that allow us to predict what happens as the number of steps increases (beyond our drawing ability).

How can we relate the numbers of each type of triangle and the total to the step number? Looking at the white triangle numbers, you might notice that the number increases by a factor of three each step. In fact, if we let \(W_{k}\) be the number of white triangles on step \(k\text{,}\)

\begin{equation*} W_{k} =3^{k} \end{equation*}
