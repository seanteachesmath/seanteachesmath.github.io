Subsection 4.3.1 Continuous uniform

Definition 4.3.1. continuous uniform distribution.

A continuous random variable \(\displaystyle X\) has a continuous uniform distribution and is referred to as a continuous uniform random variable if and only if its probability density is given by

\begin{equation*} u(x; \alpha, \beta) = \begin{cases}\dfrac{1}{\beta - \alpha}, \amp \quad \text{ for }\alpha \lt x \lt \beta\\0, \amp \quad \text{elsewhere}\end{cases} \end{equation*}

where \(\alpha, \beta \gt 0\text{.}\)

Theorem 4.3.3. mean and variance of continuous uniform.

The mean and variance of the uniform distribution are given by

\begin{equation*} \mu = \dfrac{\alpha + \beta}{2}\text{ and }\sigma^2 = \dfrac{1}{12}(\beta-\alpha)^2 \end{equation*}
Checkpoint 4.3.4. continuous uniform moment-generating function.

Find the moment generating function of the continuous uniform distribution.

Subsection 4.3.2 Exponential family

¶

Two distributions, the exponential and chi-square, are special cases (i.e., parameterizations) of a very flexible distribution called the gamma.

We are at this point fairly familiar with the calculation of factorials. Factorials underlie our methods of counting, from permutations to combinations and partitions. For example, \(5! = 5\cdot4\cdot3\cdot2\cdot1 = 120\text{.}\) What would it mean to ask for \(0.5!\text{?}\)

The gamma function allows us to generalize our concept of the factorial to noninteger, and even many nonpositive, values.

Definition 4.3.5. gamma function.

The gamma function is given by

\begin{equation*} \Gamma(\alpha) =\int_0^\infty y^{\alpha - 1}e^{-y}\,dy \end{equation*}

where \(\alpha \gt 0\text{.}\)

Definition 4.3.8. gamma distribution.

A continuous random variable \(\displaystyle X\) has a gamma distribution and is referred to as a gamma random variable if and only if its probability density is given by

\begin{equation*} f(x; \alpha, \beta) = \begin{cases}\dfrac{1}{\beta^\alpha \Gamma(\alpha)}x^{\alpha-1}e^{-\sfrac{x}{\beta}}, \amp \quad \text{ for }0 \lt x\\0, \amp \quad \text{elsewhere}\end{cases} \end{equation*}

where \(\alpha, \beta \gt 0\text{.}\)

Theorem 4.3.10. moments about the origin of the gamma.

The \(r^{\text{th}}\) moment about the origin of the gamma distribution is given by

\begin{equation*} \mu_r' = \dfrac{\beta^r\Gamma(\alpha+r)}{\Gamma(\alpha)} \end{equation*}
Theorem 4.3.11. mean and variance of gamma.

The mean and variance of the gamma distribution are given by

\begin{equation*} \mu = \alpha\beta\text{ and }\sigma^2 = \alpha\beta^2 \end{equation*}
Theorem 4.3.12. moment-generating function of the gamma.

The moment-generating function of the gamma distribution is given by

\begin{equation*} M_X(t) = (1-\beta t)^{-\alpha} \end{equation*}
Definition 4.3.15. exponential distribution.

A continuous random variable \(\displaystyle X\) has an exponential distribution and is referred to as an exponential random variable if and only if its probability density is given by

\begin{equation*} f(x; \theta) = \begin{cases}\dfrac{1}{\theta}e^{-\sfrac{x}{\theta}}, \amp \quad \text{ for } 0 \lt x\\0, \amp \quad \text{elsewhere}\end{cases} \end{equation*}

where \(\theta \gt 0\text{.}\)

Corollary 4.3.17. mean and variance of exponential.

The mean and variance of the exponential distribution are given by

\begin{equation*} \mu = \theta \text{ and }\sigma^2 = \theta^2 \end{equation*}
Definition 4.3.18. chi-suqare distribution.

A continuous random variable \(\displaystyle X\) has a chi-square distribution and is referred to as a chi-square random variable if and only if its probability density is given by

\begin{equation*} f(x; \nu) = \begin{cases}\dfrac{1}{2^{\sfrac{\nu}{2}} \Gamma(\sfrac{\nu}{2})}x^{\frac{\nu-2}{2}}e^{-\sfrac{x}{2}}, \amp \quad \text{ for }0 \lt x\\0, \amp \quad \text{elsewhere}\end{cases} \end{equation*}

where \(\nu\) is a parameter describing the number of degrees of freedom.

Subsection 4.3.3 Beta distribution

¶

In some applications, the beta distribution (which is a probability density itself) is actually used to described the distributions of other probabilities.

Definition 4.3.19. Beta distribution.

A continuous random variable \(\displaystyle X\) has a Beta distribution and is referred to as a Beta random variable if and only if its probability density is given by

\begin{equation*} f(x; \alpha, \beta) = \begin{cases}\dfrac{\Gamma(\alpha+\beta)}{\Gamma(\alpha)\Gamma(\beta)}x^ {\alpha-1}(1-x)^{\beta-1}, \amp \quad \text{ for }0 \lt x \lt 1\\0, \amp \quad \text{elsewhere}\end{cases} \end{equation*}

where \(\alpha, \beta \gt 0\text{.}\)

Remark 4.3.20.

The function used in Example 4.2.6 was a beta distribution with \(\alpha=\beta=5\text{.}\)

Subsection 4.3.4 Normal distribution

Definition 4.3.22. Normal distribution.

A continuous random variable \(\displaystyle X\) has a normal distribution and is referred to as a normal random variable if and only if its probability density is given by

\begin{equation*} n(x; \mu, \sigma) = \dfrac{1}{\sigma\sqrt{2\pi}} e^{-\dfrac{1}{2}\left(\dfrac{x-\mu}{\sigma}\right)^2}, \quad \text{ for }-\infty \lt x \lt \infty \end{equation*}

where \(\sigma \gt 0\text{.}\)

Theorem 4.3.23. mean and variance of normal.

The mean and variance of the normal distribution are given by

\begin{equation*} \mu = \mu\text{ and }\sigma^2 = \sigma^2 \end{equation*}
Theorem 4.3.24. moment-generating function of the normal.

The moment-generating function of the normal distribution is given by

\begin{equation*} M_X(t) = e^{\mu t + \sfrac{1}{2}\sigma^2t^2} \end{equation*}
Remark 4.3.25.

The PDF is parameterized by \(\mu\) and \(\sigma = \sqrt{\sigma^2} = \sqrt{\operatorname{Var}(X)}\text{.}\)

Corollary 4.3.26.

The standard normal has \(\mu = 0\) and \(\sigma = 1\) and is given by

\begin{equation*} f(x; \mu = 0, \sigma = 1) = \dfrac{1}{\sqrt{2\pi}} e^{-\dfrac{1}{2}x^2}, \quad \text{ for }-\infty \lt x \lt \infty \end{equation*}
Remark 4.3.27.

It is the standard normal distribution for which areas under the curve given by

\begin{equation*} \int_0^z \dfrac{1}{\sqrt{2\pi}} e^{-\dfrac{1}{2}x^2}\,dx \end{equation*}

are tabulated.

Other areas can be calculated by symmetry.

Theorem 4.3.29. transforming to standard normal.

If \(X\) has a normal distribution with \(\mu\) and \(\sigma\text{,}\) then \(\displaystyle Z = \dfrac{X - \mu}{\sigma}\) has the standard normal distribution.

A few special probabilities for the standard normal are given by

  1. \(P(-\infty \lt z \lt 0) = 0.5\)

  2. \(P(0 \lt z \lt \infty) = 0.5\)

  3. \(P(-\infty \lt z \lt \infty) = 1\)

  4. \(P(-a \lt z \lt a) = 2\cdot P(0 \lt z \lt a)\)

Example 4.3.30. probabilities from the standard normal.

Find the following probabilities from the standard normal distribution.

  1. \(P(|z|\lt 0.3)\)

  2. \(P(0.3 \lt z \lt 0.6)\)

  3. \(P(0.6 \lt z)\)

Solution

To find \(P(|z|\lt 0.3)\text{,}\) we must first find \(P(0 \lt z \lt 0.3)\text{.}\) Once we have that, we can double this value by symmetry of the PDF.

Now to find \(P(|z|\lt 0.3)\text{,}\) we have the following image. By table we have that \(P(0 \lt z \lt 0.3) = 0.1179\text{,}\) so \(P(|z|\lt 0.3) = 2(0.1179) = 0.2358\text{.}\) For simplicity we will use an equality here, even though the tabulated areas are numerical approximations.

Now to find \(P(0.3 \lt z \lt 0.6)\text{,}\) we have the following image. To solve this problem we find the area from \(z=0\) to \(z=0.6\text{,}\) then subtract the area from \(z=0\) to \(z=0.3\text{.}\)

Now to find \(P(0.6 \lt z)\text{,}\) we have the following image. To solve this problem we find the area from \(z=0\) to \(z=0.6\text{,}\) then subtract this from the area under the entire right half of the curve. So \(P(0.6 \lt z)\) = 0.5 - 0.2257 = 0.2743

Theorem 4.3.35. normal approximation to the binomial.

If \(X\) has a binomial distribution with parameters \(n\) and \(\theta\text{,}\) then the moment-generating function of \(\dfrac{X-n\theta}{\sqrt{n\theta(1-\theta)}}\) approaches \(M_X(t) = \underbrace{e^{\sfrac{1}{2}t^2}}_{\text{standard\newline normal}}\) as \(n\to\infty\text{.}\)

This is because of a \(1:1\) correspondence between moment-generating functions and PMFs/PDFs and the (unstated) fact that if \(M_X(t) \to M_Y(t)\text{,}\) then \(\text{the distribution of }X \to\text{ the distribution of }Y\text{.}\)

The approximation is true as \(n\to\infty\) and can be used when both \(n\theta, n(1-\theta) \gt 5\text{.}\) This is theoretically important and was of great historical importance, but is less practically important

Since calculating densities reqires intervals, a continuity correction represents the binomial integer \(k\) by the standard normal interval \((k-\sfrac{1}{2}, k+\sfrac{1}{2})\) for the purposes of integration.
