Section 4.4 Multivariate continuous densities

¶

Though we will not specifically look at special jointly-distributed continuous random variables, we certainly could. Instead here we will focus on the fundamental definitions and properties. This will be handy for example if we someday encountered a situation with a special multivariable probability density.

Subsection 4.4.1 Multivariate distributions

¶

See Sec. 3.4. Some words.

Definition 4.4.1. joint probability density.

If \(X\) and \(Y\) are continuous random variables, the function given by \(f(x,y) = P(X=x, Y=y)\) for each pair of values \((x,y)\) within the range of \(X\) and \(Y\) is called the joint probability density of \(X\) and \(Y\text{.}\)

Theorem 4.4.2. conditions for a joint probability density.

A bivariate function can serve as a joint probability density for a pair of continuous random variables \(X\) and \(Y\) if and only if its values, \(f(x, y)\text{,}\) satisfy the conditions

  1. \(f(x, y) \ge 0\) for each pair of values \((x, y)\) within its domain;
  2. \(\int\int f(x, y) \,dy\,dx= 1\) where the double summation extends over all possible pairs \((x, y)\text{.}\)
Definition 4.4.3. joint distribution function (continuous).

If \(X\) and \(Y\) are continuous random variables, the function given by

\begin{equation*} F(x, y) = P(X \le x, Y \le y) = \int\int f(s, t) \,ds\,dt\text{ for } \infty \lt x, y \lt \infty \end{equation*}

where \(f(s, t)\) is the value of the joint probability density of \(X\) and \(Y\) at \((s, t)\text{,}\) is called the joint density function or joint cumulative density of \(X\) and \(Y\text{.}\)

Definition 4.4.4. marginal density.

If \(X\) and \(Y\) are continuous random variables and \(f(x, y)\) is the value of their joint probability density at \((x, y)\text{,}\) the function given by

\begin{equation*} g(x) = \int f(x, y)\, dy \end{equation*}

for each \(x\) within the range of \(X\) is called the marginal density of \(X\text{.}\) Correspondingly, the function given by

\begin{equation*} h(y) = \int f(x, y)\,dx \end{equation*}

for each \(y\) within the range of \(Y\) is called the marginal density of \(Y\text{.}\)

Definition 4.4.5. conditional density.

conditional density

\begin{equation*} f(x|y) = \dfrac{f(x, y)}{h(y)}, h(y)\ne 0 \end{equation*}
\begin{equation*} w(y|x) = \dfrac{f(x, y)}{g(x)}, g(x)\ne 0 \end{equation*}

Subsection 4.4.2 Product moments

¶

See section 4.6.

Definition 4.4.6. product moments about the origin.

The \(\displaystyle r^\text{th}\) and \(\displaystyle s^\text{th}\) product moment about the origin of the random variables \(\displaystyle X\) and \(\displaystyle Y\text{,}\) denoted by \(\displaystyle \mu_{r,s}\text{,}\) is the expected value of \(\displaystyle X^rY^s\text{;}\) symbolically

\begin{equation*} \mu_{r,s}'=E[X^rY^s] = \int\int x^r y^s\cdot f(x, y)\,dy\,dx \end{equation*}

\(\displaystyle r = 0,1,2, \dots\) and \(\displaystyle s = 0,1,2, \dots\) when \(\displaystyle X\) and Y are discrete.

Special cases of product moments are \(\displaystyle \mu_{1,0}' = E[X^1Y^0] = E[X] = \mu_X\) and \(\displaystyle \mu_{0,1}' = E[X^0Y^1] = E[Y] = \mu_Y\text{.}\)

Definition 4.4.7. product moments about the mean.

The \(\displaystyle r^\text{th}\) and \(\displaystyle s^\text{th}\) product moment about the mean of the random variables \(\displaystyle X\) and \(\displaystyle Y\text{,}\) denoted by \(\displaystyle \mu_{r,s}'\text{,}\) is the expected value of \(\displaystyle (X-\mu_X)^r(Y-\mu_Y)^s\text{;}\) symbolically

\begin{equation*} \mu_{r,s}=E[(X-\mu_X)^r(Y-\mu_Y)^s] = \int\int (x-\mu_X)^r (y-\mu_Y)^s\cdot f(x, y)\,dy\,dx \end{equation*}

\(\displaystyle r = 0,1,2, \dots\) and \(\displaystyle s = 0,1,2, \dots\) when \(\displaystyle X\) and Y are discrete.

Subsection 4.4.6 Conditional expectation

¶

See section 4.8.

Definition 4.4.8. conditional expectation.

If \(\displaystyle X\) is a discrete random variable and \(\displaystyle f(x|y)\) is the value of the conditional probability distribution of \(\displaystyle X\) given \(\displaystyle Y = y\) at \(\displaystyle x\text{,}\) the conditional expectation of \(\displaystyle u(X)\) given \(\displaystyle Y = y\) is

\begin{equation*} E[u(X)|y] = \int u(x)\cdot f(x|y)\,dx \end{equation*}

and the conditional expectation of \(\displaystyle v(Y)\) given \(\displaystyle X = x\) is

\begin{equation*} E[v(Y)|x] = \int v(y)\cdot w(y|x)\,dy \end{equation*}
Definition 4.4.9. conditional mean.

If \(\displaystyle X\) is a discrete random variable and \(\displaystyle f(x|y)\) is the value of the conditional probability distribution of \(\displaystyle X\) given \(\displaystyle Y = y\) at \(\displaystyle x\text{,}\) the conditional mean of \(\displaystyle u(X) = X\) given \(\displaystyle Y = y\) is

\begin{equation*} \mu_{X|y} = E[X|y] = \int x\cdot f(x|y)\,dx \end{equation*}

and the conditional mean of \(\displaystyle v(Y) = Y\) given \(\displaystyle X = x\) is

\begin{equation*} \displaystyle \mu_{Y|x} = E[Y|x] = \int y\cdot w(y|x)\,dy \end{equation*}
Definition 4.4.10. conditional variance.

If \(\displaystyle X\) is a discrete random variable and \(\displaystyle f(x|y)\) is the value of the conditional probability distribution of \(\displaystyle X\) given \(\displaystyle Y = y\) at \(\displaystyle x\text{,}\) the conditional variance of \(\displaystyle X\) given \(\displaystyle Y = y\) is

\begin{equation*} \sigma^2_{X|y} = E[(X-\mu_{X|y})^2|y] = E[X^2]-\mu^2_{X|y} \end{equation*}

and the conditional expectation of \(\displaystyle Y\) given \(\displaystyle X = x\) is

\begin{equation*} \displaystyle\sigma^2_{Y|x} = E[(Y-\mu_{Y|x})^2|y] = E[Y^2]-\mu^2_{Y|x} \end{equation*}
