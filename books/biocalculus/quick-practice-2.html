Subsection 4.1 Quick practice

  1. In Question [cross-reference to target(s) "quick::area" missing] of the previous section, we talked about the relationship between area and radius. Is that relationship linear? Why or why not? [quick::quadratic]

  2. In Question [cross-reference to target(s) "quick::area" missing] of the previous section, we talked about the relationship between radius and diameter. Is that relationship linear? Why or why not? [quick::linear]

  3. (This one might not be so quick practice.) In chemistry we use the Lineweaver-Burk or ‘double-reciprocal’ plot to determine properties of a chemical reaction. We graph a surprisingly complicated looking, but still linear, relationship

    \begin{equation*} \dfrac{1}{V} = \dfrac{K_m}{V_{max}}\dfrac{1}{[S]} + \dfrac{1}{V_{max}} \end{equation*}

    where \(V\) is the reaction rate or ‘velocity’, \([S]\) is the substrate concentration, and \(V_{max}\) and \(K_m\) are parameters in the relationship between our original variables \(V\) and \([S]\text{.}\) Solve for \(V\) (not the reciprocal of \(V\)) as a function of \([S]\text{.}\)[quick::doublerecip]

  4. Roughly speaking, the phrase ‘is proportional to’ means what?[quick::propto]

Answers:

  1. In Question [cross-reference to target(s) "quick::area" missing] of the previous section, we talked about the relationship between area and radius, \(A = \pi r^2\text{.}\) This is not linear, since we must square the radius to get the area.

  2. In Question [cross-reference to target(s) "quick::area" missing] of the previous section, we talked about the relationship between radius and diameter. This is linear since \(r = \dfrac{d}{2}\text{,}\) in fact this is proportional since the radius is a constant multiple \(\left(\dfrac{1}{2}\right)\) of the diameter. This is a good reminder that dividing by two is the same as multiplying by one half.

  3. In solving for \(V\) (steps not shown), we should eventually come upon the formula \(V=\dfrac{V_{max}[S]}{K_m+[S]}\text{.}\) This an example of a type of function we will study in more detail later this semester.

  4. Roughly speaking, the phrase ‘is proportional to’ means ‘is a multiple of’.
