Section 4 Linear functions and their graphs

¶

Linear functions are the basis for most relationships that we can think about.

Definition 4.1.

A relationship \(f(x)\) defined by either

  1. \(y = f(x) = mx + b\text{,}\) with constants \(m\) and \(b\text{,}\) or

  2. \(y=f(x)=y_0+m(x−x_0)\text{,}\)with constant \(m\) and a point\((x_0, y_0)\)

is called a linear function between the variables \(x\) and \(y\text{.}\) If the formula involves only adding and subtracting constants with variables multiplied by constants, the equation describes a linear function.

Definition 4.2.

The slope of a linear function passing through two points \((x_0, y_0)\) and \((x_1,y_1)\) is defined as the ratio of the change in output over the change in input ("rise over run”).

In general, in biological applications we rarely have inputs/outputs called \(x\) and \(y\) – for example we have mass and volume. These labels, while different from what we are used to are meant to make problems more meaningful!

Example 4.3. Temperature conversion.

Here we will derive a common (and commonly mis-remembered) temperature formula.

  1. Recall: What is the formula for computing the temperature in F if you know temperature in C?

  2. Data: Consider the following data: \((0, 32), (20, 68), (37, 98.6), (100, 212)\text{.}\)

  3. Plot the points to determine whether a linear function holds.

  4. Slope: Identify a pair of points and compute the slope.

  5. Linear functions: Now write \(F(C)\text{,}\) first in point-slope form, then simplify to slope-intercept form.

Example 4.4. slope to point-slope to slope intercept.
Notice that the definition
\begin{equation*} m=\dfrac{y_1 − y_0}{x_1 − x_0} \end{equation*}
gives us all we need to derive both point-slope form and slope-intercept form. Let our point be \((x_0,y_0)\) and instead of \((x_1,y_1)\text{,}\) choose \((x,y)\) to be any other point on the line.
\begin{equation*} \begin{aligned}[t] m \amp =\dfrac{y − y_0}{x − x_0}\\ m(x-x_0) \amp =\dfrac{y − y_0}{\cancel{x − x_0}}\cancel{(x-x_0)}\\ m(x-x_0) \amp = y − y_0\\ y_0 + m(x-x_0) \amp = y\\ y \amp = y_0 + m(x-x_0) \end{aligned} \end{equation*}
Example 4.5. Recognizing linear functions.
Identify which of the following are examples of linear functions.

  1. \(C(F)= \frac{5}{9}(F−32)\)

  2. \(s(t) = −4.9t^2 − 5.0t + 100\)

  3. \(M(V)=0.8V\)

Here we look at the onset of AIDS symptoms in the years after initial HIV infection. Four years after infection \(17%\) had symptoms, and seven years after infection \(33\%\) had symptoms.

  1. Write the formula for a linear function that relates the percent of patients with AIDS symptoms to time (years measured since infection).

  2. Define and interpret the slope and intercept (units included).

  3. How many patients (what percent) have symptoms after five and a half years?
