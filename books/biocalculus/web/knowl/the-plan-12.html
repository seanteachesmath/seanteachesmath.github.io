We will learn and practice direct methods for computing derivatives of functions defined by products or quotients of terms.

Common products and ratios in science.

List a few common measurements that are defined as products or quotients based on other measurements.

Given the product \(p(x)=(x-3)(x+2)\text{,}\) find \(p'(x)\text{.}\)

The mass \(M(t)\) (in grams) of a fruit is given by the product of its density \(\rho(t)\) (in grams per cubic cm) and its volume \(V(t)\) (cubic cm),
\begin{equation*} M(t) = \rho(t)V(t). \end{equation*}
Assume that the density is decreasing according to \(\rho(t) = 1-0.1t\text{,}\) but the volume grows according to \(V(t) = 10+12t\text{.}\) Since \(\rho(t)\) decreases (a line with a negative slope) and \(V(t)\) increases, is it obvious how \(M(t)\) should behave?
  1. Find the instantaneous rate of change at any time \(t\) (the derivative) of \(M(t)\text{,}\) called \(M'(t)\text{.}\)
  2. Is the mass initially increasing or decreasing as a function of time?
  3. At what time does \(M(t)\) change behavior?
Hill functions are an important family of functions used to describe biological response to a stimulus. They were first used in 1919 (by Archibald Hill) to study and describe the cooperative binding kinetics of oxygen to hemoglobin molecules. They are defined as
\begin{equation*} h(x)=\displaystyle\frac{x^n}{1+x^n}, \quad n>0 \end{equation*}
Find the derivative of the Hill function with \(n=2\text{.}\)
For the function above compute leading behaviors at zero and infinity, as well as limits at zero and infinity.
The number of molecules \(n(t)\) in a solution is the concentration of molecules \(c(t)\) times the volume of the solution \(v(t)\text{:}\)
\begin{equation*} n(t)=c(t)v(t). \end{equation*}
Assume the concentration decreases, \(c(t)=4-t^{2}\text{,}\) and the volume increases, \(v(t)=1+3t\text{.}\)
  1. Is the total number of molecules initially increasing or decreasing?
  2. At what time does \(n(t)\) change behavior?
Consider a population of mice that obeys the equation \(N(t)=1+2t+t^{2}\text{.}\) The number of mice with hantavirus is described by \(I(t)=3t\text{,}\) (\(t\) in months). The prevalence of hantavirus in the population is defined as
\begin{equation*} p(t)=\displaystyle\frac{I(t)}{N(t)} \end{equation*}
How does the prevalence of hantavirus in the population change with time?
For the functions above (that is \(I(t)\text{,}\) \(N(t)\text{,}\) and \(p(t)\)) compute leading behaviors at zero and infinity, as well as limits at zero and infinity. What is surprising about the results of \(\displaystyle \lim_{t\to\infty} p(t)\) and \(\displaystyle \lim_{t\to\infty} I(t)\text{?}\)
