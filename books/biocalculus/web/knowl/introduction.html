Change.

Change, key to science (and calculus), is conveyed by the . When we talk about change it is helpful to note,
  • \(\Delta\) is mathematical shorthand for change in a variable:\(\Delta t\) typically means change in time \(t\text{,}\)\(\Delta x\) typically means change in variable \(x\text{,}\) and so on
  • we often talk about rates of change
Example 2.1.1. Rates of change.
  • average rate of change:
    \begin{equation*} \text{average rate of change} = \frac{\hspace{5cm}}{\hspace{5cm}} = \frac{\text{change in }\hspace{1cm}}{\text{change in }\hspace{1cm}} = \frac{\hspace{1cm}}{\hspace{1cm}} \end{equation*}

Lines, secant lines, and rates of change.

When talking about the average rate of change of a measurement we compute the ratio of change in output over change in input (often time). This ratio is also the slope of a line that we call the secant line.
The secant line connects two points that lie on the graph of a function. We call one of the points the base point.
When we pick our two points on a graph, we are still computing the slope of a line through these points (even though it has a new name – the secant line).
