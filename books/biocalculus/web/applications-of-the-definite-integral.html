Section 3.4 Applications of the definite integral

The Plan.

We look at a few applications of the definite integral.

Given that \(f(x)\) and \(g(x)\) are continuous and that \(f(x)\geq g(x)\) on the interval from \(x=a\) to \(x=b\text{,}\) the area between the curves \(f(x)\) and \(g(x)\) from \(x=a\) to \(x=b\) is given by

\begin{equation*} \int_a^b \left(f(x) - g(x)\right)\,dx \end{equation*}

‘Degree-days’ are used as a way to measure growth and development in plants and other temperature-dependent species, such as insects.

Find the area between the curves \(f(x)=x^{2}\) and \(g(x)=x^{3}\) between \(x=0\) and \(x=1\text{.}\)

The average value for a function \(f(x)\) on the interval from \(x=a\) to \(x=b\) is

\begin{equation*} \boxed{\text{average value of $f$} = \hat f = \hspace{2cm}\phantom{\frac{1}{b-a}\int_a^b f(x)\, dx}} \end{equation*}

An oil company describes the rate of change in consumption of oil resources (in billions of barrels) using the equation \(\displaystyle \frac{dc}{dt} = 1.2e^{0.04t}\) with \(t=0\) indicating the year 1998.

  1. Using a definite integral, compute the total resource consumption over the first ten years since 1998.Note: Be sure to transform the limits of integration if you use \(u\)-substitution!

  2. What is the average rate of consumption over the period?

  3. Repeat these two steps for the next ten years, the period from 2008 to 2018.

In 1998, the above company estimated 30 billion barrels in the reserve. Compute the definite integral that gives the total consumption over the first \(T\) years since 1998,

\begin{equation*} \int_{0}^T\,1.2e^{0.04t}\,dt, \end{equation*}

and use the resulting formula to find how many years it will take for the 30 billion barrel supply to be exhausted. Note: Set the result of the definite integral equal to 30 and solve for \(T\text{.}\)

Justification – average value formula.

Consider the following situation as a brief justification for the average value formula quoted above.

During an experiment to test the effects of a dietary supplement on growth of quail, a researcher takes weekly measurements of the mass of a bird in ounces.

What is the average mass of the bird during the study?

A problem.

The mass of the animal varies continuously with time according to some function \(m(t)\text{.}\) What if we were to choose different times for our measurement? How would our average mass change? What other methods might we have to compute the average mass over an interval?

Consider the function \(m(t) = -t^{2}+6t + 6\) that describes the mass of the quail during the growth experiment. Use the definite integral to calculate the average mass of the bird during the study? How does this compare to your earlier results?

Exercises Quick practice

1.

Compute the average value of \(f(x) = x\) on the interval from \(x=0\) to \(x=2\text{.}\)

Solution.

The value of the definite integral is \(\displaystyle\int_{0}^{2} x\,dx = \left.\left(\dfrac{1}{2}x^{2}\right)\right|_{0}^{2} = \dfrac{1}{2}(2^{2}) - \dfrac{1}{2}(0^{2}) = 2\text{.}\) The average value is given by

\begin{equation*} \hat f = \dfrac{1}{2-0}\int_{0}^{2} x\,dx = \dfrac{1}{2}\left(2\right) = 1 \end{equation*}

Graph this and think about the areas of the corresponding shapes.

2.

Compute the average value of \(g(x) = x-x^{2}\) on the interval from \(x=0\) to \(x=1\text{.}\)

Solution.

The value of the definite integral is \(\displaystyle\int_{0}^1 x-x^{2}\,dx = \dfrac{1}{6}\text{.}\) The average value is given by

\begin{equation*} \hat g = \dfrac{1}{1-0}\int_{0}^1 x-x^{2}\,dx = \dfrac{1}{1}\left(\dfrac{1}{6}\right) = \dfrac{1}{6} \end{equation*}

Graph this and think about the areas of the corresponding shapes.
