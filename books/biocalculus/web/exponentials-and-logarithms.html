    Theorem 1.5.1. Properties of exponents.

    Consider the following laws for for real numbers \(a\text{,}\) \(m\text{,}\) and \(n\text{.}\)
    1. \(\displaystyle a^{m}\cdot a^{n} = a^{m+n}\)
    2. \(\displaystyle (a^n)^m = a^{nm}\)
    3. \(\displaystyle a^{-n} = \frac{1}{a^{n}}\)
    4. \(\displaystyle \frac{a^n}{a^m} = a^{n-m}\)
    5. \(\displaystyle a^1 = a\)
    6. \(\displaystyle a^0 = 1\)
    Common bases in science are
    • \(10\) throughout science and nature
    • \(e\) throughout science
    • \(2\) throughout computer science
    Though we think of exponents as ‘multiplying \(a\)’s together’ certain instance only make sense if we use the above laws. How can we multiply factors of \(2\) together \((-3)\) times?

    Exercises Quick practice

    1.

    Give a few examples of measurements, relationships, or patterns defined by logarithmic or exponential functions.[quick-exp_log_meas]
    Solution.
    Good examples of measurements, relationships, or patterns that are defined using logarithmic or exponential relationships are the pH scale, the intensity and magnitude scales for earthquakes, the decibel scale for sound, and the increasing of computer RAM (in powers of two).

    2.

    Simplify \(\dfrac{2^7}{2^{4}}\text{.}\)
    Solution.
    Simplifying \(\dfrac{2^7}{2^{4}}\) we find \(\dfrac{2^7}{2^{4}} = 2^{7-4} = 2^{3} = 8\text{.}\)

    3.

    Simplify \(\ln\left(\dfrac{1}{2}\right)\text{.}\)
    Solution.
    Simplifying \(\ln\left(\dfrac{1}{2}\right)\) using a variety of rules we find \(\ln\left(\dfrac{1}{2}\right) = \ln\left(2^{-1}\right) = -1\ln(2) = -\ln(2)\text{.}\) The value of \(\ln(2)\approx0.6931\) is a popular one in many life science applications.
    You might occasionally see (as below) an axis with numbers of the form \(3\text{,}\) \(10^{3}\text{,}\) \(1,000\text{.}\) The \(3\) refers to the base 10 log of the value, \(10^{3}\) refers to the actual value in scientific notation, and \(1000\) is just the normal value. Only in the first are we actually transforming the data.
