Section 17 More complicated integrals and integration by substitution

The Plan..

We continue our work on integration by introducing exponential and logarithmic functions, as well as those involving compositions.

Evaluating complicated integrals..

We have learned a lot of techniques of differentiation. We will only learn how to undo a few of these, such as those derivatives involving \(e^x\text{,}\) \(\ln(x)\text{,}\) and the chain rule. For example, we omit the method required to ‘undo’ derivatives computed with the product rule.

Derivatives of \(e^x\) and \(\ln(x)\text{.}\).

Recall from our earlier work that \(\displaystyle\frac{d}{dx}(e^x)=e^x\) and \(\displaystyle\frac{d}{dx}(\ln{(x)})=\frac{1}{x}\text{.}\)What consequences do these facts have when we approach this problem from integration? Our new approach is to ask ‘what (unknown) function has a derivative that matches the integrand?’ rather than to ask ‘what is the derivative of the (known) function?’ In other words, ‘where did this derivative come from?’

Facts about \(e^x\) and \(\ln(x)\text{.}\).

Since the indefinite integral undoes the action of the derivative

\begin{equation*} \boxed{\int e^x \, dx=e^x +c} \end{equation*}

and

\begin{equation*} \boxed{\int \frac{1}{x} \, dx=\ln{|x|} +c} \end{equation*}

Compute \(\displaystyle\int 3e^x+\frac{1}{x}+x^2 \, dx\text{.}\)

Compute \(\displaystyle\int \displaystyle\frac{2}{x} +\frac{1}{4}e^x+\frac{1}{x^4} \, dx\text{.}\)

Compute \(\displaystyle\int 15e^{5x}\, dx\text{.}\) Taking the derivative of what function gives us \(f(x) = 15e^{5x}\text{?}\)

Undoing differentiation by the Chain Rule..

Recall that the Chain Rule says that

\begin{equation*} \boxed{\displaystyle\frac{d}{dx}\Big(f\circ g\Big)=\frac{d}{dx}\Big(f(g(x))\Big)=\frac{df}{dg}\frac{dg}{dx}} \end{equation*}

We can undo the chain rule using integration by a substitution process as well.

Before we begin, we need a brief definition for the differential \(du\text{.}\) For \(u=g(x)\text{,}\) the differential is defined as

\begin{equation*} \boxed{du = g'(x)\,dx} \end{equation*}

This is often called ‘\(u\)-substitution’ since we think of \(u\) as the name assigned to the inside function \(g(x)\) from the Chain Rule. To outline the method we will think of integrating with respect to the variable \(x\text{,}\) though where you see an \(x\) below you can imagine another variable such as \(t\text{.}\)

  1. Define a new variable, called \(u=g(x)\text{,}\) as some function of the old variable \(x\text{.}\)

  2. Compute the differential \(du = g'(x)\,dx\text{.}\)

  3. Put everything in the integral in terms of the new variable, \(u\text{.}\)(Replace the ‘inside function’ by the letter \(u\) and replace what remains by the definition for \(du\text{.}\)) Sometimes we have to rearrange a bit here so that \(du\) fits back in the integral.

  4. Try to integrate in terms of the new variable, \(u\text{.}\)

  5. After integrating, try to put everything back in terms of the old variable \(x\text{.}\)

Find \(\displaystyle\int 2xe^{x^2} \, dx\text{.}\)

Evaluate \(\displaystyle\int \frac{e^{3t}}{(1+e^{3t})} \, dt\text{.}\)

Warning..

Integration by substitution does not always work!

Try to evaluate \(\displaystyle\int 2x^2e^{x^2} \, dx\text{.}\)

The growth of female Atlantic sturgeon (fish) in the Hudson River, New York, approximately follows the differential equation

\begin{equation*} \displaystyle\frac{dL}{dt}=19.56e^{-0.074t} \end{equation*}

with \(L(0)=14.65\) (age in yr, length in cm, measured from hatching). Solve this differential equation.

Female sturgeon mature and begin to reproduce when they are roughly \(191\) cm long. How long does it take the female sturgeon to mature?

Subsection 17.1 Quick practice

  1. Compute \(\displaystyle\int 3e^{7x}\,dx\text{.}\)

  2. Compute \(\displaystyle\int \frac{1}{2x}\,dx\text{.}\)

  3. Compute \(\displaystyle\int 3e^{7x}+\frac{1}{2x}+x^2\,dx\text{.}\)

  4. In a \(u\)-substitution problem, if \(u = f(x) = 3x^2+2x\text{,}\) what is \(du\text{?}\)

Answers:

  1. The indefinite integral \(\displaystyle\int 3e^{7x}\,dx\) evaluates to \(\displaystyle\int 3e^{7x}\,dx = \dfrac{3}{7}e^{7x}+c\text{.}\)

  2. The indefinite integral \(\displaystyle\int \frac{1}{2x}\,dx\) evaluates to \(\displaystyle\int \frac{1}{2x}\,dx = \dfrac{1}{2}\int \frac{1}{x}\,dx = \dfrac{1}{2}\ln|x|+c\text{.}\)

  3. The indefinite integral \(\displaystyle\int 3e^{7x}+\frac{1}{2x}+x^2\,dx\) evaluates to \(\displaystyle\int 3e^{7x}+\frac{1}{2x}+x^2\,dx = \dfrac{3}{7}e^{7x}+\dfrac{1}{2}\ln|x|+\dfrac{1}{3}x^3+c\text{.}\)

  4. In a \(u\)-substitution problem, if \(u = f(x) = 3x^2+2x\text{,}\) then \(du\) is \(du = f'(x)dx = (6x+2)dx\text{.}\)
