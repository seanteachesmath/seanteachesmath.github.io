Prediction.

An important part of mathematics and science is describing the mechanisms by which measurements change and making a testable prediction.

Approach: Discrete-time dynamical systems.

Used to predict measurements made at discrete time intervals (e.g., measurements made once per hour, day, week). Consider a measurement \(m_t\) made at time \(t\text{,}\) to define a discrete-time dynamical system (DTDS) we write

\begin{equation*} m_{t+1} = f(m_t). \end{equation*}

Definition – solution.

The sequence of values \(m_t\) for \(t = 0, 1, 2, \dots\) is the solution of the discrete-time dynamical system \(m_{t+1} = f(m_t)\) starting from initial condition \(m_{0}\text{.}\) We sometimes graph the solution as a plot of points against time.

For our yeast growth predictions, example initial conditions above were \(y_{0}=100\) mm\({}^{2}\) and \(y_{0}=10\) mm\({}^{2}\text{.}\) Using the updating function, compute five terms of the solution starting from each of these initial conditions.

A graphical computer.

We have a powerful technique for studying the behavior of such models using only a pencil and paper. This technique is called cobwebbing, and its name will be come clear shortly.

Note: you often will not have the data points on this graph, so they have been removed.This is how we ‘cobweb’:

  1. Graph the updating function and the diagonal (the identity or line \(y=x\))

  2. Start at initial condition and go up or down to the updating function and over to the diagonal

  3. Repeat for as many steps as needed to find the pattern

  4. Sketch the solution at times \(t=0, 1, 2, \dots\)

A point \(m^*\) is called an equilibrium of the DTDS

\begin{equation*} m_{t+1} = f(m_t) \end{equation*}

if \(f(m^*) = m^*\) (i.e., if updating by the DTDS leaves the input \(m^*\) unchanged).

  • Find equilibria (more than 1 equilibrium) graphically by locating the points where the graphs of the updating function and diagonal intersect.

  • Find equilibria algebraically by setting \(f(m^*) = m^*\) and solving for \(m^*\) .
