First order - revisited.

Just like before, but now with a power of \(n = 1\) on the right-hand side, we have

\begin{equation*} \dfrac{dA}{dt} \amp = -k A\text{.} \end{equation*}

Our goal will be to move all of the \(A\)-like quantities to the left-hand side and leave any constants on the right-hand side. This turns out to give some evidence in support of a well-known technique in differential equations called separation of variables. We proceed as follows, first dividing by \(A\) on each side, then integrating each side with respect to \(t\text{,}\)

\begin{equation*} \begin{aligned}[t] \dfrac{dA}{dt} \amp = -k A\\ \dfrac{1}{A}\cdot\dfrac{dA}{dt} \amp = -k\\ \int \dfrac{1}{A}\cdot\dfrac{dA}{dt}\,dt \amp = \int -k\, dt \end{aligned} \end{equation*}

This is somewhat abstract, but we can immediately apply \(u\)-substitution. To do so, we have to recognize that the unknown function is \(u = A(t)\) and that

\begin{equation*} du = A'(t)\,dt\text{.} \end{equation*}

Now, this isn't immediately useful, but remembering our notation

\begin{equation*} A'(t) = \dfrac{dA}{dt}\text{,} \end{equation*}

so we can write,

\begin{equation*} du = \dfrac{dA}{dt}\,dt\text{.} \end{equation*}

Suddenly we are finished with our process of \(u\)-substittution and can integrate. Continuing from above

\begin{equation*} \begin{aligned}[t] \int\underbrace{\frac{1}{A}}_{\frac{1}{u}}\cdot\underbrace{\frac{dA}{ dt}\,dt}_{du} \amp = \int -k\, dt\\ \int \frac{1}{u}\,du \amp = \int -k\,dt\\ \ln|u| \amp = -kt+c \end{aligned} \end{equation*}

Remembering above that \(u = A(t)\) we can return to our chemistry notation, that

\begin{equation*} \ln|A(t)| = -kt+c\text{.} \end{equation*}

What is left is to specify a value for \(c\text{.}\) Thinking of our physical situation we have that \(A(0) = A_{0}\) (again as a chemist you might be picturing the quantity \([A]_{0}\text{,}\) but this bracket notation is somewhat unsatisfying mathematically). This gives

\begin{equation*} \ln|A(0)| = \ln|A_{0}| = -k(0) + c\text{,} \end{equation*}

or \(c = \ln|A_{0}|\text{.}\) Since we expect \(A_{0} \gt 0\text{,}\) we can neglect the absolute value bars in favor of notational simplicity. This gives the zeroth-order integrated rate law,

\begin{equation*} \ln(A(t)) = -kt+\ln(A_{0})\text{.} \end{equation*}

Under these assumptions we expect the concentration of chemical species \(A\) to decay linearly as a function of time with a slope of \(-k\text{,}\) beginning from an initial concentration of \(A_{0}\text{.}\)

In this case, the plot of the natural logarithm of the observed concentration will resemble a decreasing linear function.

We may also be aware that a first-order reaction corresponds to exponential decay, in fact, exponentiating both sides of the formula above gives

\begin{equation*} \begin{aligned}[t] \ln(A(t)) \amp = -kt+\ln(A_{0})\\ e^{\ln(A(t))} \amp = e^{-kt+\ln(A_{0})}\\ A(t) \amp = e^{-kt}\cdot e^{\ln(A_{0})}\\ A(t) \amp = e^{-kt}A_{0}\\ A(t) \amp = A_{0}e^{-kt}\\ \end{aligned} \end{equation*}

Historically it was important to express data in terms of linear relationships, so a plot of the natural logarithm of a measurement that gave rise to a linear trend was a good indicator of this relationship and estimation of the slope \(k\) was relatively easy. In contemporary times it is relatively easy to 'fit' nonlinear functions like exponentials, so it would be reasonable to plot the raw data, not its natural logarithm, and look the underlying exponential relationship and its parameter \(k\text{.}\)
