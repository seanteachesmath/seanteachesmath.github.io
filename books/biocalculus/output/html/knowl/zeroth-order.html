Zeroth order.

Within this family, the first formula comes from the zeroth order reaction given by

\begin{equation*} \dfrac{dA}{dt} = -kA^0. \end{equation*}

Since this contains \(A^0 = 1\text{,}\) the right-hand side simplifies to the constant \(-k\text{.}\) At this point, we are looking for an unknown function \(A(t)\) whose derivative is the constant \(-k\text{.}\) Thinking about this from the perspective of differentiation, we are looking for a function \(A(t)\) whose derivative is a constant. This means that is linear and that constant, \(-k\text{,}\) is its slope. So,

\begin{equation*} A(t) = -kt+A_{0}, \end{equation*}

where \(A_{0}\) is the constant of integration and gives the initial concentration of the reactant.
