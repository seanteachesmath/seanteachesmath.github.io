The Plan.

We continue our work on integration by introducing an application to autonomous differential equations via the integrated rate laws - a cornerstone of chemical kinetics.

Background.

The integrated rate laws exist at the interface of calculus and chemistry. By studying these equations from a calculus perspective, the results are much more cohesive than it might first appear. Often "we" are interested in the change in concentration of a particular chemical species. Here let,

\begin{equation*} A(t) = [A]_{t} = [A] \text{ at time }t =\text{ concentration of species }A\text{ at time }t. \end{equation*}

To be consistent with class notation and objectives, we will use \(A(t)\) to refer to the concentration.

Zeroth order.

Within this family, the first formula comes from the zeroth order reaction given by

\begin{equation*} \dfrac{dA}{dt} = -kA^0. \end{equation*}

Since this contains \(A^0 = 1\text{,}\) the right-hand side simplifies to the constant \(-k\text{.}\) At this point, we are looking for an unknown function \(A(t)\) whose derivative is the constant \(-k\text{.}\) Thinking about this from the perspective of differentiation, we are looking for a function \(A(t)\) whose derivative is a constant. This means that is linear and that constant, \(-k\text{,}\) is its slope. So,

\begin{equation*} A(t) = -kt+A_{0}, \end{equation*}

where \(A_{0}\) is the constant of integration and gives the initial concentration of the reactant.

First order.

Since the differential equation is

\begin{equation*} \dfrac{dA}{dt} = -kA\text{,} \end{equation*}

we can guess the solution by answering the question, "what function has a derivative that is a constant times itself?". Since

\begin{equation*} \dfrac{d}{dt}\left(e^{-kt}\right) = -ke^{-kt}\text{,} \end{equation*}

It turns out that multiplying by a constant we have

\begin{equation*} \dfrac{d}{dt}\left(A_{0}e^{-kt}\right) = -A_{0}ke^{-kt} = -k\underbrace{A_{0}e^{-kt}}_{A(t)} \end{equation*}
First order - revisited.

Just like before, but now with a power of \(n = 1\) on the right-hand side, we have

\begin{equation*} \dfrac{dA}{dt} \amp = -k A\text{.} \end{equation*}

Our goal will be to move all of the \(A\)-like quantities to the left-hand side and leave any constants on the right-hand side. This turns out to give some evidence in support of a well-known technique in differential equations called separation of variables. We proceed as follows, first dividing by \(A\) on each side, then integrating each side with respect to \(t\text{,}\)

\begin{equation*} \begin{aligned}[t] \dfrac{dA}{dt} \amp = -k A\\ \dfrac{1}{A}\cdot\dfrac{dA}{dt} \amp = -k\\ \int \dfrac{1}{A}\cdot\dfrac{dA}{dt}\,dt \amp = \int -k\, dt \end{aligned} \end{equation*}

This is somewhat abstract, but we can immediately apply \(u\)-substitution. To do so, we have to recognize that the unknown function is \(u = A(t)\) and that

\begin{equation*} du = A'(t)\,dt\text{.} \end{equation*}

Now, this isn't immediately useful, but remembering our notation

\begin{equation*} A'(t) = \dfrac{dA}{dt}\text{,} \end{equation*}

so we can write,

\begin{equation*} du = \dfrac{dA}{dt}\,dt\text{.} \end{equation*}

Suddenly we are finished with our process of \(u\)-substittution and can integrate. Continuing from above

\begin{equation*} \begin{aligned}[t] \int\underbrace{\frac{1}{A}}_{\frac{1}{u}}\cdot\underbrace{\frac{dA}{ dt}\,dt}_{du} \amp = \int -k\, dt\\ \int \frac{1}{u}\,du \amp = \int -k\,dt\\ \ln|u| \amp = -kt+c \end{aligned} \end{equation*}

Remembering above that \(u = A(t)\) we can return to our chemistry notation, that

\begin{equation*} \ln|A(t)| = -kt+c\text{.} \end{equation*}

What is left is to specify a value for \(c\text{.}\) Thinking of our physical situation we have that \(A(0) = A_{0}\) (again as a chemist you might be picturing the quantity \([A]_{0}\text{,}\) but this bracket notation is somewhat unsatisfying mathematically). This gives

\begin{equation*} \ln|A(0)| = \ln|A_{0}| = -k(0) + c\text{,} \end{equation*}

or \(c = \ln|A_{0}|\text{.}\) Since we expect \(A_{0} \gt 0\text{,}\) we can neglect the absolute value bars in favor of notational simplicity. This gives the zeroth-order integrated rate law,

\begin{equation*} \ln(A(t)) = -kt+\ln(A_{0})\text{.} \end{equation*}

Under these assumptions we expect the concentration of chemical species \(A\) to decay linearly as a function of time with a slope of \(-k\text{,}\) beginning from an initial concentration of \(A_{0}\text{.}\)

In this case, the plot of the natural logarithm of the observed concentration will resemble a decreasing linear function.

We may also be aware that a first-order reaction corresponds to exponential decay, in fact, exponentiating both sides of the formula above gives

\begin{equation*} \begin{aligned}[t] \ln(A(t)) \amp = -kt+\ln(A_{0})\\ e^{\ln(A(t))} \amp = e^{-kt+\ln(A_{0})}\\ A(t) \amp = e^{-kt}\cdot e^{\ln(A_{0})}\\ A(t) \amp = e^{-kt}A_{0}\\ A(t) \amp = A_{0}e^{-kt}\\ \end{aligned} \end{equation*}

Historically it was important to express data in terms of linear relationships, so a plot of the natural logarithm of a measurement that gave rise to a linear trend was a good indicator of this relationship and estimation of the slope \(k\) was relatively easy. In contemporary times it is relatively easy to 'fit' nonlinear functions like exponentials, so it would be reasonable to plot the raw data, not its natural logarithm, and look the underlying exponential relationship and its parameter \(k\text{.}\)

Second order.

Just like before, but now with a power of \(2\) on the right-hand side

\begin{equation*} \begin{aligned}[t] \dfrac{dA}{dt} \amp = -k A^{2}\\ \dfrac{1}{A^2}\cdot\dfrac{dA}{dt} \amp = -k\\ \int A^{-2}\cdot\dfrac{dA}{dt}\,dt \amp = \int -k\, dt \end{aligned} \end{equation*}

Again the unknown function is \(u = A(t)\) and that

\begin{equation*} du = A'(t)\,dt\text{.} \end{equation*}

Now, this isn't immediately useful, but remembering our notation

\begin{equation*} A'(t) = \dfrac{dA}{dt}\text{,} \end{equation*}

so we can write,

\begin{equation*} du = \dfrac{dA}{dt}\,dt\text{.} \end{equation*}

Suddenly we are finished with our process of \(u\)-substittution and can integrate. Continuing from above

\begin{equation*} \begin{aligned}[t] \int\underbrace{A^{-2}}_{u^{-2}}\cdot\underbrace{\frac{dA}{ dt}\,dt}_{du} \amp = \int -k\, dt\\ \int u^{-2}\,du \amp = \int -k\,dt\\ \left(\frac{1}{-2+1}\right)u^{-2+1} \amp = -kt+c\\ -u^{-1}\amp = -kt+c\\ -\frac{1}{u}\amp = -kt+c\\ -\frac{1}{A(t)}\amp = -kt+c\\ \end{aligned} \end{equation*}

Using the initial condition,

\begin{equation*} -\frac{1}{A(0)} = -\frac{1}{A_{0}} = -k(0) + c \end{equation*}

or \(c = -\frac{1}{A_{0}}\)

Solving for \(A(t)\) we have

\begin{equation*} \begin{aligned}[t] -\frac{1}{A(t)}\amp = -kt-\frac{1}{A_{0}}\\ \frac{1}{A(t)}\amp = kt+\frac{1}{A_{0}}\\ A(t)\amp = \frac{1}{kt+A_{0}}\\ \end{aligned} \end{equation*}

The plot of the reciprocal of \(A(t)\) is an increasing linear function with a slope of \(k \gt 0\text{.}\)

Summary.

Three very different looking formulas

\begin{equation*} \begin{aligned}[t] \text{zeroth order: }\amp A(t) = -kt + A_{0}\\ \text{first order: }\amp \ln(A(t)) = -kt + \ln(A_{0})\\ \text{second order: }\amp \frac{1}{A(t)} = kt + \frac{1}{A_{0}} \end{aligned} \end{equation*}

all result from integration of

\begin{equation*} \dfrac{dA}{dt} = -kA^n \end{equation*}

for different values of \(n\) corresponding to the reaction order. Solving each for \(A(t)\) suggests that \(A(t)\) evolves very differently in each case.
