Section 1.1 What is bio-calculus?

The Plan.

We will introduce the basic ideas of mathematical modeling and highlight concepts important to theoretical and empirical research.

What role does mathematics have in the biological (and physical) sciences?

Or, where have you encountered ‘math’ in class or research?

Mathematics as a language.

  • We have plenty of ways of understanding and describing our natural and physical world. We have names for levels of biological organization, names for molecules, notation for reactions, and so on.

  • Similar to the rules of written languages, the symbols and rules of mathematics can help express certain ideas and facts, and even relationships between multiple interacting quantities.

  • Until reaching calculus (and beyond), we have a relatively limited vocabulary and set of rules to work with when describing complicated situations.

  • In particular, it takes calculus to be able to clearly and compactly describe certain concepts relating to ‘change’ and ‘amount’.

Basic motivation.

Two observations:

  • Observing, describing, and understanding how things change is the critical and interesting part of the biological (and physical) sciences.

  • Calculus is the mathematical study of change.

Applied mathematics.

Using mathematical theory, notation, methods, and tools to answer general scientific questions (biology, chemistry, physics, geology, …)

  1. make a measurement

  2. describe how the measurement changes

  3. develop a model – translate from the languages of science (words, diagrams, descriptions) to math (symbols and equations)

  4. find solution using mathematical methods and make predictions about the original problem

Mathematical biology.

Special branch of applied mathematics focused on biological dynamics.

Abstraction.

One (of many) important skills to succeed in this class and beyond is abstraction. This is

  • the realization that the letter \(x\) in an equation might represent the physical size of a cell, the height of a tree, the size of a population, the age of an animal

  • the understanding that although we are interested in a specific biological problem, we have to carefully apply the rules of mathematics to achieve a solution – we solve an equation the same way whether or not it is purely a mathematical problem or represents a real-world problem

  • the recognition that many visibly different biological dynamics might be linked by the same mathematical description, and that what we learn from one system could teach us about another – even though we might not recognize any direct biological similarities between the two systems
