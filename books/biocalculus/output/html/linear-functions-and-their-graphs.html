Section 1.4 Linear functions and their graphs

The Plan.

Linear functions are the basis for most relationships that we can think about.

Definition 1.4.1. linear function.

A relationship \(f(x)\) defined by

  1. \(y=f(x)=mx+b\text{,}\) with constants \(m\) and \(b\text{,}\) or

  2. \(y=f(x)= y_{0} + m(x-x_{0})\text{,}\) with constant \(m\) and a point \((x_{0}, y_{0})\)

is called a linear function between the variables \(x\) and \(y\text{.}\) If the formula involves only adding and subtracting constants with variables multiplied by constants, the equation describes a linear function.

The slope of a linear function passing through two points \((x_{0}, y_{0})\) and \((x_{1}, y_{1})\) is defined as the ratio of the change in output over the change in input (“rise over run”),

Notation.

In general, in biological applications we rarely have inputs/outputs called \(x\) and \(y\) – for example we have mass and volume. These labels, while different from what we are used to are meant to make problems more meaningful!

Once again rewrite this as slope-intercept form.

Definition 1.4.7.

A proportional relation is a linear relation in which the output is proportional to input. In general

\begin{equation*} y = f(x) = ax, \text{where $a$ constant.} \end{equation*}

A fundamental relation \(M=\rho V\text{,}\) links mass measured in \(g\) and volume measured in \(cm^{3}\text{.}\)

What is constant?

  • the ratio of output over inputproportional relation:

    \begin{equation*} \frac{\text{output}}{\text{input}} = \text{constant} \end{equation*}

  • the ratio of change in output over change in inputlinear function:

    \begin{equation*} \dfrac{\text{change}~\text{in}~\text{output}}{\text{change}~\text{in}~\text{input}} = \text{constant}, \end{equation*}

    (i.e., slope is constant)

2.

In Question Exercise 1.4.1 of the previous section, we talked about the relationship between radius and diameter. Is that relationship linear? Why or why not?

Solution.
In Question Exercise 1.3.1 of the previous section, we talked about the relationship between radius and diameter. This is linear since \(r = \dfrac{d}{2}\text{,}\) in fact this is proportional since the radius is a constant multiple \(\left(\dfrac{1}{2}\right)\) of the diameter. This is a good reminder that dividing by two is the same as multiplying by one half.
3.

(This one might not be so quick practice.) In chemistry we use the Lineweaver-Burk or ‘double-reciprocal’ plot to determine properties of a chemical reaction. We graph a surprisingly complicated looking, but still linear, relationship

\begin{equation*} \dfrac{1}{V} = \dfrac{K_m}{V_{max}}\dfrac{1}{[S]} + \dfrac{1}{V_{max}} \end{equation*}

where \(V\) is the reaction rate or ‘velocity’, \([S]\) is the substrate concentration, and \(V_{max}\) and \(K_m\) are parameters in the relationship between our original variables \(V\) and \([S]\text{.}\) Solve for \(V\) (not the reciprocal of \(V\)) as a function of \([S]\text{.}\)

Solution.
In solving for \(V\) (steps not shown), we should eventually come upon the formula \(V=\dfrac{V_{max}[S]}{K_m+[S]}\text{.}\) This an example of a type of function we will study in more detail later this semester.
