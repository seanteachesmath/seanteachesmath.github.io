Section 2.1 Introduction to derivatives: Rates of change

Introduction.

The is perhaps the most important descriptive and computational tool in applied mathematics and the physical and life sciences.

Change.

Change, key to science (and calculus), is conveyed by the . When we talk about change it is helpful to note,

  • \(\Delta\) is mathematical shorthand for change in a variable:\(\Delta t\) typically means change in time \(t\text{,}\)\(\Delta x\) typically means change in variable \(x\text{,}\) and so on

  • we often talk about rates of change

Example 2.1.1. Rates of change.

Consider a bacteria population \(b(t)\) measured as a function of time \(t\text{,}\) we can describe two kinds of change

  • average rate of change:

    \begin{equation*} \text{average rate of change} = \frac{\hspace{5cm}}{\hspace{5cm}} = \frac{\text{change in }\hspace{1cm}}{\text{change in }\hspace{1cm}} = \frac{\hspace{1cm}}{\hspace{1cm}} \end{equation*}

  • instantaneous rate of change:(We will come back to this in the next section.)

Rates of change have units! There should be more than one correct answer for each option.

  • What two quantities are changing if we describe average velocity?

  • What are the units of the rate of change that describes how an infectious disease spreads?

Lines, secant lines, and rates of change.

When talking about the average rate of change of a measurement we compute the ratio of change in output over change in input (often time). This ratio is also the slope of a line that we call the secant line.

The secant line connects two points that lie on the graph of a function. We call one of the points the base point.

When we pick our two points on a graph, we are still computing the slope of a line through these points (even though it has a new name – the secant line).

Exercises Quick practice

1.

What is a rate that you are familiar or comfortable with, and thinking of that rate as the slope between two points on a graph, what measurements would the corresponding axes on that graph represent?

Solution.

Most of what I understand in physics is limited to the fact that velocity describes the change in position relative to the change in time. This means the vertical axis on the corresponding graph would be ‘position’ and the horizontal axis would be ‘time’.

2.

The height of a plant at two time points is given by the values \(h(2) = 3.4\) and \(h(8) = 6.9\) what is the average rate of change in height over the interval? Height is in centimeters and time is in weeks.

Solution.

The height of a plant at two time points is given by the values \(h(2) = 3.4\) and \(h(8) = 6.9\text{,}\) the average rate of change in height over the interval is

\begin{equation*} \dfrac{\Delta h}{\Delta t} = \dfrac{6.9 - 3.4}{8-2} \approx 0.583~\dfrac{\text{cm}}{\text{wk}} \end{equation*}
