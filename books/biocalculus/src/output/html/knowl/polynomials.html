Polynomials.

Since a polynomial (e.g., a quadratic or a cubic function) is the sum of terms that involve variables (like \(x\)) to powers (like \(x^{2}\)) multiplied by constants (like \(3x^{2}\)), we could decompose limits of polynomials using Theorem [cross-reference to target(s) "thm::combine" missing] and evaluate them with Theorem [cross-reference to target(s) "thm::basic" missing].

Write the following functions using only constant and identity functions:

  1. \(\displaystyle f(x) = 5x+6\)

  2. \(\displaystyle g(x) = 3x^{3}+2x^{2}+x+6\)

Use Theorem [cross-reference to target(s) "thm::combine" missing] to evaluate the limits of \(f(x)\) and \(g(x)\) from the previous example.

  1. \(\displaystyle\lim_{x\rightarrow1} f(x) = \lim_{x\rightarrow1}5x+6 =\) We can now evaluate limits of polynomials by direct substitution.

  2. \(\displaystyle \displaystyle\lim_{x\rightarrow-1} g(x) = \lim_{x\rightarrow-1} 3x^{3}+2x^{2}+x+6 =\)
