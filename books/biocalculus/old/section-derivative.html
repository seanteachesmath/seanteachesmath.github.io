Subsection 11.2 The limit definiton of the derivative

¶

We will extend our notion of slope to allow ourselves to make precise descriptions of how values of all functions, not just linear functions, change.

The slope of the secant line that connects the points \((a, f(a))\) and \((b, f(b))\) on the graph of \(f(t)\) is,

\begin{equation*} m_{\text{sec}} =\dfrac{f(b)-f(a)}{b-a} = \dfrac{\Delta f}{\Delta t}, \end{equation*}

which we call the average rate of change of \(f(t)\) between \(t = a\) and \(t = b\text{,}\) in meters per second. By bringing \(b\) closer and closer to \(a\text{,}\) our secant line ecomes a tangent line. To do this we introduce a limit,

\begin{equation*} m_{\text{tan}} = \lim_{b\to a} \dfrac{f(b)-f(a)}{b-a} \end{equation*}

which we call the instantaneous rate of change of \(f(t)\) at \(t = a\text{,}\) again in meters per second.

Remark 11.1. Mathematical aside (\(b \to a\) vs. \(\Delta t \to 0\)).

Last time in our limit definition, we used the limit as \(\Delta t \to 0\text{.}\) We will briefly show why that is equivalent to the approach above and computationally easier. Since \(\Delta t = b a\text{,}\) we have \(b = a+\Delta t\text{.}\) Taking \(b \to a\) is the same as taking \(\Delta t \to 0\text{.}\) Substitute \(b = a+\Delta t\) in the definition for \(m_{\text{tan}}\) and simplify,

\begin{equation*} \begin{aligned}[t] m_{\text{tan}} \amp = \lim_{b\to a}\dfrac{f(b)-f(a)}{b-a}\\ amp = \lim_{\Delta t \to 0}\dfrac{f(a+\Delta t)-f(a)}{(a+\Delta t)-a}\\ m_{\text{tan}} amp = \lim_{\Delta t \to 0}\dfrac{f(a+\Delta t)-f(a)}{\Delta t} \end{aligned} \end{equation*}
Definition 11.2. derivative.

The instantaneous rate of change at \(t=a\) to be the slope of the tangent line to \(f(t)\) at \(t=a\text{,}\) given by the derivative \(f'(a)\text{,}\) below,

\begin{equation*} \text{inst. rate of change} = m_{tan} = f'(a) = \dfrac{df}{dt}\Big|_{t=a} = \lim_{\Delta t\to 0}\dfrac{\Delta f}{\Delta t} = \lim_{\Delta t\to 0}\dfrac{f(a+\Delta t)-f(a)}{\Delta t} \end{equation*}

inside this limit, the change in output is \(\Delta f = f(a+\Delta t)-f(a)\) while the change in input is simply \(\Delta t\text{.}\)

Remark 11.3. Notation (in the definiton).

Rather than using the two symbols required to write \(\Delta t\text{,}\) we often use a single symbol \(h\) to denote the change from \(a\) to \(b\) in the definition (and in the picture above). This amounts to saying that \(b= a+h\) rather than \(b=a+\Delta t\) (since \(h=\Delta t\)), so that the derivative of \(f(x)\) at \(x=a\) is

\begin{equation*} f'(a) = \lim_{h \rightarrow 0}\dfrac{f(a+h)-f(a)}{h} \end{equation*}
Remark 11.4. Notation (using derivatives).

For a function \(f(x)\text{,}\) the derivative is written using the notation

\begin{equation*} \text{prime}\rightarrow f'(x) = \dfrac{df}{dx} \leftarrow \text{differential}, \end{equation*}

and is to be interpreted in any of the three following ways,

  1. intuitively as the instantaneous rate of change

  2. geometrically as the slope of the tangent line

  3. mathematically as \(\displaystyle f'(x) = \dfrac{df}{dx} = \lim_{h\to0}\dfrac{f(x+h) - f(x)}{h}\)

Definition 11.5. differentiable.

A function \(f\) is differentiable at the point a if the function has a well-defined tangent line with finite slope at point \(a\text{.}\) If we say a function is differentiable without mentioning a particular point, we mean it is differentiable everywhere.

Remark 11.6. problem behaviors.

Functions fail to be differentiable at points on the graph with jumps, corners, and vertical tangents. See graphs from the earlier lecture as examples of discontinuities.

A function \(f\) has a critical point at \(x\) if \(f(x) = 0\) or if the derivative is not defined at \(x\text{.}\)

Theorem 11.13. Sum Rule for Derivatives.

Suppose \(s(x) = f(x)+g(x)\) where \(f\) and \(g\) are both differentiable functions. Then the derivative of the sum is the sum of the derivatives

\begin{equation*} \begin{aligned}[t] \dfrac{ds}{dx} \amp = \dfrac{df}{dx} + \dfrac{dg}{dx},\text{or}\\ s'(x) \amp f'(x) + g'(x) \end{aligned} \end{equation*}
Example 11.14. derivatives by rule.

Find the derivative of \(f(x) = x^2 + 3x + 7\text{.}\)

Theorem 11.15. Constant product rule for derivatives.

Suppose \(p(x) = cf(x)\) where \(f\) is differentiable and \(c\) is a constant. Then the derivative of a constant times a function is the constant times the derivative of the function, or

\begin{equation*} \dfrac{d}{dx}\Big(cf(x)\Big) = c\dfrac{d}{dx}\Big(f(x)\Big) = c\dfrac{df}{dx} = cf'(x) \end{equation*}
Example 11.16. derivatives by rule.

Find the derivative of \(f(x) = 2x^2 + 3x + 7\text{.}\)

Theorem 11.17. Power rule for derivatives.

Suppose \(f(x) = x^n\) for integers \(n \ne 0\) (this allows \(n\) to be negative or positive)

\begin{equation*} \dfrac{d}{dx}\Big(x^n\Big) = \dfrac{1}{n+1}x^{n+1} \end{equation*}

This works for all powers n including fractions, provided that x > 0.

Example 11.18. lots of power functions and polynomials.

Use the previous theorem(s) to compute derivatives of the following power functions, each with respect to the appropriate variable.

  1. \(x^4\)

  2. \(t^{-2}\)

  3. \(3z^4+\dfrac{1}{z^2}\)

  4. \(x^2+x^{-2}\)

  5. \(1+x-x^2-x^3\)

  6. \(y^{4}+y^{-3}-\dfrac{1}{y^2}+2y\)

Remark 11.19. Higher derivatives.

We will learn how to interpret some higher derivatives soon, but we can already differentiate quite a few functions. We wll cover this soon, but you might as well try computing the second derivatives, as illustrated below.

\begin{equation*} \begin{aligned}[t] \text{first derivative} \amp = f'(x) = \dfrac{d}{dx}\Big(f(x)\Big) = \dfrac{d}{dx}\Big(the function\Big)\\ \text{second derivative} \amp = f''(x) = \dfrac{d}{dx}\Big(f'(x)\Big) = \dfrac{d}{dx}\Big(the derivative\Big) \end{aligned} \end{equation*}

The mechanics are just the same. For problems involving exponentials and logarithms be sure to simplify as fully as possible first using logarithm rules, otherwise we might not know what to do to compute the derivative!
