Section 6 Introduction to discrete-time dynamical systems (DTDS)

The Plan..

We look at a powerful modeling technique that has subtle connections to calculus.

Prediction.

An important part of mathematics and science is describing the mechanisms by which measurements change and making a testable prediction.

Approach: Discrete-time dynamical systems.

Used to predict measurements made at discrete time intervals (e.g., measurements made once per hour, day, week). Consider a measurement \(m_t\) made at time \(t\text{,}\) to define a discrete-time dynamical system (DTDS) we write

\begin{equation*} m_{t+1} = f(m_t). \end{equation*}

Let \(y_t\) denote the measured area (mm\({}^2\)) of the yeast measured on day \(t\text{.}\) Define the DTDS that predicts the future measurement, called \(y_{t+1}\text{.}\)

Definition – solution.

The sequence of values \(m_t\) for \(t = 0, 1, 2, \dots\) is the solution of the discrete-time dynamical system \(m_{t+1} = f(m_t)\) starting from initial condition \(m_0\text{.}\) We sometimes graph the solution as a plot of points against time.

For our yeast growth predictions, example initial conditions above were \(y_0=100\) mm\({}^2\) and \(y_0=10\) mm\({}^2\text{.}\) Using the updating function, compute five terms of the solution starting from each of these initial conditions.

A graphical computer..

We have a powerful technique for studying the behavior of such models using only a pencil and paper. This technique is called cobwebbing, and its name will be come clear shortly.

Note: you often will not have the data points on this graph, so they have been removed.This is how we ‘cobweb’:

  1. Graph the updating function and the diagonal (the identity or line \(y=x\))

  2. Start at initial condition and go up or down to the updating function and over to the diagonal

  3. Repeat for as many steps as needed to find the pattern

  4. Sketch the solution at times \(t=0, 1, 2, \dots\)

Extra DTDS examples.

The following completely nonbiological examples illustrate some interesting mathematical behaviors. These will help us understand behaviors of more complicated biological models next time.

Each day a patient uses up half of the medication in his bloodstream, but he is also given a new dose that raises the concentration in the blood stream by 1 mg/L. Let \(M_t\) denote the concentration on day \(t\text{.}\)

  • Write a DTDS describing this situation and plot the updating function.

  • What does the solution look like if you start with \(M_0\) = 5 mg/L? \(M_0\) = 1 mg/L?

Plot the diagonal and updating function for the previous model and cobweb five terms of the solution starting from \(M_0\) = 5 mg/L and then from \(M_0\) = 1 mg/L.

What happens if you start at \(M_0=2\text{?}\)

A point \(m^*\) is called an equilibrium of the DTDS

\begin{equation*} m_{t+1} = f(m_t) \end{equation*}

if \(f(m^*) = m^*\) (i.e., if updating by the DTDS leaves the input \(m^*\) unchanged).

  • Find equilibria (more than 1 equilibrium) graphically by locating the points where the graphs of the updating function and diagonal intersect.

  • Find equilibria algebraically by setting \(f(m^*) = m^*\) and solving for \(m^*\) .

Compute equilibrium values for the following DTDS.

Yeast model:

\begin{equation*} y_{t+1} = f(y_t) \end{equation*}

Drug model:

\begin{equation*} M_{t+1} = f(M_t) \end{equation*}

Subsection 6.1 Quick practice

  1. Is \(x^*=0\) an equilibrium point of \(x_{t+1} = 3x_t -1\text{?}\)[quick::dtds1]

  2. A population of size \(p_t\) increases by a factor of 1.2, but only after 3 units are removed. Write the discrete-time dynamical system describing the population dynamics.[quick::dtds2]

Answers:

  1. Since \(3(0) - 1 \neq 0\) (if I plug in zero and do not get back zero), zero is not an equilibrium point.

  2. To start we subtract \(3\) from \(p_t\text{,}\) that resulting quantity is multiplied by the growth factor of \(1.2\) resulting in the model \(p_{t+1} = 1.2(p_t-3)\text{.}\)
