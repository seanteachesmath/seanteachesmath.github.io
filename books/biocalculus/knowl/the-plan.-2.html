The Plan..

Dimensions and units are critical to our ability to collect data, make valid quantitative and even qualitative comparisons, and draw conclusions.

Measurements have units (e.g., cm, pounds, liters)..

We use identities to move between units and we use fundamental relations (e.g., geometry formulas) to move between dimensions.

Facts..

To compare quantities we need a common unit, we convert to a common unit, or we give up.

  • Two sides of an equation must have identical units.

  • Two terms added together must have identical units.

  • Two terms multiplied/divided are allowed to have different units.

To convert between units, multiply by identities (e.g., 1 yd = 3 ft) to ‘cancel’ unwanted units and obtain desired units.

Instead of identities, use fundamental relations (e.g., geometry formulas) to convert between measurements with different dimensions.

Keeping track of units on functions..

When appropriate we want to make good use of the benefits of function notation and function manipulation, while preserving the unit of the underlying measurement. In particular, we want to be able to use given information to determine when we are evaluating a function (units of the input) or solving an equation (units of the output).

Consider two mathematical functions used to represent the change in these quantities, \(r(t) = \textit{radius of a colony as a function of time}\) and \(A(r) = \textit{area of a colony as a function of radius}\text{.}\)

  • What are dimensions and sensible units for each?

  • How can we combine these functions (measurements) to define other realistic measurements?

Take-away messages..

  • Dimensional analysis – Always check to make sure dimensions match on each side of the equals sign!

  • Functions describe relations between measurements, so inputs and outputs have units and dimensions.

  • Only compose functions when their units and dimensions match.
