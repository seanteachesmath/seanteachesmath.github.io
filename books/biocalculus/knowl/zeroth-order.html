Zeroth order.

Within this family, the first formula comes from the zeroth order reaction given by

\begin{equation*} \dfrac{dA}{dt} = -kA^0. \end{equation*}

Since this is \(A^0\text{,}\) the right-hand side simplifies to the constant \(-k\text{.}\) At this point, we are looking for an unknown function \(A(t)\) whose derivative is a constant \(-k\text{.}\) Thinking about this from the perspective of differentiation, we are looking for a function \(A(t)\) that is linear with a slope of \(-k\text{.}\) So,

\begin{equation*} A(t) = -kt+A_{0}, \end{equation*}

where \(A_{0}\) is the constant of integration.

This turns out to be one of the simplest, most useful, but also most cleverly disguised applications of the technique of integration by \(u\)-substitution. Our goal is to put the \(A\)-like variables on the left and leave everything else on the right-hand side and immediately integrate with respect to time \(t\text{.}\) This results in

\begin{equation*} \begin{aligned}[t] \dfrac{dA}{dt} \amp = -k A^{0}\\ \dfrac{dA}{dt} \amp = -k\\ \int\dfrac{dA}{dt}\,dt \amp = \int -k\, dt \end{aligned} \end{equation*}

In what follows the higher order integrated rate law formulas are obtained with the same approach. The only thing that separates remaining cases from the first is the power on the concentration \(A = A(t)\) on the right hand side of the original differential equation. This influences how the details of integration play out, but really nothing more.
