Section 1.2 Derivatives (Differentiation)

Subsection 1.2.1 Rates of change

Given a function \(f(x)\) and two points \((x, f(x))\) and \((x+h, f(x+h))\text{,}\)

  • avg. r.o.c:.

    \(\displaystyle m_{sec} = \dfrac{f(x+h)-f(x)}{h}\) gives the slope of the secant line connecting these points on the graph of the function and describes the average rate of change of the function over the interval

  • inst. r.o.c:.

    \(\displaystyle m_{tan} = f'(x) = \dfrac{df}{dx} = \lim_{h\to0} \dfrac{f(x+h)-f(x)}{h}\) gives the slope of the tangent line touching the graph at a point \((x, f(x))\) by the \textit{limit definition of the derivative} and describes the instantaneous rate of change of the function at the point

Notice that \(\displaystyle m_{tan} = \lim_{h\to0} m_{sec}\text{.}\)

Subsection 1.2.2 Basic derivatives

  • power rule.

    \(\dfrac{d}{dx}\left(x^n\right) = nx^{n-1}\) (multiply by the old power, then reduce the power by one)

  • polynomials.

    Polynomials can be differentiated term by term by repeated application of the power rule to each term.

Subsection 1.2.3 Products and Quotients

  • product rule.

    \(\displaystyle \displaystyle \dfrac{d}{dx}\left(F(x)S(x)\right) = F(x)S'(x) + S(x)F'(x)\)

  • quotient rule.

    \(\displaystyle \displaystyle \dfrac{d}{dx}\left(\dfrac{T(x)}{B(x)}\right) = \dfrac{B(x)T'(x) - T(x)B'(x)}{(B(x))^2}\)

Subsection 1.2.4 Trigonometric functions (limits and derivative)

The following two special limits are each of the form \(\dfrac{0}{0}\) and show up when applying the limit definition of the derivative to trigonometric functions,

  • \(\displaystyle \displaystyle\lim_{x\to0}\dfrac{\sin(x)}{x} = 1\)

  • \(\displaystyle \displaystyle\lim_{x\to0}\dfrac{1-\cos(x)}{x} = 0\)

Since sines and cosines are continuous, we can move the limit inside the sine or cosine, take the limit of the terms inside, and finally evaluate the sine or cosine,

\begin{equation*} \begin{aligned} \displaystyle\lim_{x\to\infty}\sin\left(\dfrac{3\pi x^5+ \text{lower order terms}}{2x^5+\text{lower order terms}}\right) \amp = \sin\left(\lim_{x\to\infty}\dfrac{3\pi x^5+\dots}{2x^5+\dots}\right)\\ \amp = \sin\left(\lim_{x\to\infty}\dfrac{3\pi x^5}{2x^5}\right)\\ \amp = \sin\left(\lim_{x\to\infty}\dfrac{3\pi}{2}\right)\\ \amp = \dots \end{aligned} \end{equation*}

To finish the problem take the limit then evaluate the sine.

  • Derivatives of sine and cosine can be calculated by the definition.

  • Derivatives of the other trigonometric functions can be calculated by the quotient rule.

Subsection 1.2.5 Chain Rule, implicit differentiation, and related rates

For derivatives of compositions \(\displaystyle \dfrac{d}{dx}\left(f(g(x))\right) = f'(g(x))g'(x) = \dfrac{df}{dg}\cdot\dfrac{dg}{dx}\)

Implicit differentiation, an application of the chain rule, is useful when a relationship is defined by an equation too complicated (or inconvenient) to solve for \(y\text{.}\)

  • We differentiate each side of an equation and solve for \(\dfrac{dy}{dx}\text{.}\)

  • One application of this is to find slopes or equations of lines tangent to complicated curves at points on those curves.

Applications of implicit differentiation and the chain rule, related rates problems are used to relate two rates of change by an underlying formula or geometric relationship. This is done with a 5-step process that includes a picture.
