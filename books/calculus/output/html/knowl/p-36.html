This classic technique is used to evaluate more complicated integrals. We choose \(u = g(x)\) and compute the differential \(du = g'(x)dx\text{.}\) We substitute these into the original integral, and translate from \(x\) and \(dx\) to \(u\) and \(du\text{.}\) In substituting,

  • we sometimes have to multiply or divide both sides of the definition for \(du\) by a constant. We \textit{never} solve completely for \(dx\) by dividing by expressions involving \(x\text{.}\)

  • Sometimes, for complicated problems, we are required to solve \(u = g(x)\) for \(x\) as part of the substitution step.
