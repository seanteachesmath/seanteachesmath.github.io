Subsection 1.11.3 Student Perspective

During class, I encountered problems with watching my curly bracket swhen build newcommands. The more complex a new command becomes, the more important it is to watch your curly brackets. When learning about newcommands, I thought they were similar to when building a function’s definition with a parameter in other programming language like Python. Both allow you to pass arguments through them and reduce complexity in code.

\newcommand is a way to prevent long and similar code from repeating in your LaTeX file. \newcommand is composed of three main parts \newcommand{command call name}{# of arguments}{command syntax} Note: You can have more than one function to pass your argument(s) through. When you call a new command it will be: the \call_name{argument 1}...{arguments n} where n is your total amount of arguments. The major takeaway from introducing \newcommand is that it allows your LaTeX code to be concise, easier to read, and faster to write.
