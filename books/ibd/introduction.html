Subsubsection 1.2.1 Review

Following the introduction of birth-immigration-death-emigration (BIDE) 1  models

\begin{equation*} \dfrac{dN}{dt} = rn, \text{where }r = b-m \end{equation*}

and

\begin{equation*} \dfrac{dN}{dt} = rn - g, \text{where additionally \(g\) is the emigration rate} \end{equation*}

we were challenged to consider models of immigration. Let \(a\) denote the ‘arrival’ or immigration rate. The model becomes

\begin{equation*} \dfrac{dN}{dt} = rn + a \end{equation*}

We sometimes call this BIDE because it is slightly more pronounceable than BDIE, and because it emphasizes and groups the ‘gain’ terms and the ‘loss’ terms.

The equilibrium is found from \(\dfrac{dN}{dt} = 0\) or setting and solving \(0 = rN^* + a\text{.}\) Hence, the equilibrium is \(n^* = -\dfrac{a}{r}\text{.}\) Now, this only makes sense when \(r<0\text{,}\) otherwise \(N^*\) itself is negative. If \(r>0\) the population grows naturally, aided more so by immigration.

Subsubsection 1.2.2 Model classification

Given very limited exposure to mathematical models, it is worth pausing to see how this model fits into a broader context. We will pause from work developing and analyzing specific models to look at the features of our model(s) so far, and what other alternatives are available to us: dimension, linearity versus nonlinearity, qualitative versus quantitative analysis, and determinism versus stochasticity.

Dimension.

The dimension of a mathematical model refers to the number of variables being tracked. For example,

\begin{equation*} \dfrac{dN}{dt} = rN \end{equation*}

is a one-dimensional (1D) ordinary differential equations model for \(N(t)\text{.}\) Tracking additional populations (e.g., other species) or subpopulations (e.g., groups or strata within a species) requires models of higher dimensions, soon enough we will arrive at these.

Linearity.

Mathematical models that ignore interactions between individuals are linear models (for example, on the right hand side of the differential equation). Explicitly modeling interactions between individuals introduces nonlinearities into the model. This happens regardless of the model dimension. We can study 1D nonlinear models (that include interactions between individuals) or higher-dimensional nonlinear models.

Quantitative and qualitative analyses.

Trained as mathematicians, we are much more likely to be familiar with exact or ‘quantitative’ approaches, methods, or solutions. In particular, when we imagine the solution to a particular problem we are likely to expect

  • a specific formula, or

  • a specific numerical value

Despite this, there is much room for ‘qualitative’ or more descriptive approaches, methods, or solutions. In particular, through development and analysis of mathematical models we want to gain an appreciation for qualitative (graphical) solutions. To compare the two, we will outline potential pros and cons, or strengths and drawbacks. But, as one develops experience, what once may have seemed like a drawback might begin to look more and more like a strength of a particular approach.

Drawbacks of qualitative analysis.

Perhaps the biggest drawback of qualitative methods is that we are really limited to applying these approaches to models of lower dimension. These methods work well for models of 1 or 2 dimensions (i.e., variables). Occasionally we are lucky enough to have opportunities to view higher dimensional systems in lower dimension by clever analysis techniques, so we can stretch the territory of qualitative methods a bit in some cases. We are able to get only broad information about our solutions or the behaviors of our model 2 . We could distinguish between

if you omit the word only, however, this potential drawback looks suspiciously like a strength.

  • oscillations or not, or

  • growth or decay

and identify conditions under which these behaviors occur.

Strengths of qualitative analysis.

Qualitative analysis requires minimal assumptions about the numerical values for rates (parameters), which in many cases are hard to find, especially at the beginning of a project. This analysis can also help give information about interesting relationships between parameters. In particular, based on the discussion above, understanding under which parameters oscillations occur or not, or under which parameters solutions grow or decay, is powerful information.

Drawbacks of quantitative analysis.

Counter to our intuition, ‘closed form’ solutions to interesting models are often impossible to find or are uselessly complex when found. If we seek numerical solutions or graphs of analytical solutions, we need precise numerical values for initial conditions and parameters. For example, when applying our qualitative methods to our emigration model, we found that \(N^* = \dfrac{g}{r}\text{.}\) This analysis require little, if any, assumptions on \(g\) and \(r\) other than that they were ‘some positive values.’

Strengths of quantitative analysis.

Sometimes we get lucky and models are solvable and solutions provide intuition. In other cases, even ‘good guesses’ of parameter values can help generate “intuition building”’ numerical results about the model and its dynamics.

Deterministic or stochastic models.

Models presented so far have been deterministic, meaning that the structure of the model, combine with its initial conditions and parameter values completely determine the behavior of the solutions. In contrast, to this are stochastic models in which each application or investigation of the model gives a unique realization of the dynamics. In other words, the same model and conditions can give rise to very different dynamics. These are connected in that we understand the deterministic analog of a stochastic model to capture the mean behavior (see Fig [cross-reference to target(s) "fig::det_stoch" missing]).

Keep in mind that both deterministic models and stochastic models have their place in mathematical modeling, but that more importantly each allows us to apply different methods and to ask different questions.

Deterministic models.

In general we are unable to investigate certain questions about extinction with models like

\begin{equation*} \dfrac{dN}{dt} = rN \end{equation*}

because the solution

\begin{equation*} N(t) = N_0e^{rt} \end{equation*}

only approaches zero in the limit as \(t\to\infty\) (given \(r<0\)), but never reaches 0. In contrast to this, stochastic models, whose realizations are illustrated in Figs. [cross-reference to target(s) "fig::coin_flips" missing] and [cross-reference to target(s) "fig::coin_flips_100" missing] certainly demonstrate extinction. Moreover, the stochastic results allow a variety of new questions about phenomenon related to extinction. We could investigate things such as the variability (i.e., variance) in population size as time goes on or the average time to extinction for small populations where stochasticity might be important.

Subsubsection 1.2.3 Harvesting and logistic growth

Key concepts in continuous-time models.

In dynamical systems and mathematical modeling we are often interested in the ‘long-term’ behavior of solutions to our models. To lay out a few definitions and concepts we will consider a generic dynamical system,

\begin{equation*} \frac{d^{} {x}}{d {t}^{}}=f(x), \end{equation*}

where \(f(x)\) is some nice function and \(x(0)=x_0\) is a known initial value.

(Definition) Equilibrium point..

An equilibrium point is a value of the solution that is unchanging in time.For a differential equation

\begin{equation*} \frac{d^{} {x}}{d {t}^{}}=f(x), \end{equation*}

\(x^*\) is an equilibrium point if \(f(x^*)=0\text{.}\) Stability of equilibria helps us characterize the long term behavior of a model using information from equilibria. While there are mathematical varieties of stability we will consider local asymptotic stability.

(Definition) Stable equilibrium point..

A stable equilibrium point attracts solutions that start nearby, alternatively, it is stable if small perturbations from the equilibrium decay to zero.An equilibrium \(x^*\) of a dynamical system is a stable equilibrium point if solutions starting ‘near‘ \(x^*\) approach \(x^*\) in time.

(Definition) Unstable equilibrium point..

An unstable equilibrium point repels solutions that start nearby, alternatively, it is unstable if small perturbations from the equilibrium grow.An equilibrium \(x^*\) of a dynamical system is an unstable equilibrium point if solutions starting ‘near‘ \(x^*\) diverge from \(x^*\) in time.

For the remainder of this section, we will study the logistic growth equation, its relationship to the discrete logistic model, its relationship to disease transmission in a closed population, and a few extensions. The logistic growth equation is given by,

\begin{equation*} \frac{d^{} {N}}{d {t}^{}}=rN\Big(1-\frac{N}{K}\Big). \end{equation*}

Let \(N^*\) be an equilibrium point, so that we can write \(N(t)=N^*+n(t).\) We will derive a stability condition for an arbitrary equilibrium point, starting by identifying the dynamics of \(n(t)\text{,}\)

\begin{equation*} \begin{aligned} \frac{d^{} {N}}{d {t}^{}}&=\frac{d^{} {}}{d {t}^{}}\Big(N^*+n(t)\Big)\\ &=0+\frac{d^{} {n}}{d {t}^{}}.\end{aligned} \end{equation*}

Now, substituting the formula for \(N(t)\) and expanding,

\begin{equation*} \begin{aligned} \frac{d^{} {n}}{d {t}^{}}&= \frac{d^{} {N}}{d {t}^{}}\\ &=r(N^*+n)\Big(1-\frac{N^*+n}{K}\Big)\\ &=rN^*\Big(1-\frac{N^*+n}{K}\Big)+rn\Big(1-\frac{N^*+n}{K}\Big)\\ &=\cancel{rN^*\Big(1-\frac{N^*}{K}\Big)}-\frac{rN^*n}{K}+rn\Big(1-\frac{N^*}{K}\Big)-\cancel{\frac{rn^2}{K}}\\ &=r\Big(1-\frac{2N^*}{K}\Big)n\\ \frac{d^{} {n}}{d {t}^{}}&= f'(N^*)n\end{aligned} \end{equation*}

In the last step we can see that the terms that remain are equivalent to the derivative of \(f(N)\text{,}\) with respect to \(N\) evaluated at \(N^*\text{.}\) Here we linearize by substituting our formula for the solution and dropping al higher order terms in \(n(t)\text{.}\) You could do this, more formally or generally, by Taylor series.

Solutions to

\begin{equation*} \frac{d^{} {n}}{d {t}^{}}= f'(N^*)n \end{equation*}

decay to zero if \(f'(N^*)<0\) and grow if \(f'(N^*)>0\text{.}\) Hence, an equilibrium point \(N^*\) (trivial or not) is stable if \(f'(N^*)<0\) and unstable if \(f'(N^*)>0\text{.}\)

Evaluating derivatives should work in general, but it is not our only tool. Today we will use graphical methods to develop a qualitative understanding of the dynamics of a harvested population. If we think about what our model means, we can make progress without tackling sometimes frustrating, other times uninspiring, algebra.

A disease model.

Consider a herd of Ebola-carrying zombies, where the ‘healthy’ zombies are susceptible to Ebola (\(S\)) and the ‘infectious’ zombies are capable of transmitting Ebola (\(I\)).

=[draw, fill=blue!20, minimum size=2em, circle] =[draw, fill=red!20, minimum size=2em, circle]

Much like in class we will build a word model as we go.

\begin{equation*} \begin{aligned} \text{change in }S & = \text{gain from recovery} - \text{loss to infection}\\ \text{change in }I & = \text{loss to recovery} + \text{gain from infection},\end{aligned} \end{equation*}

and replace the terms with symbols. Our ‘first’ term is ‘loss to recovery’ in the \(I\) equation. We can think of \(\gamma\) as the leaving rate from the \(I\) compartment, much like \(m\) was the ‘leaving rate from the living’ (i.e., dying) compartment of a population model. Everything, in this model, that leaves one compartment enters another (i.e., there are no ‘leaks’). We can also argue for the infection rate as follows, since infection is only transmitted directly and by infectious hosts, we might expect that the rate that \(S\) individuals gain infection is proportional to the density (zombies per hectare) of infectious zombies, \(I\text{.}\) In fact here, we assume that the infection rate is a linearly increasing function of \(I\text{.}\) There are plenty of other models of transmission, some we will study in the homework, others later in the semester, still more in our projects.

\begin{equation*} \begin{aligned} {2} \text{change in }S & = \text{gain from recovery} &&-\text{loss to infection}\\ \frac{d^{} {S}}{d {t}^{}}&=\gamma I && - {\beta I} S\\ \text{change in }I & = \text{loss to recovery} && +\text{gain from infection}\\ \frac{d^{} {I}}{d {t}^{}}&=-{\gamma} I && + \beta I S\\\end{aligned} \end{equation*}

Since the total number of zombies \(N\) is preserved, we can replace the equation for \(S\) by an algebraic constraint \(S=N-I\text{.}\) We can substitute this into the equation for \(I\) and derive a 1-dimensional, nonlinear model,

\begin{equation*} \frac{d^{} {I}}{d {t}^{}}=\beta I (N-I)-\gamma I. \end{equation*}

If we define our model \(h(I)=\beta I (N-I)-\gamma I,\) we can plot the more interesting version of our phase line as in Fig. [cross-reference to target(s) "fig::phase" missing]. Ignoring graphs for the moment, we can also compute equilibria of continuous time models, defined as solutions to \(h(I^*)=0\text{.}\) This gives \(I^*=0\) and \(I^*=N-\frac{\gamma}{\beta}.\) It might prove useful to notice that our nontrivial solution exists when \(\gamma<\beta N\) this suggests a limitation on the recovery rate that depends on the contact parameter \(\beta\) and the total zombie population size \(N\text{.}\)

Subsubsection 1.2.4 Introduction to stochastic models

Motivation.

So far we have worked with deterministic models, most recently in continuous time. An example of a deterministic model is

\begin{equation*} \frac{d^{} {N}}{d {t}^{}}=rN, \end{equation*}

where \(r\) is the growth rate of species \(N\text{.}\) The solution to such a model is

\begin{equation*} N(t)=N_0e^{rt}, \end{equation*}

where \(N_0\) is the initial population size. As \(t\rightarrow\infty\text{,}\) we have either \(N\rightarrow 0\) if \(b<m\) \(r<0\)), or \(N\rightarrow \infty\) if \(b>m\) \(r>0\)). In decaying populations \(r<0\)), solutions decay exponentially towards zero, rather than actually reaching zero. Thus, we reach extinction asymptotically, rather than in finite time.

But, at low numbers chance events become important. A single cell could divide or die. If it dies, we have extinction. If \(r<0\) we expect extinction, but what about when \(r>0\text{?}\) If \(r>0\text{,}\) a single cell could die rather than divide, by chance, even if it should divide and grow in number.

Since we are focusing on the fates of individual cells, it is perhaps important to think in terms of integer-valued states. Let \(N_0=n\) be the initial number of cells present, perhaps in come cell culture of a fascinating biological experiment. We should note that, while the process is discrete (in the number of cells), time is continuous. The number of animals changes by a single individual through births (to the right) and deaths (to the left) as illustrated in Fig. [cross-reference to target(s) "fig::bdp" missing].

=[draw, fill=white, minimum size=3em, circle] =[ fill=white, minimum size=3em, circle]

To make use of the diagram in Fig. [cross-reference to target(s) "fig::bdp" missing] we should let

\begin{equation*} P_n(t)=\text{Prob}(\text{counting \(n\) animals at time \(t\)}), \end{equation*}

and consider how these probabilities evolve in time for all \(n\text{.}\) One important fact is that

\begin{equation*} P_i(0)=\begin{cases}1, \,\text{if \(i=n\)}\\0, \,\text{otherwise}\end{cases}, \end{equation*}

since we know that we start from \(N_0=n\) animals with absolute certainty. We can derive the differential equation by conservation,

\begin{equation*} \begin{aligned} {2} P_n(t+\Delta t) & =\text{Prob(a birth)}\times\text{Prob}(\text{\(n-1\) animals at time \(t\)}) &&\\ & +\text{Prob(a death)}\times\text{Prob}(\text{\(n+1\) animals at time \(t\)}) &&\\ & +\text{Prob(no change)}\times\text{Prob}(\text{\(n\) animals at time \(t\)}) &&\end{aligned} \end{equation*}

First we will apply the definition of \(P_n(t)\) and the others (i.e., \(P_{n-1}(t)\)). Notice that the equation is presented in two columns, on the left is the format ‘in words’ and on the right introduces the corresponding notation,

\begin{equation*} \begin{aligned} {2} P_n(t+\Delta t) & =\text{Prob(a birth)}\times\text{Prob}(\text{\(n-1\) animals at time \(t\)}) &&\qquad=\text{Prob(a birth)}\times P_{n-1}(t) \\ & +\text{Prob(a death)}\times\text{Prob}(\text{\(n+1\) animals at time \(t\)}) &&\qquad+\text{Prob(a death)}\times P_{n+1}(t)\\ & +\text{Prob(no change)}\times\text{Prob}(\text{\(n\) animals at time \(t\)}) &&\qquad+\text{Prob(no change)}\times P_{n}(t) \end{aligned} \end{equation*}

Below we could also consider \(\text{Prob(no change)}\) as \((1-\text{Prob(change)})\text{,}\) where \(\text{Prob(change)}\) is given by the probability of events resulting in exit from size \(n\text{.}\) The event probabilities are,

\begin{equation*} \begin{aligned} {2} P_n(t+\Delta t) & =\text{Prob(a birth)}\times\text{Prob}(\text{\(n-1\) animals at time \(t\)}) &&\qquad=(n-1)r\Delta t P_{n-1}(t) \\ & +\text{Prob(a death)}\times\text{Prob}(\text{\(n+1\) animals at time \(t\)}) &&\qquad+(n+1)\mu\Delta t P_{n+1}(t)\\ & +\text{Prob(no change)}\times\text{Prob}(\text{\(n\) animals at time \(t\)}) &&\qquad+(1-nr\Delta t-n\mu\Delta t) P_{n}(t) \end{aligned} \end{equation*}

Finally, we can begin rearranging to derive the differential equation for \(P_n(t)\text{,}\)

\begin{equation*} \begin{aligned} P_n(t+\Delta t) &=(n-1)r\Delta t P_{n-1}(t) +(n+1)\mu\Delta t P_{n+1}(t)+(1-nr\Delta t-n\mu\Delta t) P_{n}(t) \\ P_n(t+\Delta t) -P_n(t)&=(n-1)r\Delta t P_{n-1}(t) +(n+1)\mu\Delta t P_{n+1}(t)-(nr\Delta t-n\mu\Delta t) P_{n}(t) \\ \frac{P_n(t+\Delta t) -P_n(t)}{\Delta t}&=(n-1)r P_{n-1}(t) +(n+1)\mu P_{n+1}(t)-n(r -\mu) P_{n}(t) \end{aligned} \end{equation*}

Now, taking the limit \(\Delta t\rightarrow0\text{,}\)

\begin{equation*} \frac{d^{} {P_n(t)}}{d {t}^{}}=(n-1)r P_{n-1}(t) +(n+1)\mu P_{n+1}(t)-(nr-n\mu) P_{n}(t) . \end{equation*}

We have to assume that \(P_i(t)=0\) for any \(i<0\text{,}\) and accept a system of coupled equations for \(n=0, 1, 2, \dots\text{.}\) We will study this particular model with a suite of techniques, including naive calculation, inspection, and somewhat elegant probability theory.

Naive calculation.

Looking at the first equation for \(n=0\text{,}\)

\begin{equation*} \frac{d^{} {P_0(t)}}{d {t}^{}}=(-1)r P_{-1}(t) +(1)\mu P_{1}(t)-0\cdot (r -\mu) P_{0}(t), \end{equation*}

we simplify to

\begin{equation*} \frac{d^{} {P_0(t)}}{d {t}^{}}= \mu P_{1}(t). \end{equation*}

If we were to look at the equilibrium of \(P_0\text{,}\) assuming \(\mu\neq0\text{,}\) we would need \(P_1(t)\rightarrow0\text{.}\) Since \(P_1\) appears in the next equation, we require all \(P_i(t)\rightarrow0\) for \(i>0\text{.}\) Since \(P_i(t)\) are probabilities, we require \(\displaystyle\sum_0^\infty P_i(t)=1\text{,}\) which means that \(P_0(t)\rightarrow1\text{.}\) This suggests that extinction is unavoidable, but a bit more work provides additional insight. Specifically populations with \(b>m\) can escape extinction, with the probability of extinction of

\begin{equation*} \left(\dfrac{m}{b}\right)^{N_0} \end{equation*}

which for \(b>m\) decays geometrically as a function of the initial population size. With quite a bit of effort, this result is very nicely illustrated in classic texts.

Subsubsection 1.2.5 Birth-death processes and the Gillespie algorithm

A coin-flipping exercise.

We will simulate a pure-death process with events chosen by random ‘coin’ flips.

  1. Start with a room full of participants.

  2. At the start of each discrete round, everyone should flip a coin:

    1. a ‘head’ means death, so sit down and remain seated.

    2. a ‘tail’ means survival, so keep standing.

  3. Repeat until all participants have vanished, recording the number alive at the start of each round.

A sample result for two populations is plotted in Fig. [cross-reference to target(s) "fig::death_sim" missing].

Bold claim revisited.

Previously, we claimed that all populations go to extinction (in the absence of immigration). If we wish to simulate the process described in Fig. [cross-reference to target(s) "fig::bdp2" missing], we need to track a few pieces of information: the event that occurs (a birth or a death), and the time that the event occurs. =[draw, fill=white, minimum size=3em, circle] =[ fill=white, minimum size=3em, circle]

Using Fig. [cross-reference to target(s) "fig::bdp2" missing], the probability that a birth occurs as the ‘next event’ is

\begin{equation*} \operatorname{Prob}({\text{a birth}})=\frac{rn}{rn+\mu n}= \frac{r}{r+\mu}, \end{equation*}

and similarly for a death,

\begin{equation*} \operatorname{Prob}({\text{a death}})=1-\operatorname{Prob}({\text{a birth}})=1-\frac{r}{r+\mu}=\frac{\mu}{r+\mu}. \end{equation*}

To determine what happens we consider the interval \([0,1],\) which we divide, here, into two subintervals whose widths reflect the event probabilities above. For example, if \(r=0\) (\(\mu>0\)), the probability of a birth is zero and probability of a death is one. In contrast, if \(\mu=0\) (\(r>0\)), then the probability of a death is zero and the probability of a birth is one.

More interesting cases occur if, for example, \(r=8\) and \(\mu=2\text{,}\) then the probability of a birth is \(\frac{4}{5}\) and the probability of a death is \(\frac{1}{5}\text{.}\) To illustrate this last case, we divide the unit interval into subintervals \((0, \frac{4}{5}]\text{,}\) representing the probabilities of birth, and \((\frac{4}{5}, 1)\text{,}\) representing the probabilities of death. Then we choose a uniform random number (a dart) and throw it at the divided number line (a target). The interval in which it lands identifies our next event. In this example, since \(r>>\mu\text{,}\) the probability that a birth occurs is much larger than the probability that a death occurs. Thus, the birth interval in the target is much larger than the death interval, and we are more likely to have a birth.

Given that we can identify the event that occurs, our next task is to identify the time until the event occurs. Here we assume that he events occur at a constant rate

\begin{equation*} \lambda=(r+\mu)n, \end{equation*}

and that the time until the next event, \(T\text{,}\) is exponentially distributed with parameter \(\lambda\text{.}\) Thus,

\begin{equation*} T\sim\exp(\lambda). \end{equation*}

We can easily sample numbers from certain distributions. To sample exponentially distributed random numbers, we take advantage of its cumulative distribution function, \(F(t)=1-\exp(-\lambda t)\text{.}\) We pick a uniform random number \(r_2\) and solve for \(t\) using \(r_2=F(t)\) to find our even time.

\begin{equation*} \begin{aligned} r_2 & = F(t)\\ &= 1-\exp(-\lambda t)\\ \exp(-\lambda t) & = 1-r_2\\ -\lambda t & =\ln(1-r_2)\\ t & =-\frac{\ln(1-r_2)}{\lambda}\end{aligned} \end{equation*}

Importantly, since \(r_2\) is a random number so is \(1-r_2\text{.}\) In slight abuse of notation, we relabel the argument of \(\ln(\cdot)\text{,}\) which is \(1-r_2\text{,}\) by \(r_2\text{,}\)

\begin{equation*} t =-\frac{\ln(r_2)}{\lambda}. \end{equation*}

Since \(r_2\in(0,1)\text{,}\) the logarithm is negative, so the negative in front ensures a positive event time \(t\text{.}\)

Now that we have identified both our event and the event eating time, \(t\text{,}\) we simply update our state and repeat the process for until we reach either a fixed number of events, a fixed final time, or a population that becomes extinct (or reaches some other pre-specified condition). An example solution to the birth-death process is plotted in Fig. [cross-reference to target(s) "fig::bdp_result" missing], where unlike in Fig. [cross-reference to target(s) "fig::death_sim" missing] the rate of birth is non-zero.

While the details of evaluating our claim are beyond our needs, we can and should temper our ‘everything vanishes’ statement. In the absence of immigration, populations that remain finite are bound to reach extinction. They tend to do so rather quickly, after a string of bad luck. Otherwise populations can grow to infinity, avoiding and escaping the threat of extinction.

More sophisticated approaches, using generating functions (special power series) let us calculate the mean extinction time for a given problem.
