Stability.

Consider the Jacobian matrix \(J^*\) from the linearization of a 2D model. For

\begin{equation*} J^*=\left(\begin{array}{cc}a & b\\c& d\end{array}\right), \end{equation*}

the associated characteristic polynomial is

\begin{equation*} C(\lambda)=\lambda^2-\tau \lambda+\Delta, \end{equation*}

where \(\tau\) is the trace and \(\Delta\) is the determinant. Solving this with the quadratic formula,

\begin{equation*} \lambda_\pm=\frac{\tau\pm\sqrt{\tau^2-4\Delta}}{2}. \end{equation*}

We’ll take a graphical approach to understanding the behavior of roots. First, notice that the vertex of the parabola is at \(\frac{\tau}{2},\) since the roots are symmetric about the vertex. We’ll assume the eigenvalues are distinct and that \(\lambda_+>\lambda_-\text{.}\)

For the leading eigenvalue, \(\lambda_+<1\text{,}\) we need

\begin{equation*} \begin{aligned} \lambda_+=\frac{\tau}{2}+\frac{\sqrt{\tau^2-4\Delta}}{2}&<1\\ \frac{\sqrt{\tau^2-4\Delta}}{2}&<1-\frac{\tau}{2}\\ \frac{\tau^2-4\Delta}{4}&<\left(1-\frac{\tau}{2}\right)^2\\ \frac{\tau^2}{4}-\Delta&<1-\tau+\frac{\tau^2}{4}\\ -\Delta&<1-\tau\Rightarrow -1+\tau<\Delta\end{aligned} \end{equation*}

If the determinant satisfies

\begin{equation*} -1+\tau<\Delta, \end{equation*}

then \(\lambda_+<1\text{.}\)

For the other eigenvalue, \(\lambda_->-1\text{,}\) we need

\begin{equation*} \begin{aligned} \lambda_-=\frac{\tau}{2}-\frac{\sqrt{\tau^2-4\Delta}}{2}&>-1\\ 1+\frac{\tau}{2} & > \frac{\sqrt{\tau^2-4\Delta}}{2}\\ \left(1+\frac{\tau}{2}\right)^2 & > \frac{\tau^2-4\Delta}{4}\\ 1+\tau+\frac{\tau^2}{4} & > \frac{\tau^2}{4}-\Delta\\ 1+\tau& > -\Delta\Rightarrow -1-\tau<\Delta\end{aligned} \end{equation*}

If the determinant satisfies

\begin{equation*} -1-\tau<\Delta, \end{equation*}

then \(\lambda_->-1\text{.}\)

We need both eigenvalue conditions satisfied simultaneously, so that

\begin{equation*} \Delta>|\tau|-1. \end{equation*}

The second line from each calculation is easily visualized in the following picture, roughly, the distance from the vertex to the root,

\begin{equation*} \frac{\sqrt{\tau^2-4\Delta}}{2} \end{equation*}

is less than the distance from the vertex to the enclosing endpoint (see Fig. [cross-reference to target(s) "fig::roots" missing]).

Lastly, we need complex roots which arise when \(\tau^2-4\Delta<0\text{,}\) to satisfy \(|\lambda_i|<1\text{.}\)

\begin{equation*} \begin{aligned} \lambda_\pm&=\frac{\tau}{2}\pm\frac{\sqrt{\tau^2-4\Delta}}{2}\\ &=\frac{\tau}{2}\pm i \frac{\sqrt{4\Delta-\tau^2}}{2}\\ |\lambda_\pm|&=\frac{\tau^2}{4}+ \Delta-\frac{\tau^2}{4}\\ |\lambda_\pm|&=\Delta\Rightarrow \Delta<1\end{aligned} \end{equation*}
Stability triangle.

We need

  • \(\displaystyle \operatorname{det}(J^*)<1\)

  • \(\displaystyle \operatorname{det}(J^*) > -1+| \operatorname{Tr}(J^*)|\)

  • Under \(\operatorname{det}(J^*)=\frac{\operatorname{Tr}(J^*)^2}{4}\) eigenvalues are real.
