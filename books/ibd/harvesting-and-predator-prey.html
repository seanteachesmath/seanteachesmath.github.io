Section 3.2 Harvesting and predator-prey

Key concepts in continuous-time models.

Consider the model

\begin{equation*} \begin{aligned} \frac{d^{} {N}}{d {t}^{}}&=\left(r\left(1-\frac{N}{K}\right)-\frac {hF}{1+sN}\right)N\\ &= (f(N)-g(N))N,\end{aligned} \end{equation*}

that describes the dynamics of a logistically-growing population subjected to harvesting. Equilibria of this model are \(N^*\) and \(f(N^*)=g(N^*)\text{.}\) The removal rate due to harvesting is

\begin{equation*} \frac{hF}{1+sN}, \end{equation*}

per \(N\text{.}\) One short derivation of this functional form is given here, we will see another derivation in a few lectures.

Derivation of a functional response.

A harvester spends its time, of which it has \(T\text{,}\) searching (\(T_S\)) or handling (\(T_H\)), thus,

\begin{equation*} T=T_S+T_H. \end{equation*}

The handling time depends on the number of prey captured (\(N_C\)) and the handling time per prey (\(\hat s\)), so that,

\begin{equation*} T_H=\hat s N_C. \end{equation*}

In turn, the number captured depends on the searching or finding rate (\(\hat h\)), the number of prey available (\(N\)), and the time spend searching by predators or harvesters (\(T_S\)),

\begin{equation*} N_C=\hat h N T_S. \end{equation*}

Solving for the search time,

\begin{equation*} T_S=\frac{N_C}{\hat h N}, \end{equation*}

so that

\begin{equation*} T=T_S+T_H=\hat s N_C+\frac{N_C}{\hat h N}= N_C\left(\hat s + \frac{1}{\hat h N}\right). \end{equation*}

Solving for \(N_C\text{,}\)

\begin{equation*} N_C=T\left(\frac{\hat h N}{1+\hat s \hat h N}\right). \end{equation*}

The per predator feeding rate is,

\begin{equation*} \frac{N_C}{T}=\left(\frac{\hat h N}{1+\hat s \hat h N}\right), \end{equation*}

since the definition of \(T\) was ‘per predator’. Redefining constants \(h=\hat h\) and \(s=\hat s \hat h\text{,}\) we can finally write the per predator harvesting rate as

\begin{equation*} \frac{N_C}{T}=h_{II}(N)=\left(\frac{h N}{1+ s N}\right), \end{equation*}

so that the harvesting term is \(h_{II}(N)F.\) If predators instantaneously consume their prey, \(s=0\) which returns the Type I response, because saturation vanishes.

Consider now, a two dimensional extension where \(F\) is no longer a parameter, but instead a dynamic variable. Whose growth is proportional to the consumption of prey, up to a scaling factor \(\epsilon\text{.}\) Such a model is,

\begin{equation*} \begin{aligned} \frac{d^{} {N}}{d {t}^{}}&=\left(r\left(1-\frac{N}{K}\right)-\ frac{hF}{1+sN}\right)N\\ \frac{d^{} {F}}{d {t}^{}}&=\frac{\epsilon hFN}{1+sN}-mF.\end{aligned} \end{equation*}

We can give ourselves a slightly simpler situation if we assume that the predator is a ‘Type I’ predator,

\begin{equation*} \begin{aligned} \frac{d^{} {N}}{d {t}^{}}&=\left(r\left(1-\frac{N}{K}\right)- hF\right)N\\ \frac{d^{} {F}}{d {t}^{}}&=\epsilon hFN-mF.\end{aligned} \end{equation*}

A common approach to studying such a model is to first ‘nondimensionalize’ the variables by scaling by some relevant value. Here we have a carrying capacity for \(N\text{,}\) so if we scale \(N\) by \(K\) we can define \(x=\frac{N}{K},\) and study the dynamics of \(x\) which describes the proportion of carrying capacity that is filled. The goal of this is to combine parameters and reduce the dimension of parameter space. We also rescale time by setting \(T=rt\) and replacing the derivative \(\displaystyle\frac{d^{} {}}{d {t}^{}}\) by \(\displaystyle r\frac{d^{} {}}{d {T}^{}}\text{.}\) The definition involving \(F\) is less clear from the start, but as we step through the process we find that using \(\displaystyle y=\frac{h}{r}F\) works pretty well. Ultimately, after some failed attempts, we find something like,

\begin{equation*} \begin{aligned} \frac{d^{} {x}}{d {t}^{}}&=\left(\left(1-x\right)-y\right)x\\ \frac{d^{} {y}}{d {t}^{}}&=\beta(x-\alpha)y.\end{aligned} \end{equation*}

Just as we used phase lines in one dimension, we will use phase planes in two dimensions. To introduce this process we will use an even simpler model describing the densities of prey (\(X\)) and predator (\(Y\)),

\begin{equation*} \begin{aligned} \frac{d^{} {X}}{d {t}^{}}&=rX-mXY\\ \frac{d^{} {Y}}{d {t}^{}}&=nXY-sY.\end{aligned} \end{equation*}

First, we hope to compute equilibria. We can start by solving when each of the equations is equal to zero (i.e., when individual quantities are unchanging). Setting

\begin{equation*} \frac{d^{} {X}}{d {t}^{}}=0\Rightarrow (r-mY)X=0. \end{equation*}

This equation implies that \(X\) is unchanging along the lines \(X=0\) and \(Y=\frac{r}{m}\) in the \((X, Y)\)-plane. Additionally, setting

\begin{equation*} \frac{d^{} {Y}}{d {t}^{}}=0\Rightarrow (nX-s)Y=0. \end{equation*}

This equation implies that \(Y\) is unchanging along the lines \(Y=0\) and \(X=\frac{s}{n}\) in the \((X, Y)\)-plane. Equilibria require both equations be satisfied simultaneously, thus opposite nullclines must intersect so that both of \(X\) and \(Y\) are unchanging. We will jump ahead and plot everything in Fig. [cross-reference to target(s) "fig::pred_prey_pp" missing], this includes trajectories in the phase plane and ‘direction arrows.’

Direction arrows in Fig.. [cross-reference to target(s) "fig::pred_prey_pp" missing], are obtained by treating the individual differential equations as inequalities. The motivation is that we know that

\begin{equation*} \frac{d^{} {X}}{d {t}^{}}>0 \end{equation*}

when

\begin{equation*} (r-mY)X>0. \end{equation*}

Since \(X>0\) is of interest, the to ensure the derivative is positive, we need

\begin{equation*} \frac{r}{m}>Y. \end{equation*}

Thus, \(X\) is increasing below the (nonzero) \(X\)-nullcline. Similarly, \(X\) is decreasing above this nullcline. Lastly, we can show that \(Y\) is decreasing ‘to the left’ of the (again, nonzero) vertical \(Y\)-nullcline, and decreasing to the right. This shows that motion is counterclockwise. Numerical solutions show that we have closed orbits around the equilibrium point \((X^*, Y^*)\text{.}\) We call this point neutrally stable.

If we hadn’t computed solutions, we wouldn’t necessarily know from the phase portrait that these solutions do not spiral in towards or out from the equilibrium. To know for certain, we would need to study the ‘linearized’ dynamics near each equilibrium point, including the point \((0, 0)\text{,}\) as well. Since we have solutions, we might as well plot them in time (see Fig. [cross-reference to target(s) "fig::pred_prey" missing]).

Subsection 3.2.1 The ‘Trace-Determinant Method’

Diagnostics for eigenvalues and stability.

In many cases, the calculation of eigenvalues from the Jacobian matrix at equilibria is unnecessary. Instead, we prefer a simpler approach that we refer to as the ‘trace-determinant method.’ Combined with a cautious sketch of the phase plane, the inspection of \(\tau=\operatorname{Tr}{J^*}\) and \(\Delta=\operatorname{det}{J^*}.\)

In some situations, the phase plan suggests that trajectories might oscillate about a fixed point. Yet, without the aid of computers we are often unable to distinguish between equilibria that attract, repel, or do neither. We call these attracting (repelling) equilibria stable (unstable) foci, the plural of focus, or neutrally stable centers, in the third case. Since we expect solutions to oscillate given the phase plane, all that remains is to determine whether or not the real part of the eigenvalue is positive, negative, or zero. This is obvious from the sign of \(\tau\text{.}\) The eigenvalues corresponding to the characteristic polynomial

\begin{equation*} C(\lambda)=\lambda^2-\tau \lambda +\Delta, \end{equation*}

are given by

\begin{equation*} \lambda_\pm=\frac{\tau}{2}\pm\frac{\sqrt{\tau^2- 4\Delta}}{2}. \end{equation*}

We can see that \(\tau>0\) implies \(\operatorname{Re}(\lambda)>0\text{,}\) which means perturbations from equilibria decay and a particular equilibrium is a stable focus. We can repeat the argument for \(\tau<0\text{,}\) which suggests an unstable focus, characterized by growing oscillations. Eigenvalues are purely imaginary, giving rise to neutrally stable centers, if \(\tau=0\text{.}\) Thus, if we expect oscillations, \(\tau\) tells us whether oscillations grow (\(\tau>0\)), decay (\(\tau<0\)), or are sustained (\(\tau=0\)), all without ever calculating the actual eigenvalue.

Another interesting case, that gives us yet another obvious conclusion, is \(\Delta<0\text{.}\) If we consider a matrix, \(J^*\text{,}\) then \(\tau\) is the sum of its eigenvalues while \(\Delta\) is the product. Thus, for a \(2\times2\) matrix \(J^*\text{,}\) \(\Delta=\lambda_1\lambda_2.\) If \(\Delta<0\text{,}\) then eigenvalues must be of opposite sign. This gives rise the the dynamics of a saddle point, stable in one direction, unstable in the other.

The only other nondegenerate cases occur when we have distinct, real eigenvalues of the same sign. The corresponding equilibria are termed nodes, with stability governed by the sign of \(\tau\text{,}\) since \(\Delta>0\text{.}\)

We have our degenerate cases on the \(\Delta=0\) axis and along the curve \(\Delta=\frac{\tau^2}{4}\text{.}\) The case \(\Delta=0\) gives us a line of fixed points, where one quantity remains fixed (since one eigenvalue is zero) while the other grows from (\(\tau>0\)) or decays towards (\(\tau<0\)) zero. Along the curve \(\Delta=\frac{\tau^2}{4}\) equilibria are called ‘degenerate nodes,’ characterized by a repeated eigenvalue.

Stable points, which attract trajectories, are often called ‘sinks.’ Unstable points, which repel trajectories, are often called ‘sources,’ since solutions emanate from such a source.

Subsection 3.2.2 Ecological competition

Method.

To review, we apply the following method to study 2D nonlinear dynamical systems.

  1. Write a model.

  2. Read and interpret the model (dimensional analysis of parameters.

  3. Compute nullclines and equilibria.

  4. Plot phase portraits with direction arrows and trajectories.

  5. Refine knowledge of stability with linearization via Jacobian.

Consider two species \(A(t)\) and \(B(t)\) for ants and beetles, respectively. Assume that each grows (in the absence of the other) according to logistic growth,

\begin{equation*} \begin{aligned} \begin{cases} \displaystyle\frac{d^{} {A}}{d {t}^{}}&=r_A A\Big(1-\frac{A}{K_A}\Big)\\[10pt] \displaystyle\frac{d^{} {B}}{d {t}^{}}&=r_B B\Big(1-\frac{B}{K_B}\Big). \end{cases}\end{aligned} \end{equation*}

Here, each species grows independently of the other. Beetles have no effect on ants; ants have no effect on beetles. Our mechanism for interaction will be interference competition, which leads in increased mortality.

\begin{equation*} \begin{aligned} \begin{cases} \displaystyle\frac{d^{} {A}}{d {t}^{}}&=r_A A\Big(1-\frac{A}{K_A}-\frac{\alpha B}{K_A}\Big)\\[10pt] \displaystyle\frac{d^{} {B}}{d {t}^{}}&=r_B B\Big(1-\frac{B}{K_B}-\frac{\gamma A}{K_B}\Big). \end{cases}\end{aligned} \end{equation*}

To understand interspecific competition, we need to understand the units and magnitude of \(\alpha\) and \(\gamma\text{.}\) Roughly, \(\alpha\) translates from units of beetle density to an “effective ant density.” According to the ratio \(\frac{B}{K_A}\text{,}\) beetles ‘fill’ some of the carrying capacity allotted to ants. Just how much of the capacity that they fill is given by the parameter \(\alpha\text{,}\) which when multiplied by \(B\) gives the ‘ant equivalency’ of \(B\) beetles. We will look more closely at the equation for \(B\text{,}\) but note that we could make similar observations about the equation for \(A\text{.}\)

The dynamics of \(B\) are given by,

\begin{equation*} \frac{d^{} {B}}{d {t}^{}}=r_B B\Big(1-\frac{B+\gamma A}{K_B}\Big), \end{equation*}

which slightly more directly illustrates that both \(A\) and \(B\) fill the carrying capacity allotted to \(B\text{.}\) In one dimension, equilibria depend on the zeros of the right hand side of an equation like this. In two dimensions, equilibria depend on the values of both quantities \(A\) and \(B\text{.}\) The first step, as before, is to compute the nullclines for \(B\text{.}\) Setting \(\frac{d^{} {B}}{d {t}^{}}=0\) implies that either \(B=0\) or

\begin{equation*} 1-\frac{B+\gamma A}{K_B}=0. \end{equation*}

With a single species, this equation looks like

\begin{equation*} 1-\frac{\text{animals}}{K_B}=0. \end{equation*}

We can solve this equation for the density of animals at equilibrium to find,

\begin{equation*} \text{animals}=K_B. \end{equation*}

But we know that we have a particular composition of animals, such that

\begin{equation*} \text{animals}=B+\gamma A. \end{equation*}

Combining these equations,

\begin{equation*} K_B=B+\gamma A, \end{equation*}

gives \(B\) as a function of \(A\text{,}\) the abundance of \(A\) and \(B\) that guarantee that \(B\) is unchanging,

\begin{equation*} B=K_B -\gamma A. \end{equation*}

Subsection 3.2.3 Parameters, rates, and units: Prelude to chemical kinetics

Parameters, rates, and units.

We always need to keep in mind the fact that our parameters and rates have important units. We will review a few familiar examples to motivate this concept before moving on to biochemical kinetics of enzymatic reactions.

Example 3.2.7. SIR models.

Recall the SIR model (illustrated in Fig. [cross-reference to target(s) "fig::sir_flow" missing]), where \(S\text{,}\) \(I\text{,}\) and \(R\) are the host population densities of susceptible, infectious, and recovered (or removed) hosts. Susceptible hosts are infected at a rate \(\beta(N)\left(\frac{I}{N}\right)\) and infectious hosts recover (or are removed by death) at a rate \(\gamma\text{.}\) =[draw, fill=white, minimum size=3em, circle] =[ fill=white, minimum size=3em, circle]

The prevailing models in the literature are called ‘mass action’ and ‘constant contact’, which refers specifically to the choice of the function \(\beta(N)\) within our transmission function.

constant contact (frequency dependent) assumes that \(\beta(N)=\beta_0\)

\begin{equation*} \begin{aligned} \beta(N)\left(\frac{I}{N}\right)S&=\overset{ \overset{\displaystyle{\left[\frac{\text{1}}{\ text{time}}\right]}}{\big\downarrow}}{\beta_0} \times\overset{ \overset{\displaystyle{\left[\frac{\text{host}}{ \text{host}}\right]}}{\big\downarrow}}{\left(\ frac{I}{N}\right)} \times\overset{ \overset{\displaystyle{\left[\text{host}\right]} }{\big\downarrow}}{S} \end{aligned} \end{equation*}

mass action (density dependent) assumes that \(\beta(N)=\beta_1 N\)

\begin{equation*} \begin{aligned} \beta(N)\left(\frac{I}{N}\right)S&=\ beta_1 N\left(\frac{I}{N}\right)S\\ &=\underset {\underset{\displaystyle{\left[\frac{\text{1 }}{\text{host $\cdot$ time}}\right]}}{\big\uparrow}}{ \beta_1} \times\underset {\underset{\displaystyle{\left[\text{host}\ right]}}{\big\uparrow}}{ I}\times\underset {\underset{\displaystyle{\left[\text{host}\ right]}}{\big\uparrow}}{ S}\end{aligned} \end{equation*}

Notice that we have to assign different units to the ‘transmission parameter’ \(\beta(N)\) depending on its form.

Example 3.2.8. Predator - Prey equations.

Consider the densities of prey (\(X\)) and its predator (\(Y\)), given by the following (slightly incorrect, or at least misleading equations),

\begin{equation*} \begin{aligned} \frac{d^{} {X}}{d {t}^{}}&=rX-sXY\\ \frac{d^{} {Y}}{d {t}^{}}&=sXY-qY.\end{aligned} \end{equation*}

The interaction term, \(sXY\text{,}\) is the term that we focus on. This term represents predation in the \(\frac{d^{} {X}}{d {t}^{}}\)-equation, but growth in the \(\frac{d^{} {Y}}{d {t}^{}}\)-equation (see Fig. [cross-reference to target(s) "fig::PP_units" missing]).

Indeed, \(sXY\) describes the rate of predation on \(X\text{,}\) but it cannot describe, as it is now, the growth rate of \(Y\text{.}\) The unit’s in the second equation are inconsistent, thus, the model must be wrong! In fact, we have

\begin{equation*} \begin{aligned} \frac{d^{} {X}}{d {t}^{}}&=rX-sXY\\ \frac{d^{} {Y}}{d {t}^{}}&=\varepsilon sXY-qY, \end{aligned} \end{equation*}

where

\begin{equation*} \varepsilon\sim\frac{\text{predator}}{\ text{prey}}, \end{equation*}

is some (probably small) number describing the ‘conversion efficiency’ of producing new predators from consumed prey.

Michaelis-Menten kinetics.

The introduction to units will highlight the ‘fact’ that units in reaction kinetics are somewhat less tangible and slightly more difficult to interpret. We will study the dynamics of cell receptors (\(X_0, X_1\)) as they shuttle extracellular nutrients (\(N\)) into the cell. We denote the concentration of cellular nutrient by \(C\text{.}\)

We wish to apply the ‘law of mass action’ which simply states,

in a bimolecular reaction, the reaction rate is proportionalto the product of the concentrations of reactants.

Assuming that \([A]\) and \([B]\) are the concentrations of reactants,

\begin{equation*} \begin{aligned} \text{rate}&\propto [A][B]\\ &=k[A][B],\end{aligned} \end{equation*}

where the constant of proportionality, \(k\text{,}\) is called the ‘rate constant.’

Since the law of mass action relies on concentrations of reactants, we must reconsider our receptors and nutrients in terms of ‘concentrations’. For a given cellular component \(Z\text{,}\) the ‘volumetric concentration of \(Z\)’ is,

\begin{equation*} \left[\begin{tabular}{c}\text{concentration}\\\ text{of $Z$}\end{tabular}\right]=\left[\begin{tabular}{c}\ text{average number}\\\text{of $Z$ per cell}\end{tabular}\right]\times\left[\begin{tabular} {c}\text{average number of}\\\text{cells per volume}\end{tabular}\right]. \end{equation*}

Treating \(N\text{,}\) \(X_0\text{,}\) \(X_1\text{,}\) and \(C\) as volumetric concentrations we can use the reaction diagram (Fig. [cross-reference to target(s) "fig::nutrient_recept" missing]) to build differential equations to describe the changes in concentrations.

As an example, there are two ways that the extracellular nutrient concentration changes giving two terms to the \(\frac{d^{} {N}}{d {t}^{}}\) equation.

\begin{equation*} \begin{aligned} \frac{d^{} {N}}{d {t}^{}} & = -\left(\text{losses when free nutrient binds to empty receptor}\right)\\ &\qquad +\left(\text{gains when occupied receptors release nutrient extracellularly}\right)\\ & = -k_+ N X_0 + k_- X_1\end{aligned} \end{equation*}

Similarly, we can add the remaining equations,

\begin{equation*} \begin{aligned} \frac{d^{} {N}}{d {t}^{}} & = -k_+ N X_0 + k_- X_1\\ \frac{d^{} {X_0}}{d {t}^{}} & = -k_+ N X_0 + k_- X_1 + k_i X_i\\ \frac{d^{} {X_1}}{d {t}^{}} & = k_+ N X_0 - k_- X_1 - k_i X_i\\ \frac{d^{} {C}}{d {t}^{}} & = k_i X_1\end{aligned} \end{equation*}

Given this model, our first goal is to make some simplifications through astute observations. Both \(X_0\) and \(X_1\) are receptor states, and if we add the two differential equations,

\begin{equation*} \frac{d^{} {X_0}}{d {t}^{}}+\frac{d^{} {X_1}}{d {t}^{}}=0, \end{equation*}

we see that the total receptor concentration is conserved, \(R\text{.}\) With this we can write \(X_0=R-X_1.\)

\begin{equation*} \begin{aligned} \frac{d^{} {N}}{d {t}^{}} & = -k_+ N (R-X_1) + k_- X_1\\ \frac{d^{} {X_1}}{d {t}^{}} & = k_+ N (R-X_1) - k_- X_1 - k_i X_i\end{aligned} \end{equation*}

Reshuffling a bit,

\begin{equation*} \begin{aligned} \frac{d^{} {N}}{d {t}^{}} & = -k_+ N R+(k_+N + k_-) X_1\\ \frac{d^{} {X_1}}{d {t}^{}} & = k_+ N R- (k_+ N + k_- + k_i )X_i\end{aligned} \end{equation*}

We can study these equations in the phase plane. Assuming all parameters are positive, both nullclines saturate to a value \(X_1\rightarrow R\) at large \(N\text{.}\) Knowing very little about parameters, we know that the half saturation constant of the \(X_1\) nullcline is larger than that of the \(N\) nullcline. Subsection 3.2.4 Nullclines and Neuroscience

Following on the heels of the quasi-steady state assumption, we will close with an application of fast and slow dynamics that arises in mathematical models of neurons.

This example, called the FitzHugh-Nagumo model allows us to synthesize much of the material from continuous time dynamical systems: phase plane, equilibria, analysis, and approximation.
