Subsubsection 3.1.1 Introduction to models and notation

What is mathematical biology?
What is mathematics?

The study of number, quantity, space.

What is biology?

The study of life.

What is mathematical biology?

Using mathematical tools to study life.

  • Understanding the details of interactions between interacting populations of chemicals, cells, or individuals

  • Understanding or hypothesizing the mechanisms by which any biological quantities (numbers, densities, concentrations) change over time (or space)

Biological science vs. Physical science.

Physics has ‘laws’, biology has no such ‘laws’.

What are our goals for mathematical biology.

  • Draw qualitative conclusions about biological processes.

  • Apply simplified mechanisms to complex systems.

  • Guide thought experiments and physical experiments.

  • Occasionally make precise, quantitative predictions.

What is a mathematical model?
Features and definitions.

A mathematical model is an abstract mathematical description of any (biological) system, using logic, notation, and techniques of mathematics.

A familiar example.

Picture a tall, cylindrical water tower with radius \(r\) in which the height of the water is \(h\text{.}\) We want to know how fast the water level is dropping as we draw water from the bottom of the tank at a fixed flow. Our situation relies on the following relationship between volume of water and height of water.

\begin{equation*} (\text{change in volume})\propto(\text{change in height}) \end{equation*}

But what is the actual relationship between the two, and how do we replace ‘\(\propto\)’ with ‘\(=\)’? First,

\begin{equation*} V=\pi r^2 h, \end{equation*}

and differentiating,

\begin{equation*} \frac{d^{} {V}}{d {t}^{}} = \pi r^2 \frac{d^{} {h}}{d {t}^{}}. \end{equation*}

Pause to think about this. We want to know how the height \(h(t)\) is changing as a function of time. We are thinking of a cylinder with a fixed radius, \(r\text{.}\) We will assume that we are in control of the outflow from the tank, \(\displaystyle \frac{d^{} {V}}{d {t}^{}}\text{.}\)

We can write a model for the height,

\begin{equation*} \frac{d^{} {h}}{d {t}^{}}=\frac{\frac{d^{} {V}}{d {t}^{}}}{\pi r^2}. \end{equation*}

Let’s assume we fix the outflow at \(\displaystyle100\,\frac{\text{ft}^3}{\text{sec}}\text{,}\) by adjusting the outflow knob. The first consequence is that the volume of water in the tank is decreasing, so that \(\displaystyle\frac{d^{} {V}}{d {t}^{}}\) should be negative.

We’ll now use our model to study the dynamics of water depth in two theoretical water tanks: one tall and thin and the other short and thick.

When \(r=10\text{,}\)

\begin{equation*} \frac{d^{} {h}}{d {t}^{}}=\frac{-100\,\frac{\text{ft}^3}{\text{sec}}}{\pi \times(10 \text{ft})^2}=-\frac{1}{\pi}\,\frac{\text{ft}}{\text{sec}}. \end{equation*}

So that the water height drops about 1 foot in approximately 3 seconds.

When \(r=100\text{,}\)

\begin{equation*} \frac{d^{} {h}}{d {t}^{}}=\frac{-100\,\frac{\text{ft}^3}{\text{sec}}}{\pi \times(100 \text{ft})^2}=-\frac{1}{100\pi}\,\frac{\text{ft}}{\text{sec}}. \end{equation*}

So that the water height drops about 1 foot in approximately 5 minutes!

This model allows us to discuss two important and often confused concepts in modeling: the distinction between parameters and (state) variables. The “state variable” is the ‘thing’ that we are making an effort to model. Here it is the height, \(h(t)\) of the water in the tower. Everything else - the outflow volume \(\left(\displaystyle \frac{d^{} {V}}{d {t}^{}}\right)\) and the radius \((r)\) is known, or under our control. We call these things parameters. Changing parameters affects the evolution of state variables.

Why do we write models and what can we model?
Counting.

Models allow us to count things that might be too expensive, too time consuming, or otherwise impossible to count: animals, cells, proteins, viruses, molecules.

What do we count?

  • Populations of individual plants or animals that interact (Ecology)

  • Populations of cells within individuals that interact (Physiology, e.g., Immunology)

  • Populations of molecules and chemicals within cells that interact (Physiology, e.g., Neurobiology)

What does a model look like?

A model is a description of “how things change” (like the height of water in a water tower). Biologically similar things might change in very different ways and be governed by very different processes.

Thinking about time.

We should think about how the organisms, or components of our model, interact with one another.Dynamics of some plants and animals are characterized by discrete, non-overlapping generations.

  • Insects might reproduce over just a few days and be dormant over the remainder of the year.

  • Plants might set seeds over a short season.

Alternatively, we might have a set of measurements at a regular interval (e.g., 2-weeks) that we wish to explain. Many human diseases are often reported to organizations like CDC and WHO at weekly, bi-weekly, or monthly intervals (e.g, measles, cholera, HIV).

Other organisms like yeast, bacteria, mice, and so on, might be best described by ‘continuous-time’ dynamics.

Thinking about interactions.

Interactions might be characterized as linear or non-linear.

  • From chemistry, “first-order reactions” are linear - exponential growth or decay result from first order reactions (linear-models).

  • Reactions with multiple reactants (interactions between Na and Cl, foxes and bunnies, humans and zombies are give rise to nonlinear models - products or functions state variables.

Do we always arrive at the same answer?

Most of the models we study will be deterministic. This means our model (a set of rules, and initial conditions) will always give the same answer.

In contrast, some models can handle (random) variability between individuals or at different points in time. These are stochastic models, and while often more complicated, they allow us to ask and answer very different questions. As an example we’ll look at “birth-death process” models a bit later.

One-dimensional, Linear, discrete-time dynamical systems.
Example.

Consider a population of dandelions living in your front yard over the course of one summer. Let,

Our model for the growth of dandelions over the summer is,

\begin{equation*} N_{t+1}=r N_t, \end{equation*}

where \(N_t\) is the state variable and \(r\) is a parameter that characterizes our population. At the beginning of summer we are lucky enough to have only \(N_0=2\) dandelions in the front yard.

This is one of the few models we can actually solve.

\begin{equation*} \begin{aligned} N_1&=r N_0\\ N_2&=r N_1\\ &=r(r(N_0)\\ N_2&=r^2 N_0\end{aligned} \end{equation*}

In general, \(N_t=r^t N_0.\) As we have safely assumed, \(N_0\text{,}\) is a positive number (of flowers). When do the dandelions take over our yard?

\(N_t\) increases each generation if \(r>1\) and decreases each generation if \(r<1\text{.}\) Since we defined \(r\) as the ‘per capita production of new flowers,’ this should be comforting. If each flower does better than replacing itself we will always have an increasing number of flowers. If each flower fails to replace itself, our population will shrink. Solutions are tabulated in Tab. [cross-reference to target(s) "tab::dandelion_grow" missing].

We can extract more biological information by considering the factors contributing to the per capita production rate, \(r\text{.}\) Population growth depends on seedling recruitment and survival. Let,

We can re-write our model

\begin{equation*} \begin{aligned} N_{t+1} &= rN_t\\ &=s\sigma N_t+a N_t\\ N_{t+1} &= (s\sigma+a)N_t\end{aligned} \end{equation*}

This suggests a few ways that a population can grow,

  • highly productive adult plants that generate lots of seedlings (large \(s\))

  • highly viable seedlings that are likely to survive to ‘adulthood’

  • tough adult plants that are likely to survive

In all, we need \(r=s\sigma+a>1\) for the plants to grow.

Thinking about equilibria is key to understanding dynamics of dandelions. Equilibria (or steady-states, or fixed-points) are values at which the solution is unchanging. With our notation this means \(N_{t+1}=N_t=N^*\text{.}\) By inspection of Figure [cross-reference to target(s) "fig::dandelion" missing], the only equilibrium point is \(N^*=0\text{,}\) this is the only place that either of our labeled lines intersect the diagonal.

Subsubsection 3.1.2 Linear discrete-time models (2D)

More on linear models (1D).
Example II.

It might be the case, however, that our neighbors secretly transplant dandelions in our yards at night. To mimic this unkind reality, assume that \(F\geq0\) flowers are arriving each week. Our new model is,

\begin{equation*} N_{t+1}=r N_t+F. \end{equation*}

How does ‘flower immigration’ affect dynamics? If we replace both \(N_{t+1}\) and \(N_t\) by \(N^*\) the equilibrium value, we have

\begin{equation*} N^*=rN^*+F. \end{equation*}

This gives us a value for the flower population,

\begin{equation*} N^*=\frac{F}{1-r}, \end{equation*}

which tells us that \(N^*>0\) if \(r<1\) and \(N^*<0\) if \(r>1\text{.}\) It is important to notice early on that only non-negative equilibria make sense on most biological situations, especially with regard to population dynamics. With immigration (importation of plants), solutions can be traced on the diagrams in Fig. [cross-reference to target(s) "fig::dandelion2" missing].

Computational trick..

One way to solve this problem is to study deviations from the equilibrium, \(N^*\text{.}\) We’re going to assume that \(r<1\text{,}\) since it is only then that we have a sensible equilibrium.

Let \(x_t\) be the deviation from equilibrium at time \(t\) so that,

\begin{equation*} \begin{aligned} {2} N_t &=N^*+x_t & \qquad\text{(the definition)}\\ N_0&=N^*+x_0 & \qquad\text{(the initial condition)}\\\end{aligned} \end{equation*}

And finally,

\begin{equation*} \begin{aligned} {2} x_{t+1} &=N_{t+1}-N^* & \qquad\text{(from the definition)}\\ x_{t+1} &=\left(rN_t+F\right)-N^* & \qquad\text{(from the model)}\\ x_{t+1} &=\left(r\left(N^*+x_t\right)+F\right)-N^* & \qquad\text{(from the definition)}\\ x_{t+1} &=\left(rN^*+F\right)+rx_t-N^* & \qquad\text{(shuffle)}\\ x_{t+1} &=\left(N^*\right)+rx_t-N^* & \qquad\text{(equilibrium condition)}\\ x_{t+1} &=rx_t & \qquad\text{(evolution of }x_t\text{)}\\\end{aligned} \end{equation*}

So, it looks like \(x_t\) evolves according to

\begin{equation*} x_t=r^tx_0. \end{equation*}

Which gives,

\begin{equation*} \begin{aligned} N_t&=N^*+x_t\\ &=N^*+r^tx_0\\ &=N^*+r^t\left(N_0-N^*\right)\\ N_t&=(1-r^t)N^*+r^tN_0.\end{aligned} \end{equation*}

Thus, the solution is a weighted average of \(N^*\) and \(N_0\text{,}\) and depends on the weight \(r\text{.}\) For \(r<1\) weight moves from \(N_0\) to \(N^*\) as \(t\) increases (the abundance of the initial flowers is reduced). Since we have a formula for \(N^*\text{,}\) we can also plug it into the final equation above

\begin{equation*} \begin{aligned} {2}N_t&=(1-r^t)\left(\frac{F}{1-r}\right) + r^tN_0 & \hspace{-2cm}\text{(from the equilibrium, }N^*)\\ N_t&=\left(\sum_{k=0}^{n-1} r^k\right)F + r^tN_0 & \hspace{-2cm}\text{(geometric series)}\\ N_t&=\left(1+r+r^2+\dots+r^{t-1}\right)F + r^tN_0 & \hspace{-2cm}\text{(expanding a geometric progression)}\\ N_t&=\underbrace{F}_{\text{\begin{tabular}{c}current \\[-6pt]immigrant\\[-6pt] flowers\end{tabular}}}+\underbrace{rF}_{\text{\begin{tabular}{c}last week's\\[-6pt] immigrant \\[-6pt]flowers\end{tabular}}} + r^2F+ \dots +\underbrace{r^{t-1}F}_{\text{\begin{tabular}{c}first week's \\[-6pt] immigrant \\[-6pt]flowers\end{tabular}}} + \underbrace{r^tN_0}_{\text{\begin{tabular}{c}initial flowers\\[-6pt] that remain\end{tabular}}} & \hspace{-2.5cm}\text{(distributing terms)}\end{aligned} \end{equation*}

We can see what ‘generations’ form our population at any given week using the last of the above formulas. Of course, we could have started here by applying our model recursively. But, this approach gives some nice information about our solution in terms of equilibria (\(N^*\)), initial values (\(N_0\)), and perturbations (\(x_t\)).

Matrix models: 2D, Linear, Discrete-time Dynamics.

Recall our example concerning weekly dandelion dynamics. Previously, we counted only the number of flowering plants present in our ‘yard’ each week. We can count a bit more carefully, and take advantage of more biological information if we think about the ’structure’ of the population. Ignoring seeds, in reality we have at least a few ways to break down the plant community:

  • juveniles vs. adults

  • seedlings vs. vegetative vs. flowering vs. seed-setting

  • non-reproductive vs. reproductive

We will lump together the seedlings and vegetative plans as ‘non-reproductive’ and the flowering and seed-setting plants as ‘reproductive’. Let \(V_t\) be the number of non-reproductive (vegetative) plants in the yard and \(R_t\) be the number of reproductive plants in the yard, both at week \(t\text{.}\)

We will use familiar notation for parameters. Since it is challenging to count seeds after they have left the plant, and nobody vacuums seeds from the yard, we’ll focus on the life stages above.

This means we can write

\begin{equation*} \begin{aligned} V_{t+1}&=0\cdot V_t+s\cdot R_t\\ R_{t+1}&=\sigma \cdot V_t+ a\cdot R_t.\end{aligned} \end{equation*}

This looks like a matrix equation,

\begin{equation*} \left(\begin{tabular}{c}$V_{t+1}$\\$R_{t+1}$\end{tabular}\right) = \left(\begin{tabular}{cc}$0$ & $s$\\$\sigma$ & $a$\end{tabular}\right)\left(\begin{tabular}{c}$V_{t}$\\$R_{t}$\end{tabular}\right). \end{equation*}

With a single equation, it was easy enough to determine growth. This is less obvious with a higher dimensional model. Additionally, we might be interested in the following questions:

  • Will the population grow?

  • If it grows, how fast is it going to grow?

  • What is the balance between reproductive and non-reproductive dandelions in the population?

For a plant starting in the non-reproductive state (a one week old plant), we track its lifetime reproduction in the following table:

Next we append a column for the reproductive value of each age (remembering that our model only tracks two age classes, but once plants flower they could survive indefinitely):

Starting from seedling, the expected lifetime reproduction of a plant is

\begin{equation*} ELR=s\sigma+s\sigma a + s\sigma a^2 + \dots . \end{equation*}

By definition \(a\) is a weekly survival probability for adult plants, so \(a\leq1\text{.}\) Keep this in mind as we press on:

\begin{equation*} \begin{aligned} ELR & = s\sigma+s\sigma a + s\sigma a^2 + \dots \\ & = s\sigma\left(1+a+a^2+\dots\right)\\ & = s\sigma \sum_{k=0}^\infty a^k\end{aligned} \end{equation*}

Knowing what we do about geometric series,

\begin{equation*} ELR = s\sigma \sum_{k=0}^\infty a^k = s\sigma\left(\frac{1}{1-a}\right). \end{equation*}

So, \(ELR >1\) when \(s\sigma>1-a\text{.}\) This is identical to our ‘adult plant’-only model result,

\begin{equation*} s\sigma+a>1! \end{equation*}
Questions.

  • What’s so special about ELR as a measure of growth?

  • How does ELR relate to eigenvalues?

Answers.

For a matrix \(A\text{,}\) like our transition matrix above, a number \(\lambda\) is an eigenvalue with eigenvector \(x\) if

\begin{equation*} Ax=\lambda x. \end{equation*}

The eigenvalue reduces the dimension in multidimensional problems. It turns matrix multiplication into scalar multiplication. How do we find eigenvalues?

Linear algebra review.

We first want to write our eigenvalue problem as:

\begin{equation*} Ax-\lambda x=0. \end{equation*}

Then, since \(\lambda\) is a number,

\begin{equation*} (A-\lambda I)x=0, \end{equation*}

where \(I\) is the appropriate ‘identity matrix’.

Let’s take

\begin{equation*} A= \left(\begin{tabular}{cc}$a$ & $b$\\$c$ & $d$\end{tabular}\right), \end{equation*}

so that

\begin{equation*} A-\lambda I= \left(\begin{tabular}{cc}$a-\lambda$ & $b$\\$c$ & $d-\lambda$\end{tabular}\right). \end{equation*}

Eigenvalues solve

\begin{equation*} \operatorname{det}(A-\lambda I)=0. \end{equation*}
\begin{equation*} \begin{aligned} 0&=\operatorname{det}(A-\lambda I)\\ &=(a-\lambda)(d-\lambda)-bc\\ 0&=\lambda^2-(a+d)\lambda+ad-bc\end{aligned} \end{equation*}

Solutions are

\begin{equation*} \lambda_{\pm}=\frac{(a+d)\pm\sqrt{(a+d)^2-4(ad-bc)}}{2}. \end{equation*}

From here it helps to consider specific examples so we do not have to solve equations containing obtuse algebraic expressions.

Subsubsection 3.1.3 Linear discrete-time models (2D)

Question..

How do structured and unstructured populations differ?From a one dimensional model ignoring plant developmental stage,

\begin{equation*} N_t=r N_t, \end{equation*}

where \(r=s\sigma+a\text{,}\) we wrote a two-dimensional model,

\begin{equation*} P_{t+1}=MP_t, \end{equation*}

where \(P_0=(V_0, R_0)^T\) and

\begin{equation*} M=\left(\begin{tabular}{cc}$0$ & $s$\\$\sigma$ & $a$\end{tabular}\right). \end{equation*}

Keeping with our example, eigenvalues of \(M\) are found using

\begin{equation*} \det\left(\begin{tabular}{cc}$-\lambda$ & $s$\\$\sigma$ & $a-\lambda$\end{tabular}\right)=0. \end{equation*}

This gives

\begin{equation*} \begin{aligned} 0&=-\lambda(a-\lambda)-s\sigma\\ &=\lambda^2-a\lambda-s\sigma\end{aligned} \end{equation*}

Using the quadratic formula,

\begin{equation*} \lambda_\pm = \frac{a\pm\sqrt{a^2+4s\sigma}}{2}. \end{equation*}

Suppose we know the initial plant distribution,

\begin{equation*} P_0=\left(\begin{tabular}{c}$V_0$\\$R_0$\end{tabular}\right), \end{equation*}

the solution to our model is

\begin{equation*} P_t=M^tP_0. \end{equation*}

First write, \(P_0=c_1 v_1+c_2 v_2\text{,}\) where \(c_1, c_2\) are constants and \(v_1, v_2\) are eigenvectors. We are assuming that \(v_1, v_2\) are linearly independent, thus they form a basis in two-dimensions, such that any other vector can be written as a linear combination.

\begin{equation*} \begin{aligned} {2} P_1&=A P_0 & \quad\text{(start)}\\ & = A(c_1 v_1+c_2 v_2)& \quad\text{(basis vectors)}\\ & = c_1 Av_1+c_2 Av_2& \quad\text{(distributive property)}\\ P_1& = c_1 \lambda_1 v_1+c_2 \lambda_2v_2& \quad\text{(eigenvalues!)}\end{aligned} \end{equation*}

We can compute \(P_2\text{,}\)

\begin{equation*} \begin{aligned} P_2&=A P_1\\ & = A(c_1 \lambda_1 v_1+c_2 \lambda_2 v_2)\\ & = (c_1 \lambda_1) Av_1+(c_2\lambda_2) Av_2\\ & = c_1 \lambda_1^2 v_1+c_2 \lambda_2^2v_2\end{aligned} \end{equation*}

And similarly,

\begin{equation*} P_t=c_1\lambda_1^tv_1+c_2\lambda_2^tv_2. \end{equation*}

If we assume that \(|\lambda_1|>|\lambda_2|\text{,}\) then

\begin{equation*} P_t\approx c_1 \lambda_1^t v_1. \end{equation*}

We will see how this approximation works at the end of the following example. But, perhaps motivated by this approximation, we see that the magnitude of \(\lambda_1\) is important to the growth of the population, both \(V_t\) and \(R_t\text{.}\) The population changes by a factor \(\lambda_1\) each generation. It grows if \(\lambda_1>1\) and shrinks if \(\lambda_1<1\text{.}\)

Let \(\lambda_1\) be the positive eigenvalue, \(\lambda_1>1\text{,}\)

\begin{equation*} \begin{aligned} \lambda_1 = \frac{a+\sqrt{a^2+4s\sigma}}{2}&>1\\ a+\sqrt{a^2+4s\sigma}&>2\\ \sqrt{a^2+4s\sigma}&>2-a\\ a^2+4s\sigma&>(2-a)^2\\ a^2+4s\sigma&>4-4a+a^2\\ 4s\sigma&>4-4a\\ s\sigma&>1-a\\ s\sigma+a&>1\end{aligned} \end{equation*}

The positive eigenvalue \(\lambda_1>1\) if \(r=s\sigma+a>1\text{,}\) but in general, \(ELR\neq\lambda_1\text{!}\) But, if \(\lambda_1>1\) so is \(ELR\text{,}\) and vice versa. Again, our characteristic polynomial, that governs the eigenvalues is

\begin{equation*} C(\lambda)=\lambda^2-a\lambda-s\sigma. \end{equation*}

Since \(s, \sigma>0\text{,}\) we have \(C(0)<0\) and we know that \(C(\lambda)\) is concave up. If \(C(1)<0\text{,}\) we know that the leading eigenvalue \(\lambda>1\text{.}\) Lastly, let’s study the relationships between \(1, \lambda_+\text{,}\) and \(r\text{.}\) We’ve already looked at some of the relationships involving \(1\text{.}\) Now, \(\lambda<r=s\sigma+a\) when

\begin{equation*} \begin{aligned} 0&<C(r)\\ 0&<r^2-ar-s\sigma\\ s\sigma&<r(r-a)\\ s\sigma&<(s\sigma+a)(s\sigma+a-a)\\ s\sigma&<s\sigma(s\sigma+a)\\ 0&<s\sigma(s\sigma+a)-s\sigma\\ 0&<s\sigma(s\sigma+a-1)\end{aligned} \end{equation*}

This is satisfied when \(r=s\sigma+a>1\text{,}\) thus \(1<\lambda<ELR\text{.}\) For the reverse, \(ELR<\lambda<1\) when \(r=s\sigma+a<1\text{.}\) We will also look at the relationships between \(r\text{,}\) \(ELR\text{,}\) and \(\lambda\) in the example, so that we can actually compute eigenpair.

Example..

Recall \(r=s\sigma+a\) was the per generation growth rate from the one-equation model. In that model, we made no distinction between plant growth stages, an assumed that all plants were capable of reproducing. When we considered the reproductive schedule of our plants, we derived \(ELR\) as a measure of the replacement rate of each individual plant. Let \(a=\frac{2}{3}\text{,}\) \(s=\frac{16}{9}\text{,}\) \(\sigma=\frac{1}{2}\text{,}\) so that, \(r=\left(\frac{16}{9}\right)\left(\frac{1}{2}\right)+ \left(\frac{2}{3}\right)= \left(\frac{14}{9}\right)\approx1.\overline{55}\text{.}\) Also, \(ELR=\frac{\left(\frac{16}{9}\right)\left(\frac{1}{2}\right)}{\frac{1}{3}}=\frac{8}{3}\approx2.\overline{66}\text{.}\)When vegetative plants are less likely to mature (small \(\sigma\text{,}\) Fig. [cross-reference to target(s) "fig::elr_eig" missing]) weekly adult survivorship, \(a\text{,}\) must increase in order for population growth to occur (\(r, ELR, \lambda>1\)).

Question..

What is the effect of population structure (tracking flower types), in growing and shrinking flower populations (see Fig. [cross-reference to target(s) "fig::elr_eig" missing])?

Answer..

Imposing structure reduces the growth rate (relative to \(r\)) in growing populations. Imposing structure slows the decay rate (relative to \(r\)) in shrinking populations.In this parameterization,

\begin{equation*} \begin{aligned} \lambda_\pm &= \frac{\frac{2}{3}\pm\sqrt{\frac{4}{9}+4\left(\frac{16}{9}\right)\left(\frac{1}{2}\right)}}{2}\\ &=\frac{1}{3}\pm\frac{1}{2}\sqrt{\frac{4}{9}+\frac{32}{9}}\\ &=\frac{1}{3}\pm1\\ \lambda_\pm&=\frac{4}{3}, -\frac{2}{3}.\end{aligned} \end{equation*}
\begin{equation*} \left(\begin{tabular}{cc}$0$ & $\frac{16}{9}$\\[2pt]$\frac{1}{2}$ & $\frac{2}{3}$\end{tabular}\right)\left(\begin{tabular}{c}$x_1$\\$x_2$\end{tabular}\right)= \frac{4}{3}\left(\begin{tabular}{c}$x_1$\\$x_2$\end{tabular}\right). \end{equation*}

Notice that \(ELR>\lambda_+>1\text{,}\) so that the population grows.

The eigenvector associated with \(\lambda=\frac{4}{3}\) is of the form

\begin{equation*} v_1=\left(\begin{tabular}{c}$x_1$\\$\frac{3}{4}x_1$\end{tabular}\right). \end{equation*}

The eigenvector associated with \(\lambda=-\frac{2}{3}\) is of the form

\begin{equation*} v_1=\left(\begin{tabular}{c}$x_1$\\$-\frac{3}{8}x_1$\end{tabular}\right). \end{equation*}
Question..

How do the eigenvectors help explain long term population growth (see Fig. [cross-reference to target(s) "fig::SAD" missing])?

Answer..

Long-term proportions approach the distribution described by the leading eigenvector. Numbers increase geometrically.Start with a typical initial condition \(P_0=(V_0, R_0)=(0,1)\text{.}\) We first want to express \(P_0\) as a linear combination of eigenvectors.

\begin{equation*} \left(\begin{tabular}{c}$0$\\$1$\end{tabular}\right) = c_1\left(\begin{tabular}{c}$1$\\$\frac{3}{4}$\end{tabular}\right)+c_2\left(\begin{tabular}{c}$1$\\$-\frac{3}{8}$\end{tabular}\right). \end{equation*}

Skipping a few calculations, \(c_1=-c_2\) and \(c_1=\frac{8}{9}\text{.}\) Finally we can write,

\begin{equation*} \begin{aligned} P_1&=A P_0\\ & = A\left(c_1\left(\begin{tabular}{c}$1$\\$\frac{3}{4}$\end{tabular}\right)+c_2 \left(\begin{tabular}{c}$1$\\$-\frac{3}{8}$\end{tabular}\right)\right)\\ & = c_1 A\left(\begin{tabular}{c}$1$\\$\frac{3}{4}$\end{tabular}\right)+c_2 A\left(\begin{tabular}{c}$1$\\$-\frac{3}{8}$\end{tabular}\right)\\ P_1& = c_1 \lambda_1^2 \left(\begin{tabular}{c}$1$\\$\frac{3}{4}$\end{tabular}\right)+c_2 \lambda_2^2\left(\begin{tabular}{c}$1$\\$-\frac{3}{8}$\end{tabular}\right)\\ P_1& = \left(\frac{8}{9}\right) \left(\frac{4}{3}\right)^2 \left(\begin{tabular}{c}$1$\\$\frac{3}{4}$\end{tabular}\right)- \left(\frac{8}{9}\right) \left(\frac{2}{3}\right)^2\left(\begin{tabular}{c}$1$\\$-\frac{3}{8}$\end{tabular}\right),\end{aligned} \end{equation*}

as before. Iterating this solution we wind up with,

\begin{equation*} P_t=\frac{8}{9}\left(\frac{4}{3}\right)^t\left(\begin{tabular}{c}$1$\\$\frac{3}{4}$\end{tabular}\right)-\frac{8}{9}\left(\frac{2}{3}\right)^t\left(\begin{tabular}{c}$1$\\$-\frac{3}{8}$\end{tabular}\right) \end{equation*}

Since the second term contains a value \(\lambda_2<1\) which is raised to a larger and larger power, its contribution decreases. If we ignore it (at large \(t\)),

\begin{equation*} P_t\approx\frac{8}{9}\left(\frac{4}{3}\right)^t\left(\begin{tabular}{c}$1$\\$\frac{3}{4}$\end{tabular}\right). \end{equation*}

The population will grow 33% larger each generation, but the proportion of young (non-reproductive) and old (reproductive) plants will remain fixed. This suggests that juvenile (tasty young plants) will outnumber the older flowering plants, specifically, the flowering plants will be 75% the abundance of young plants. Alternatively, young plants will be 33% more abundant than old plants. It might also be useful to use our leading eigenvector approximation, rather than our exact matrix solution. We plot the approximate dynamics of each plant type, as well as the comparison of the exact and approximate solutions in Fig. [cross-reference to target(s) "fig::exac_app" missing]

Subsubsection 3.2.1 Nonlinear discrete-time models (1D)

Review: Linear models.

We want to outline our options for solving linear, discrete-time, matrix models. We’ll take a model

\begin{equation*} P_{t+1}=M P_t, \end{equation*}

for now we will think of \(M\) as a matrix and \(P_t\) as a vector.

per capita production and nonlinear effects.

So far our model has roughly been,

\begin{equation*} N_{t+1}=r N_t, \end{equation*}

or

\begin{equation*} P_{t+1}=M P_{t}, \end{equation*}

where \(N_t\) and \(r\) are scalars, or \(P_t\) is a vector and \(M\) is a matrix.

As an example of a 1D model, our linear model assumes that

\begin{equation*} \frac{N_{t+1}}{N_t}=r, \end{equation*}

or the ratio of current population to previous population is \(r\text{,}\) no matter the size of the previous generation. Instead, we might expect that, for a variety of reasons (like space, food, aggression, or lack of resources) this ratio decreases as the current population size increases. Mathematically, this requires \(r=r(N_t)\text{.}\)

In words, small populations are capable of large increases from one generation to the next. But, large populations might only increase by slight amounts over generations. In fact, really large populations might actually shrink. The comparison of linear and nonlinear models is given in Fig. [cross-reference to target(s) "fig::pcp" missing].

Notice the peculiarity of the black line (or curve) in the right panel of Fig. [cross-reference to target(s) "fig::pcp" missing](or [cross-reference to target(s) "fig::nonlin_upd" missing]). It quite happily ‘goes negative’, which means for an initially large enough population, the subsequent generation is multiplied by a negative number. Simply stated, that is weird. Fortunately, our other models circumvent this problem by requiring \(p.c.p.>0\) at all population sizes.

Multiplying \(p.c.p\) by population size \(N_t\) gives our updating function, \(f(N_t)=(p.c.p.)\times N_t\text{.}\) Then, our model is

\begin{equation*} N_{t+1}=f(N_t)=(p.c.p.)\times N_t. \end{equation*}

Some sample updating functions are plotted in Fig. [cross-reference to target(s) "fig::upd" missing]. Notice that there are some major qualitative changes when we compare the line in the left panel to any of the curves in the right panel. Most importantly, the updating function intersects the resetting line more than once. Again, these intersections suggest that

\begin{equation*} N_{t+1}=N_t=N^*. \end{equation*}

Recalling our previous notation and definitions, these nonlinear models possess multiple equilibria!

Example..

We now consider a population \(G_t\text{,}\) which is the number of guppies living in a bowl on our desk. For simplicity, we will assume that we start with a female guppy who is gravid. Guppies are fascinating creatures that can spawn indefinitely after mating once. We’ll ignore the fact that she gives birth to males and females, so that ‘number of guppies’ above actually means ‘number of female guppies.

Guppies are fascinating, but cruel - they are quite happy to eat each other. It’s hard to track, but we know that as the guppy tank fills we see fewer baby guppies (i.e., per capita production drops as in Fig. [cross-reference to target(s) "fig::pcp" missing]). For a model of guppy dynamics, we will assume

\begin{equation*} G_{t+1}=rG_t e^{-kG_t}. \end{equation*}

This model has been sketched above in the green curves of Figs. [cross-reference to target(s) "fig::pcp" missing], [cross-reference to target(s) "fig::upd" missing].

For a particular choice of parameters we find the following diagram and iterated solution.

It appears that these solutions converge to a value \(G^*\approx8.4\) guppies. Can we do better than this guess? We know that our equilibrium satisfies,

\begin{equation*} \begin{aligned} G^*&=rG^* e^{-kG^*}\\ 1&=r e^{-kG^*}\\ e^{kG^*}&=r \\ kG^*&=\ln r \\ G^*&=\frac{\ln r }{k}\end{aligned} \end{equation*}

Immediately, we should see a few things. First \(G^*=0\) is always a solution to the first equation. Ignoring that, for \(k>0\text{,}\) we have a positive equilibrium only when \(r>1\) so that the logarithm is positive. We now know that for \(r=3.7, k=0.15\text{,}\) \(G^*\approx8.722\text{.}\)

Depending on the intrinsic growth rate \(r\) (rmax in the code) of our guppies, we might see oscillatory or non-oscillatory approaches to equilibria. The solution generated by the black updating function (with \(r=1\)) approaches \(G^*=0\) (i.e., it has no ‘nontrivial’ equilibrium point). Looking again at Fig. [cross-reference to target(s) "fig::f3_rmax" missing], try to uncover a relationship between the tendency of the trajectory to oscillate (right panel) and the crossing point of the updating function and resetting line (left panel).

A note on stability.

As before (perturbations of linear non-homogeneous models), let \(G_t=G^*+x_t\) and recall \(G_{t+1}=rG_t e^{-kG_t}=F(G_t)\text{.}\) We have,

\begin{equation*} \begin{aligned} \begin{split} x_{t+1}&=G_{t+1}-G^*\\ &=F(G_t)-G^*\\ &=\left(F(G^*)+F'(G^*)x_t+{\mathcal{O}}(x_t^2)\right)-G^*\\ &=\left(\cancel{G^*}+F'(G^*)x_t+\cancel{{\mathcal{O}}(x_t^2)}\right)-\cancel{G^*}\\ x_{t+1}&=F'(G^*)x_t \end{split}\end{aligned} \end{equation*}

Now, perturbations are governed by the linear equation

\begin{equation*} x_{t+1}=F'(G^*)x_t=ax_t, \end{equation*}

where \(\displaystyle a=\left.\frac{d^{} {F}}{d {G_t}^{}}\right|_{G^*}\) describes its growth. The slope of the updating function governs stability. When,

  • \(a\) is ‘too steep’ at \(G^*\) perturbations near \(G^*\) grow, so solutions diverge from \(G^*\text{.}\)

  • \(a\) is ‘shallow enough’ at \(G^*\) perturbations near \(G^*\) decay, so solutions converge to \(G^*\text{.}\)

\begin{equation*} N_{t+1}=N_t=N^*. \end{equation*}

This suggests an alternative method to identify equilibria, which might sometimes be pictured more simply:

Equilibria satisfy \(N_{t+1}=N_t,\) and we denote them by \(N^*=N_{t+1}=N_t\text{.}\) Given a model \(N_{t+1}=f(N_t)N_t\text{,}\) we can see that equilibria occur at points where \(f(N_t)=1\text{.}\) At values of \(N_t\) where our per capita production takes a value of one, we have equilibria.

As before (perturbations of linear non-homogeneous models), let \(G_t=G^*+x_t\) and recall \(G_{t+1}=rG_t e^{-kG_t}=F(G_t)\text{.}\) We have,

\begin{equation*} \begin{aligned} \begin{split} x_{t+1}&=G_{t+1}-G^*\\ &=F(G_t)-G^*\\ &=\left(F(G^*)+F'(G^*)x_t+{\mathcal{O}}(x_t^2)\right)-G^*\\ &=\left(\cancel{G^*}+F'(G^*)x_t+\cancel{{\mathcal{O}}(x_t^2)}\right)-\cancel{G^*}\\ x_{t+1}&=F'(G^*)x_t \end{split}\end{aligned} \end{equation*}

Now, the dynamics of perturbations are governed by the linear equation

\begin{equation*} x_{t+1}=F'(G^*)x_t=ax_t, \end{equation*}

where \(\displaystyle a=\left.\frac{d^{} {F}}{d {G_t}^{}}\right|_{G^*}\) describes its growth. The slope of the updating function at or near the equilibrium point governs stability. When,

  • \(a\) is ‘too steep’ at \(G^*\) perturbations near \(G^*\) grow, so solutions diverge from \(G^*\text{.}\)

  • \(a\) is ‘shallow enough’ at \(G^*\) perturbations near \(G^*\) decay, so solutions converge to \(G^*\text{.}\)

From our linear theory, we know that \(x_t\rightarrow0\) when \(|a|<1\text{.}\)

Example (cont)..

Evaluated at the equilibrium point, \(G^*=\frac{\ln(r)}{k}\text{,}\)

We need \(|F'(G^*)|<1\text{,}\) see Fig. [cross-reference to target(s) "fig::rick_bif" missing]. Also, \(G^*=0\) is stable when \(r<1\text{.}\)

Subsubsection 3.2.4 Nonlinear discrete-time models (2D)

Motivation: the discrete logistic.

The discrete logistic model is

\begin{equation*} Y_{t+1}=r(1-Y_t)Y_t, \end{equation*}

where \(Y_t\) between \([0,1]\) is the ‘scaled density’ of hosts, and \(p.c.p.=r(1-Y_t).\) Expanding the model we can write,

\begin{equation*} Y_{t+1}=rY_t-(rY_t)Y_t. \end{equation*}

Reading the model in this form, we can think of constant per capita births \(r\) but it looks like the per capita mortality is of the form \(rY_t\text{.}\) One interpretation of this is,

  • births are independent of population density

  • death increases with population density

What if instead of your own species controlling your mortality (assuming you are a \(Y_t\)) a second species, \(Z_t\text{,}\) is responsible for your mortality. We’ll assume that it benefits from eating you, but would otherwise die out, its a specialist. We will assume that it is either better or worse at eating you than you are at eating/killing yourself, so that your per capita mortality as a function of the density of \(Z_t\) is \(pZ_t\text{.}\) We can rewrite ‘your’ equation as,

\begin{equation*} Y_{t+1}=rY_t-(pZ_t)Y_t. \end{equation*}

We’ll assume that a \(Z_t\) turns some fraction \(\epsilon\) of consumed \(Y_t\)’s into new \(Z_t\) (i.e., growth of \(Z_t\) depends directly on the amount of \(Y_t\) consumed). But again, in the absence of food \(Z_t\) will die. The equation for \(Z_t\) looks like,

\begin{equation*} Z_{t+1}=\epsilon(pY_t)Z_t-mZ_t. \end{equation*}

Putting these together, we have our first 2D nonlinear system,

\begin{equation*} \begin{aligned} Y_{t+1}&=rY_t-pZ_tY_t\\ Z_{t+1}&=\epsilon pY_tZ_t+mZ_t, \quad\text{this should be a $+m$ not a $-m$!}\end{aligned} \end{equation*}

Notice that we could define \(g=\epsilon p\) if we wanted to simplify (the appearance) of the first term in \(Z_{t+1}\text{.}\)

\begin{equation*} \begin{aligned} Y_{t+1}&=rY_t-pZ_tY_t\\ Z_{t+1}&= gY_tZ_t+mZ_t, \quad\text{this should be a $+m$ not a $-m$}!\end{aligned} \end{equation*}
An approach to nonlinear, multi-species dynamics..

To study this model we could,

  • Iterate solutions numerically

  • Attempt to ‘solve it’

To iterate our model, we would have to have parameter values and a suitable initial condition. We might not have a clue what these values are so we will ignore that approach and try to ‘solve it.’ Solving such a model typically means,

  • compute equilibria

  • compute linearization

  • analyze stability

As before, equilibria requires \(Y_{t+1}=Y_t=Y^*\) and simultaneously \(Z_{t+1}=Z_t=Z^*\text{.}\)

\begin{equation*} \begin{aligned} Y^*&=rY^* -pZ^*Y^*\\ Z^*&= gY^*Z^*+mZ^*, \quad\text{this should be a $+m$ not a $-m$!}\end{aligned} \end{equation*}

To satisfy the first, either \(Y^*=0\) or \(1=r-pZ^*\text{,}\) the second of which implies,

\begin{equation*} Z^*=\frac{r-1}{p}. \end{equation*}

To satisfy the second, either \(Z^*=0\) or \(1=gY^*+m,\) the second of which implies,

\begin{equation*} Y^*=\frac{1-m}{g}. \end{equation*}

Thus our equilibria are \((Y^*, Z^*)=(0, 0)\) or \((Y^*, Z^*)=(\frac{1-m}{g}, \frac{r-1}{p})\text{.}\)

We have to figure out how to determine stability at each equilibrium point to know whether or not species persist or go extinct. We might think that linear matrix theory, determinants, and eigenvalues would be of use here. Our first step is to form a linear approximation to our nonlinear dynamics near equilibria. To do so, we compute the Jacobian matrix, a matrix of partial derivatives.

We notice that our model can be written as a set of two updating functions each depending on two variables,

\begin{equation*} \begin{aligned} Y_{t+1}&=F(Y_t, Z_t)=rY_t-pZ_tY_t\\ Z_{t+1}&= G(Y_t, Z_t)=gY_tZ_t+mZ_t, \quad\text{this should be a $+m$ not a $-m$!}\end{aligned} \end{equation*}

Now, the motivation and methodology are the same as with 1D models, we are interested in thinking about \(Y_t=Y^*+y_t\) and \(Z_t=Z^*+z_t\text{,}\) which means each solution (e.g., \(Y_t\)) is a combination of equilibrium value (e.g., \(Y^*\)) and some small perturbation (e.g., \(y_t\)). The Jacobian is a matrix of partial derivatives of our updating functions with respect to the state variables. We could derive this as we did with a Taylor series expansion of a 1D model.

Then, skipping some steps, we can compute the Jacobian as follows (we will drop the subscripts for simplicity),

\begin{equation*} J(Y, Z)=\left(\begin{array}{cc} \frac{\partial^{} {F}}{\partial {Y}^{}} & \frac{\partial^{} {F}}{\partial {Z}^{}}\\[8pt] \frac{\partial^{} {G}}{\partial {Y}^{}} & \frac{\partial^{} {G}}{\partial {Z}^{}} \end{array}\right). \end{equation*}

Our work is not done, just yet. Since we are concerned with the dynamics ‘near equilibrium’ we have to evaluate the Jacobian at the equilibrium. An example, in just a minute, should make this clear.

\begin{equation*} \left(\begin{array}{cc} y_{t_1}\\[8pt] z_{t+1} \end{array}\right)=\left.\left(\begin{array}{cc} \frac{\partial^{} {F}}{\partial {Y}^{}} & \frac{\partial^{} {F}}{\partial {Z}^{}}\\[8pt] \frac{\partial^{} {G}}{\partial {Y}^{}} & \frac{\partial^{} {G}}{\partial {Z}^{}} \end{array}\right)\right|_{(Y^*, Z^*)}\times\left(\begin{array}{cc} y_{t}\\[8pt] z_{t} \end{array}\right). \end{equation*}
Example..

Continuing with our current model, we will compute the Jacobian.

\begin{equation*} \begin{aligned} \frac{\partial^{} {F}}{\partial {Y}^{}} &= \frac{\partial^{} {}}{\partial {Y}^{}}\left(rY-pZY\right)= r-pZ\\ \frac{\partial^{} {F}}{\partial {Z}^{}} &= \frac{\partial^{} {}}{\partial {Z}^{}}\left(rY-pZY\right)= -pY\\ \frac{\partial^{} {G}}{\partial {Y}^{}} &= \frac{\partial^{} {}}{\partial {Y}^{}}\left(gYZ-mZ\right)= gZ\\ \frac{\partial^{} {G}}{\partial {Z}^{}} &= \frac{\partial^{} {}}{\partial {Z}^{}}\left(gYZ-mZ\right)= gY+m, \quad\text{this should be a $+m$ not a $-m$}.\end{aligned} \end{equation*}

So,

\begin{equation*} J(Y,Z)=\left(\begin{array}{cc} r-pZ\ & -pY\\[8pt] gZ & gY+m \end{array}\right), \quad\text{this should be a $+m$ not a $-m$}. \end{equation*}

So at the ‘zero’ equilibrium,

\begin{equation*} J(0,0)=\left(\begin{array}{cc} r\ & 0\\[8pt] 0 & m \end{array}\right), \quad\text{this should be a $+m$ not a $-m$}.. \end{equation*}

So at the nontrivial equilibrium,

\begin{equation*} J^*=J(Y^*,Z^*)=\left(\begin{array}{cc} r-p\left(\frac{r-1}{p}\right)\ & -p\left(\frac{1-m}{g}\right)\\[8pt] g\left(\frac{r-1}{p}\right) & g\left(\frac{1-m}{g}\right)+m \end{array}\right)= \left(\begin{array}{cc} 1 & -p\left(\frac{1-m}{g}\right)\\[8pt] g\left(\frac{r-1}{p}\right) & 1 \end{array}\right). \end{equation*}

Rather than compute and study the eigenvalues directly, we’ll take the scenic route to our solution. We will compute a few key properties of the matrix to understand the behavior of eigenvalues: trace, determinant, and discriminant. The trace of \(J^*\text{,}\)

\begin{equation*} \tau(J^*)=\operatorname{Tr}(J^*), \end{equation*}

is the sum of its diagonal entries, and in this case the diagonal sums to \(\tau=2\text{.}\) The determinant of \(J^*\) is,

\begin{equation*} \Delta(J^*)=\operatorname{det}(J^*)=1+(1-m)(r-1). \end{equation*}

The discriminant of \(J^*\text{,}\) is \(\operatorname{Disc}(J^*)=\tau^2-4\Delta\text{,}\) and

\begin{equation*} \operatorname{Disc}(J^*)=-4(1-m)(r-1). \end{equation*}

Eigenvalues are given by,

\begin{equation*} \lambda_\pm=\frac{\tau\pm\sqrt{\tau^2-4\Delta}}{2}, \end{equation*}

and they are real if \(\operatorname{Disc}(J^*)=\tau^2-4\Delta>0\) and imaginary otherwise. One thing is certain, we need

\begin{equation*} -1<\frac{\tau}{2}<1. \end{equation*}

This is because we want the roots to be between \((-1,1)\text{.}\) If we want roots in this interval, the vertex of the parabola better be there too. We’ll use the location of the vertex and distance to the roots to compute some stability criterion and build a stability diagram, next time.

We will briefly see why this is a worrisome model, and move on to more biologically interesting models. The trivial equilibrium \((0, 0)\) will be stable for stupidly small values of \(r, m\text{.}\) The nontrivial equilibrium is unstable and oscillatory, meaning that practically a species will crash into the number zero and go extinct.

Subsubsection 3.2.5 Nonlinear discrete-time models (2D): Stability criterion

Stability.

Consider the Jacobian matrix \(J^*\) from the linearization of a 2D model. For

\begin{equation*} J^*=\left(\begin{array}{cc}a & b\\c& d\end{array}\right), \end{equation*}

the associated characteristic polynomial is

\begin{equation*} C(\lambda)=\lambda^2-\tau \lambda+\Delta, \end{equation*}

where \(\tau\) is the trace and \(\Delta\) is the determinant. Solving this with the quadratic formula,

\begin{equation*} \lambda_\pm=\frac{\tau\pm\sqrt{\tau^2-4\Delta}}{2}. \end{equation*}

We’ll take a graphical approach to understanding the behavior of roots. First, notice that the vertex of the parabola is at \(\frac{\tau}{2},\) since the roots are symmetric about the vertex. We’ll assume the eigenvalues are distinct and that \(\lambda_+>\lambda_-\text{.}\)

For the leading eigenvalue, \(\lambda_+<1\text{,}\) we need

\begin{equation*} \begin{aligned} \lambda_+=\frac{\tau}{2}+\frac{\sqrt{\tau^2-4\Delta}}{2}&<1\\ \frac{\sqrt{\tau^2-4\Delta}}{2}&<1-\frac{\tau}{2}\\ \frac{\tau^2-4\Delta}{4}&<\left(1-\frac{\tau}{2}\right)^2\\ \frac{\tau^2}{4}-\Delta&<1-\tau+\frac{\tau^2}{4}\\ -\Delta&<1-\tau\Rightarrow -1+\tau<\Delta\end{aligned} \end{equation*}

If the determinant satisfies

\begin{equation*} -1+\tau<\Delta, \end{equation*}

then \(\lambda_+<1\text{.}\)

For the other eigenvalue, \(\lambda_->-1\text{,}\) we need

\begin{equation*} \begin{aligned} \lambda_-=\frac{\tau}{2}-\frac{\sqrt{\tau^2-4\Delta}}{2}&>-1\\ 1+\frac{\tau}{2} & > \frac{\sqrt{\tau^2-4\Delta}}{2}\\ \left(1+\frac{\tau}{2}\right)^2 & > \frac{\tau^2-4\Delta}{4}\\ 1+\tau+\frac{\tau^2}{4} & > \frac{\tau^2}{4}-\Delta\\ 1+\tau& > -\Delta\Rightarrow -1-\tau<\Delta\end{aligned} \end{equation*}

If the determinant satisfies

\begin{equation*} -1-\tau<\Delta, \end{equation*}

then \(\lambda_->-1\text{.}\)

We need both eigenvalue conditions satisfied simultaneously, so that

\begin{equation*} \Delta>|\tau|-1. \end{equation*}

The second line from each calculation is easily visualized in the following picture, roughly, the distance from the vertex to the root,

\begin{equation*} \frac{\sqrt{\tau^2-4\Delta}}{2} \end{equation*}

is less than the distance from the vertex to the enclosing endpoint (see Fig. [cross-reference to target(s) "fig::roots" missing]).

Lastly, we need complex roots which arise when \(\tau^2-4\Delta<0\text{,}\) to satisfy \(|\lambda_i|<1\text{.}\)

\begin{equation*} \begin{aligned} \lambda_\pm&=\frac{\tau}{2}\pm\frac{\sqrt{\tau^2-4\Delta}}{2}\\ &=\frac{\tau}{2}\pm i \frac{\sqrt{4\Delta-\tau^2}}{2}\\ |\lambda_\pm|&=\frac{\tau^2}{4}+ \Delta-\frac{\tau^2}{4}\\ |\lambda_\pm|&=\Delta\Rightarrow \Delta<1\end{aligned} \end{equation*}
Stability triangle.

We need

  • \(\displaystyle \operatorname{det}(J^*)<1\)

  • \(\displaystyle \operatorname{det}(J^*) > -1+| \operatorname{Tr}(J^*)|\)

  • Under \(\operatorname{det}(J^*)=\frac{\operatorname{Tr}(J^*)^2}{4}\) eigenvalues are real.

Subsubsection 3.2.6 Nicholson-Bailey and on to continuous tim

Nicholson-Bailey.

Stability of 2D, nonlinear, discrete-time models is summarized in Fig. [cross-reference to target(s) "fig::tri" missing], using properties (matrix trace, determinant, and discriminant) of the Jacobian matrix evaluated at equilibrium. An equilibrium is stable only when its trace and determinant map to regions within the triangle. Transient dynamics are governed by the sign of the discriminant, when the discriminant is negative, eigenvalue are complex and trajectories are oscillatory. Otherwise, eigenvalues are real and non-oscillatory.

Why do we want ‘small’ eigenvalues? We want small eigenvalues because we have linearized the system near an equilibrium point. We want perturbations to decay so that our solution returns (in some way, oscillatory or not) to equilibrium. Thus, we want the \((0,0)\) equilibrium of our perturbed coordinate system to be stable. If we write for some 2D nonlinear system \((X_t, Y_t)=(X^*+\hat x_t, Y^*+\hat y_t)\text{,}\) where \(\hat x_t\) and \(\hat y_t\) are our perturbations, we want both \(\hat x_t, \hat y_t\rightarrow0\) so that \((X_t, Y_t)\rightarrow(X^*, Y^*)\text{.}\) We keep using the phrase ‘want’ because biologically \((X_t\rightarrow0,Y_t\rightarrow0)\) and \((X_t\rightarrow\infty, Y_t\rightarrow\infty)\) are often uninteresting outcomes.

A quick example..

We built our first 2D model from a poor starting point, the discrete logistic model. We learned, intentionally and accidentally, that multiplying by negative numbers is a bad idea. We hope to develop models where the right hand sides can become small, but remain positive.

We will quickly look at the classic Nicholson-Bailey model. The model assumes that caterpillars are an engine for turning out parasitoids, each infected caterpillar produces a fixed amount of parasitoid larvae. The model of invertebrate host-parasitoid dynamics is of the form,

\begin{equation*} \begin{aligned} H_{t+1}&= (\text{host fecundity})\times(\text{host fraction surviving parasitism})\times(\text{host population size})\\ P_{t+1}&= (\text{parasitoid production per host})\times(\text{host fraction succumbing parasitism})\\ &\qquad\times(\text{host population size})\end{aligned} \end{equation*}

Host fecundity is just a constant, \(\lambda\text{.}\) The host fraction surviving parasitism is, for now, some arbitrary function of \(P_t\) and \(H_t\text{,}\) \(f(H_t, P_t)\text{.}\) The parasitoid production per host is another constant, \(c\text{.}\) Since all hosts are either parasitized or not, the fraction that succumbs to parasitism is \(1-f(H_t, P_t)\text{.}\)

The fraction that survives parasitism, \(f(H_t, P_t)\text{,}\) is an important component of the model. We will now build a model for this term using some basic logic, a few key assumptions, and probability. We define the number of host-parasitoid encounters, \(E\text{,}\) and assume it is proportional to the densities of \(H_t\) and \(P_t\text{,}\)

\begin{equation*} E=aH_tP_t. \end{equation*}

The parameter \(a\) is the constant of proportionality, and the number of encounters is bilinear in \(H_t\) and \(P_t\text{.}\) Caterpillars are a fixed size and only ’have space’ for a fixed number of parasitoids. Think of them as a parasitoid hotel, at some point all rooms are full and it doesn’t matter how many new travelers stop - they can try but there are no more rooms left.

On to our last piece of the model: the Poisson distribution. This distribution governs the number of events to occur in some time interval, when events occur at some fixed rate, \(\mu\text{.}\) The probability of \(r\) events is

\begin{equation*} Q(r)=\frac{e^{-\mu}\mu^r}{r!}. \end{equation*}

The average number of attacks per host per generation is

\begin{equation*} \mu=\frac{E}{H_t}=\frac{aH_tP_t}{H_t}=aP_t. \end{equation*}

According to our second assumption we only care whether a host is parasitized or not (i.e., whether \(r=0\) or \(r>0\)). Thus, we want

\begin{equation*} f(N_t, P_t)=Q(0)=\frac{e^{-aP_t}(aP_t)^0}{0!}=e^{-aP_t}. \end{equation*}

Plugging in \(f(H_t, P_t)\text{,}\)

\begin{equation*} \begin{aligned} H_{t+1}&= \lambda e^{-a P_t}H_t\\ P_{t+1}&=c (1-e^{-a P_t})H_t.\end{aligned} \end{equation*}

A few special cases suggest that when \(P_t=0\text{,}\) \(H_t\) grows geometrically, but when \(P_t\) is ‘large’ few hosts escape parasitism. We might wish to limit host growth in the absence of parasitism, which could be accomplished by defining \(\lambda=\lambda(H_t)\) as some reasonable decreasing function of host population density. Actually evaluating the stability conditions for this and other models is occasionally quite enlightening and can give a nice interpretation of the relationships between certain terms (rates and interactions) in our model.
