The surface \(S\) is not closed, and so is not the boundary of a solid, so we cannot apply the divergence theorem directly. But we can easily come up with a solid whose boundary contains \(S\text{.}\) Let

\begin{equation*} V=\Set{(x,y,z)}{ x^2 + y^2 + z^2 \le 4,\ 0\le z\le 1} \end{equation*}

The boundary \(\partial V\) of \(V\) consists of three parts â€” \(S\text{,}\) the bottom disk

\begin{equation*} D_b = \Set{(x,y,z)}{x^2 + y^2 \le 4,\ z=0} \end{equation*}

and the top disk

\begin{equation*} D_t = \Set{(x,y,z)}{x^2 + y^2 \le 3,\ z=1} \end{equation*}

The outward normal to \(D_t\) is \(\hk\) and the outward normal to \(D_b\) is \(-\hk\text{.}\) So the divergence theorem gives

\begin{align*} \tripInt_V\vnabla\cdot\vF\,\dee{V} &=\dblInt_{\partial V} \vF\cdot\hn\,\dee{S}\\ &=\dblInt_S \vF\cdot\hn\,\dee{S} +\dblInt_{D_t} \vF\cdot\hk\,\dee{S} +\dblInt_{D_b} \vF\cdot(-\hk)\,\dee{S} \end{align*}

On \(D_b\text{,}\) \(z=0\) so that \(\vF\cdot(-\hk) = -(0^2-1)=1\) and on \(D_t\text{,}\) \(z=1\) so that \(\vF\cdot\hk = 1^2-1=0\text{.}\) So

\begin{gather*} \dblInt_S \vF\cdot\hn\,\dee{S} =\tripInt_V\overbrace{4z}^{\vnabla\cdot\vF}\,\dee{V} -\dblInt_{D_b} \dee{S} \end{gather*}

The constant \(z\) cross-section of \(V\) is a disk of radius \(\sqrt{4-z^2}\) and hence of area \(\pi(4-z^2)\) and \(D_b\) is a disk of radius \(2\) and hence of area \(4\pi\text{.}\) So

\begin{align*} \dblInt_S \vF\cdot\hn\,\dee{S} &=\int_0^1 (4z)\ \pi(4-z^2) \ \dee{z} -4\pi =4\pi\Big[2z^2 - \frac{z^4}{4}\Big]_0^1 -4\pi =3\pi \end{align*}
