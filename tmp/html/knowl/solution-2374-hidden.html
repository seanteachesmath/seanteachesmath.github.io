The surface

\begin{align*} S&=\Set{(x,y,z)}{-1\le x\le 1,\ -1\le y\le 1,\ z\ge 0,\ z = (1-x^2)(1-y^2)}\\ &=\Set{(x,y,z)}{-1\le x\le 1,\ -1\le y\le 1,\ \ z = (1-x^2)(1-y^2)} \end{align*}

Note that when \(x=1\) or \(x=-1\) or \(y=1\) or \(y=-1\text{,}\) we have \(z= (1-x^2)(1-y^2)=0\text{.}\) So the boundary of \(S\text{,}\) call it \(C\text{,}\) is the boundary of the square \(-1\le x,y\le 1\text{,}\) \(z=0\text{,}\) oriented counterclockwise. Here is a sketch of \(C\text{.}\)

Apply Stokes' theorem. Observing that \(z=0\) on \(C\) so that \(\vF = -y\,\hi +x^3\,\hj\text{,}\)

\begin{align*} &\dblInt_S\vnabla\times\vF\cdot\hn\,\dee{S} =\oint_C\vF\cdot \dee{\vr} =\oint_C[-y\,\hi+x^3\hj]\cdot \dee{\vr}\\ &\hskip0.5in=\underbrace{\int_{-1}^1 -(-1)\,\dee{x}}_{y=-1{\rm\ side}} +\underbrace{\int_{-1}^1 (1)^3\,\dee{y}}_{x=1{\rm\ side}} + \underbrace{\int^{-1}_1 -(1)\,\dee{x}}_{y=1{\rm\ side}} + \underbrace{\int^{-1}_1 (-1)^3\,\dee{y}}_{x=-1{\rm\ side}}\\ &\hskip0.5in=8 \end{align*}
