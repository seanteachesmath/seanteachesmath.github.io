Solution 14.1.1.7.1.

(a) In the sketch below, the point \((x,y,z)\) deviates from the centre \((2,2,4)\) by \(\sin\theta\) units in the \(\hk\) direction, and by \(\cos\theta\) units in the \(\sqrt \frac1{\sqrt 2}(\hi+\hj)\) direction. So, \((x,y,z)=(2+\frac1{\sqrt 2}\cos\theta , 2+\frac1{\sqrt 2}\cos\theta , 4+\sin\theta)\text{.}\)

So, we can parametrize the circle as \((x,y,z)=(2+\frac1{\sqrt 2}\cos\theta , 2+\frac1{\sqrt 2}\cos\theta , 4+\sin\theta)\text{,}\) with \(0 \le \theta \le 2\pi\text{.}\)

Remark: it's easy to check that this equation satisfies the two properties we desire. Since the \(x\)- and \(y\) coordinates match, it's in the plane \(x=y\text{.}\) To check that it's a circle centred at \((2,2,4)\text{,}\) we note the distance from \((x,y,z)\) to \((2,2,4)\) is:

\begin{align*} d&=\sqrt{(x-2)^2+(y-2)^2+(z-4)^2}\\ &=\sqrt{\left(\frac1{\sqrt 2}\cos\theta\right)^2+\left(\frac1{\sqrt 2}\cos\theta\right)^2+\left(\sin\theta\right)^2}\\ &=\sqrt{\frac12\cos^2\theta+\frac12\cos^2\theta+\sin^2\theta}=\sqrt{\cos^2\theta+\sin^2\theta}\\ &=1 \end{align*}

So, our points all have distance one from the same point â€” that is, they lie on a circle of radius 1.

(b) Consider a point \((x,y,z)=(2+\frac1{\sqrt 2}\cos\theta , 2+\frac1{\sqrt 2}\cos\theta , 4+\sin\theta)\text{,}\) rotating \(\phi\) radians about the line \(x=y=4\text{.}\)

The new position of the point has the same height, \(z=4+\sin\theta\text{.}\) Its distance from the line \(x=y=4\) is also preserved:

\begin{align*} R&=\sqrt{(x-4)^2+(y-4)^2+(z-z)^2}\\ &=\sqrt{(\frac1{\sqrt 2}\cos\theta-2)^2+(\frac1{\sqrt 2}\cos\theta-2)^2+0)}\\ &=\cos\theta-2\sqrt2 \end{align*}

The circle traced out by a point \((x,y,z)=(2+\frac1{\sqrt2}\cos\theta,2+\frac{1}{\sqrt 2} \cos \theta,4+\sin\theta)\) on the circle is centred at \((4,4,z)\) with radius \(\sqrt2(4-x)\text{,}\) so it has equation \(x=4+\sqrt2(2-\sqrt2\cos\theta)\cos\phi\text{,}\) \(y=4+\sqrt2(2-\sqrt2\cos\theta)\sin\phi\text{,}\) \(z=4\sin\theta\text{.}\)
