First, we will define differential forms. To try and keep things as simple and concrete as possible, we'll only define 1  differential forms on \(\bbbr^3\) — all of our functions will be defined on \(\bbbr^3\text{.}\) Very roughly speaking, a \(k\)-form is what you write after the integral sign of an integral over a \(k\) dimensional object. Here \(k\) is one of \(0\text{,}\) \(1\text{,}\) \(2\text{,}\) \(3\text{.}\) As a example, a \(1\)-form is an expression of the form \(F_1(x,y,z)\,\dee{x} + F_2(x,y,z)\,\dee{y} + F_3(x,y,z)\,\dee{z}\text{.}\) For \(k=0\text{,}\) think of a point as a zero dimensional object and think of evaluating a function at a point as “integrating the function over the point”.
