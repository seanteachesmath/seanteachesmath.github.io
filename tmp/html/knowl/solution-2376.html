Solution 15.4.3.11.1.

Stokes' theorem, which is Theorem 15.4.1, says that \(\oint_C\vF\cdot \dee{\vr} =\dblInt_S\nabla\times\vF\cdot\hn\,\dee{S}\) for any surface \(S\) whose boundary is \(C\text{.}\) For the given vector field

\begin{align*} \nabla\times\vF(x,y,z) &=\det\left[\begin{matrix} \hi &\hj &\hk\\ \tfrac{\partial }{\partial x} & \tfrac{\partial }{\partial y} & \tfrac{\partial }{\partial z}\\ -z & x & y \end{matrix} \right]\\ &=\hi-\hj+\hk \end{align*}

Choose

\begin{align*} S &= \Set{(x,y,z)}{z=y,\ \tfrac{x^2}{4}+\tfrac{y^2}{2}+\tfrac{z^2}{2}\le 1}\\ &= \Set{(x,y,z)}{z=y,\ \tfrac{x^2}{4}+y^2\le 1} \end{align*}

to be the part of the plane \(z=y\) bounded by the ellipsoid.

As \(S\) is part of the plane \(z=f(x,y)=y\text{,}\) (14.3.2), gives that

\begin{align*} \hn\,\dee{S} &=\pm \big(-f_x\,,\,-f_y\,,\, 1\big)\dee{x}\dee{y}\\ &=\pm (0\,,\,-1\,,\, 1)\dee{x}\dee{y} \end{align*}

As \(C\) has the standard orientation (counter-clockwise when viewed from high on the \(z\)-axis), we want \(\hn\) to have a positive \(z\)-component. So \(\hn\,\dee{S}= (0\,,\,-1\,,\, 1)\dee{x}\dee{y} \text{.}\) From the second form of \(S\) given above, we see that as \((x,y,z)\) runs over \(S\text{,}\) \((x,y)\) runs over

\begin{equation*} D=\Set{(x,y)}{\tfrac{x^2}{4}+y^2\le 1} \end{equation*}

Consequently, Stokes' theorem gives that

\begin{align*} \oint_C\vF\cdot \dee{\vr} &=\dblInt_D\overbrace{(1,-1,1)}^{\nabla\times\vF}\cdot \overbrace{(0\,,\,-1\,,\, 1)\dee{x}\dee{y} }^{\hn\,\dee{S}}\\ &=2\dblInt_D \dee{x}\dee{y} =2\,\text{Area}(D) \end{align*}

The ellipse \(D\text{,}\) that is \(\tfrac{x^2}{4}+y^2\le 1\text{,}\) has semi-axes \(a=2\) and \(b=1\) and hence area \(\pi ab=2\pi\text{.}\) Finally

\begin{equation*} \oint_C\vF\cdot \dee{\vr} =2\,\text{Area}(D) =4\pi \end{equation*}
