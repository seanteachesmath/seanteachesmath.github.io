(c) The vector field \(\vF\) looks too complicated for a direct evaluation of the line integral. So, in preparation for an application of Stokes' theorem, we compute

\begin{align*} \vnabla\times\vF&=\det\left[\begin{matrix}\hi&\hj&\hk\\ \frac{\partial }{\partial x}& \frac{\partial }{\partial y}& \frac{\partial }{\partial z}\\ y + \sin(x^2) & z - 3x + \ln(1 + y^2) & y + e^{z^2}\end{matrix}\right]\\ &= -4\,\hk \end{align*}

So, by Stokes' theorem (Theorem 15.4.1),

\begin{align*} &\int_C\vF\cdot\dee{\vr} =\dblInt_S\vnabla\times\vF\cdot\hn\,\dee{S}\\ &\hskip0.1in= \int_0^{\pi/2}\hskip-5pt\dee{\varphi} \int_0^{\pi/2}\hskip-5pt\dee{\theta}\ \big(-4\hk\big)\cdot \big(\cos\theta\sin\varphi\ \hi +\sin\theta\sin\varphi\ \hj +\cos\varphi\ \hk\big)\ 4\sin\varphi\\ &\hskip0.1in=-16 \int_0^{\pi/2}\dee{\varphi}\int_0^{\pi/2}\dee{\theta}\ \cos\varphi\sin\varphi =16\ \frac{\pi}{2}\ \frac{\cos^2\varphi}{2}\bigg|_0^{\pi/2}\\ &\hskip0.1in=-4\pi \end{align*}
