  1. If the integral \(\int_t^b f(x)\dee{x}\) exists for all \(a \lt t \lt b\text{,}\) then
    \begin{equation*} \int_a^b f(x)\dee{x}=\lim_{t\rightarrow a+}\int_t^b f(x)\dee{x} \end{equation*}
    when the limit exists (and is finite).
  2. If the integral \(\int_a^T f(x)\dee{x}\) exists for all \(a \lt T \lt b\text{,}\) then
    \begin{equation*} \int_a^b f(x)\dee{x}=\lim_{T\rightarrow b-}\int_a^T f(x)\dee{x} \end{equation*}
    when the limit exists (and is finite).
  3. Let \(a \lt c \lt b\text{.}\) If the integrals \(\int_a^T f(x)\dee{x}\) and \(\int_t^b f(x)\dee{x}\) exist for all \(a \lt T \lt c\) and \(c \lt t \lt b\text{,}\) then
    \begin{equation*} \int_a^b f(x)\dee{x}=\lim_{T\rightarrow c-}\int_a^T f(x)\dee{x} +\lim_{t\rightarrow c+}\int_t^b f(x)\dee{x} \end{equation*}
    when both limit exist (and are finite).

When the limit(s) exist, the integral is said to be convergent. Otherwise it is said to be divergent.
