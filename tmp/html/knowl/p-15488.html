As a warm up example, and also a check that our formulae make sense, we'll find the curvature \(\ka\text{,}\) radius of curvature, \(\rho\text{,}\) unit tangent vector, \(\hat\vT\text{,}\) unit normal vector, \(\hat\vN\text{,}\) and centre of curvature of the parametrized curve

\begin{equation*} \vr(t) = a\cos t\,\hi + a\sin t\,\hj \end{equation*}

with the constant \(a \gt 0\text{.}\) This is, of course, the circle of radius \(a\) centred on the origin. As

\begin{equation*} \vv(t)=\diff{\vr}{t}(t)= -a\sin t\,\hi + a\cos t\,\hj \implies \diff{s}{t}(t) = |\vv(t)| = a \end{equation*}

we have that the unit tangent vector

\begin{equation*} \vT(t) = \frac{\vv(t)}{|\vv(t)|} = -\sin t\,\hi + \cos t\,\hj \end{equation*}

Note, as a check, that this is indeed a vector of length one and is perpendicular to the radius vector (as expected â€” the curve is a circle). As

\begin{equation*} \diff{\hat\vT}{t}(t)= -\cos t\,\hi -\sin t\,\hj \end{equation*}

we have that

\begin{align*} \hat\vN(t) &= \frac{\diff{\hat\vT}{t}(t)}{\big|\diff{\hat\vT}{t}(t)\big|} = -\cos t\,\hi -\sin t\,\hj\\ \ka(t) &= \frac{\big|\diff{\hat\vT}{t}(t)\big|}{\diff{s}{t}(t)} =\frac{1}{a}\\ \rho(t)&=\frac{1}{\ka(t)} =a \end{align*}
