Solution 12.4.1.2.1.

In this equation, \(s\) stands for arclength.

When we take a very small interval from \(t\) to \(t+h\text{,}\) the change in arclength \(s(t+h)-s(t)\) is approximately \(|\vr(t+h)-\vr(t)|\text{,}\) because our curve is approximated by a straight line. So, \(\frac{s(t+h)-s(t)}{h} \approx \frac{|\vr(t+h)-\vr(t)|}{h}\text{,}\) leading to \(\diff{s}{t}=|\diff{\vr}{t}|=|\vv(t)|\text{.}\)

The magnitude of velocity is speed; in this text we generally call this \(v\text{.}\) That is, \(v=|\vv(t)|\text{.}\) This leads to the potentially confusing (but standard) convention that \(s\) stands for arclength, while \(v\) stands for speed.
