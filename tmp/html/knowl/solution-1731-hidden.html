Thinking a little way ahead, to find the critical points we will need the gradient and to apply the second derivative test of Theorem 10.9.16 we will need all second order partial derivatives. So we need all partial derivatives of order up to two. Here they are.

\begin{alignat*}{3} f&=2x^3 - 6xy + y^2 +4y\\ f_x&=6x^2-6y & f_{xx}&=12x \qquad & f_{xy}&= -6\\ f_y&=-6x+2y+4 \qquad & f_{yy}&=2\qquad & f_{yx}&= -6 \end{alignat*}

(Of course, \(f_{xy}\) and \(f_{yx}\) have to be the same. It is still useful to compute both, as a way to catch some mechanical errors.)

We have already found, in Example 10.9.7, that the critical points are \((1,1),\ (2,4)\text{.}\) The classification is

\(\Atop{\text{critical}}{\text{point}}\) \(f_{xx}f_{yy}-f_{xy}^2\) \(f_{xx}\) type
\((1,1)\) \(12\times 2-(-6)^2 \lt 0\) saddle point
\((2,4)\) \(24\times 2-(-6)^2 \gt 0\) 24 local min

We were able to leave the \(f_{xx}\) entry in the top row blank, because

  • we knew that \(f_{xx}(1,1) f_{yy}(1,1)-f_{xy}^2(1,1) \lt 0\text{,}\) and
  • we knew, from Theorem 10.9.16, that \(f_{xx}(1,1) f_{yy}(1,1)-f_{xy}^2(1,1) \lt 0\text{,}\) by itself, was enough to ensure that \((1,1)\) was a saddle point.
