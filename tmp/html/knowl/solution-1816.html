Solution 11.1.14.1.

This integral represents the volume of a simple geometric figure and so can be evaluated without using any calculus at all. The domain of integration is

\begin{equation*} \cR=\Set{(x,y)}{0\le y\le 2,\ 0\le x\le a} \end{equation*}

and the integrand is \(f(x,y) = \sqrt{a^2-x^2}\text{,}\) so the integral represents the volume between the \(xy\)-plane and the surface \(z=\sqrt{a^2-x^2}\text{,}\) with \((x,y)\) running over \(\cR\text{.}\) We can rewrite the equation of the surface as \(x^2+z^2=a^2\text{,}\) which, as in Example 11.1.13, we recognize as the equation of a cylinder of radius \(a\) centred on the \(y\)-axis. We want the volume of the part of this cylinder that lies above \(\cR\text{.}\) It is sketched in the figure below.

The constant \(y\) cross-sections of this volume are quarter circles of radius \(a\) and hence of area \(\frac{1}{4}\pi a^2\text{.}\) The inside integral, \(\int_0^a \sqrt{a^2-x^2}\ \dee{x}\text{,}\) is exactly this area. So, as \(y\) runs from \(0\) to \(2\text{,}\)

\begin{equation*} \int_0^2\int_0^a \sqrt{a^2-x^2}\ \dee{x}\,\dee{y} =\frac{1}{4}\pi a^2\times 2 =\frac{\pi a^2}{2} \end{equation*}
