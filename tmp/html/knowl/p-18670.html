(b) False. The conditions that (1) \(\vnabla\times\vF\) = 0 and (2) the domain of \(\vF\) is simply-connected, are sufficient, but not necessary, to imply that \(\vF\) is conservative. For example the vector field \(\vF=\vZero\text{,}\) with any domain at all, is conservative with potential \(0\text{.}\) Another example (which does not depend on choosing a domain that is smaller than the largest possible domain) is \(\vF=\vnabla\frac{1}{x^2+y^2}\) with domain \(\Set{(x,y,z)}{ (x,y)\ne (0,0)}\text{.}\) That is, the domain is \(\bbbr^3\) with the \(z\)-axis removed.
