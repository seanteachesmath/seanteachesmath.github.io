(b) Given \(\vr(s)\text{,}\) then, by Lemma 9.6.12.c, \(\hat\vT(s) = \vr'(s)\) is a unit tangent to the curve at \(\vr(s)\) and

\begin{equation*} \diff{\hat\vT}{s} = \diff{\hat\vT}{\phi} \diff{\phi}{s} \tag{$*$} \end{equation*}

Now up to a sign \(\diff{\phi}{s}\) is \(\ka\text{,}\) and just because \(\phi=\measuredangle(\hi,\hat\vT)\text{,}\) with \(\hat\vT\) a unit vector,

\begin{equation*} \begin{split} \hat\vT&=\cos\phi\,\hi + \sin\phi\,\hj \\ \implies \diff{\hat\vT}{\phi}&= -\sin\phi\,\hi + \cos\phi\,\hj \end{split} \tag{$**$} \end{equation*}

So \(\diff{\hat\vT}{\phi}\) is a unit vector that is perpendicular 7  to \(\hat\vT\text{,}\) and hence to the curve at \(\vr(s)\text{,}\) and

\begin{equation*} \diff{\hat\vT}{s}(s) = \ka(s)\,\hat\vN(s) \tag{$\dagger$} \end{equation*}

with \(\hat\vN(s)\) a unit normal vector to the curve at \(\vr(s)\text{.}\) In fact, \(\hat\vN(s)\) is the unit normal vector to the curve at \(\vr(s)\) that points toward the centre of curvature.
