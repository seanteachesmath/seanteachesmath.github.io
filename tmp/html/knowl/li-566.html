Now we could again “pretend” that we have solved the equation for the ellipse for \(y=f(x)\) near \((x_0,y_0)\text{,}\) but lets not do that. Instead (as we did just before this example) just remember that when we differentiate \(y\) is really a function of \(x\text{.}\) So starting from
\begin{align*} 3x^2 + 5y^2 &=7 &\text{differentiating gives}\\ 6x + 5\cdot 2y \cdot y' &= 0 \end{align*}
We can then solve this for \(y'\text{:}\)
\begin{align*} y' &= -\frac{3x}{5y} \end{align*}
where \(y'\) and \(y\) are both functions of \(x\text{.}\)
