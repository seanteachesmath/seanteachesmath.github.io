Solution 16.2.6.1.

(a) False. Changing the orientation of a surface does not change \(\dee{S}\) at all. (It changes \(\hn\dee{S}\) by a factor of \((-1)\text{.}\)) So

\begin{equation*} \dblInt_S f\, \dee{S} = +\dblInt_{-S} f\,\dee{S} \end{equation*}

which is not \(-\dblInt_{-S} f\,\dee{S}\text{,}\) unless the integral is zero.

(b) False. For every vector field with two continuous partial derivatives, \(\vnabla\cdot(\vnabla\times\vF)=0\) (see Theorem 15.1.7.a), so the divergence theorem gives

\begin{align*} \dblInt_S (\vnabla\times\vF) \cdot \hn\,\dee{S} &=\tripInt_V \vnabla\cdot(\vnabla\times\vF)\,\dee{V} =0 \end{align*}

whether or not \(\vF\) is conservative.

(c) True. Define the vector field \(\vF=f\,\hi\text{.}\) Then, by Stokes' theorem,

\begin{gather*} \int_C f\,\dee{x} =\int_C \vF\cdot\dee{\vr} =\dblInt_S\vnabla\times\vF\cdot\hn\,\dee{S} =\dblInt_S \left(\frac{\partial f}{\partial z}\hj - \frac{\partial f}{\partial y}\hk\right)\cdot\hn\,\dee{S} \end{gather*}

(d) True. The left hand side, \((\vnabla f ) \times (\vnabla f )\text{,}\) is zero because \((\vnabla f )\) is parallel to itself and the right hand side \(\vnabla \times (\vnabla f )\) is zero by Theorem 15.1.7.b (the screening test for conservative fields).

(e) True. The curve \(\vr(t) = \big(2 \,,\, 0\,,\, 1\big) +t^3\big(4 \,,\, -1 \,,\, -2\big)\) is a straight line. Straight lines have curvature \(0\text{.}\)

(f) True. In general \(|\vr'(t)|=\diff{s}{t}\text{.}\) Under arc length parametrization \(t=s\) so that \(\diff{s}{t}=1\text{.}\)

(g) True. If \(\vF\) is a constant vector fleld, then, by the divergence thoerem,

\begin{equation*} \dblInt_S \vF \cdot \hn\,\dee{S} =\tripInt_V \vnabla\cdot\vF\,\dee{V} =\tripInt_V 0\,\dee{V} =0 \end{equation*}

(h) False. The statement \(\vnabla\times\vF=(x,y,z)\) means that \(\vF\) is a vector potential for the vector field \(\vG=(x,y,z)\text{.}\) But \(\vG\) fails the screening test \(\vnabla\cdot\vG=0\) for vector potentials.
