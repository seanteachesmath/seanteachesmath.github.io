(3),(4): False. Here is a counterxample that shows that both (3) and (4) are false. Choose a coordinate system so that \(Q\) is at the origin and choose \(\vF=\vG\text{.}\) By Stokes' theorem

\begin{equation*} \int_S \vG\cdot\dee{\vr} = \int_T \vG\cdot\dee{\vr} = 0 \end{equation*}

because \(\vnabla\times\vG=\vZero\) everywhere inside \(S\text{,}\) including at \(P\text{.}\) So now both parts (3) and (4) reduce to the claim that \(\int_U \vG\cdot\dee{\vr} = \int_R \vG\cdot\dee{\vr}\text{.}\)

We saw, in Example 15.3.8, that \(\int_R \vG\cdot\dee{\vr} = 2\pi\text{.}\)

To finish off the counterexample, we'll now show that \(\int_U \vG\cdot\dee{\vr} = -2\pi\text{.}\) Let \(\cR_2\) be the region between \(U\) and \(R\text{.}\) It is the shaded region in the figure on the right above. Note that \(\vnabla\times\vG=\vZero\) on all of \(\cR_2\text{.}\) including at \(P\text{.}\) The boundary of \(\cR_2\) is \(-U-R\text{,}\) meaning that the boundary consists of two parts, with one part being \(-U\) and the other part being \(-R\text{.}\) So, by Stokes' theorem

\begin{equation*} -\int_U \vG\cdot\dee{\vr} - \int_R \vG\cdot\dee{\vr} =\int_{\partial \cR_2} \vG\cdot\dee{\vr} =\dblInt_{\cR_2} \vnabla\times\vG\cdot\hk\, \dee{S} =0 \end{equation*}

and \(\int_U \vG\cdot\dee{\vr} =-\int_R \vG\cdot\dee{\vr} = -2\pi\)
