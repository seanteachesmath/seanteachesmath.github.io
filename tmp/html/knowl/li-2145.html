But all is not lost. We can rewrite the factor \(x^2\) in terms of the variable \(u\text{.}\) We know that \(u=1+x^2\text{,}\) so this means \(x^2 = u-1\text{.}\) Substituting this into our integral gives
\begin{align*} \int \sqrt{1+x^2}\cdot x^3\dee{x} &= \int \frac{1}{2} \sqrt{u}\cdot x^2 \dee{u}\\ &= \int \frac{1}{2} \sqrt{u}\cdot (u-1) \dee{u}\\ &= \frac{1}{2} \int \left(u^{3/2}-u^{1/2}\right) \dee{u}\\ &= \frac{1}{2} \left( \frac{2}{5} u^{5/2} - \frac{2}{3}u^{3/2} \right)\bigg|_{u=x^2+1} +C\\ &= \left(\frac{1}{5} u^{5/2} - \frac{1}{3}u^{3/2}\right) \bigg|_{u=x^2+1} +C\\ &= \frac{1}{5}(x^2+1)^{5/2} - \frac{1}{3} (x^2+1)^{3/2} +C. \end{align*}
