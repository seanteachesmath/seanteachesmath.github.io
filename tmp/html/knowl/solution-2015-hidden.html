We consider the situation geometrically. If we plot \(\vR\) in space (of the relevant dimension), regardless of its parametrization, the derivative at a point will give a vector tangent to \(\vR\text{,}\) in the direction the curve moves when the parameter is increasing. Since \(\va(t_0)\) and \(\vb(s_0)\) describe the same spot on the curve, \(\va'(t_0)\) and \(\vb'(s_0)\) will be parallel 1  — they're both tangent to the same piece of curve. Furthermore, as \(t\) increases, so does \(s\text{,}\) so the direction of increasing \(t\) is the same as the direction of increasing \(s\text{.}\) Therefore, A. holds.

Now we consider the magnitudes of the vectors, to rule out E. Recall \(|\va'(t)|\) is the speed at which the curve changes relative to \(t\text{;}\) this could be any (nonnegative) number. By the same token, \(|\vb'(s)|=1\text{.}\) So, \(\vb'(s_0)\) is a unit vector, while \(\va'(t_0)\) may or may not be. Then the two vectors are not necessarily equal (although they could be).

So, the best answer is A.
