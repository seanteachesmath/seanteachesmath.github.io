The arclength of \(\vr(t)\) traced out by an interval of \(t\) of length \(\theta\) is \(3\theta\text{.}\) That is, \(s=3t\text{.}\) Our reparametrization of the circle in terms of arclength is \(\vR(s)=(3\sin(s/3),3\cos(s/3))\text{.}\)

We can calculate the vectors tangent to the circle, then normalize them (i.e. make them length one) to find \(\hT\text{.}\)

\begin{align*} \vv(t)&=\vr'(t)=(3\cos t, -3\sin t) \\ \hT(t)&=\frac{\vr'(t)}{|\vr'(t)|}=\frac{(3\cos t, -3\sin t)}{3}=(\cos t, -\sin t)\\ \hT(s)&=\vR'(s)=(\cos(s/3),-\sin(s/3)) \end{align*}

Note \(\vR'(s)\text{,}\) because it's parametrized in terms of arclength, has derivative vectors of length one. So, we don't need to normalize them (although if we did, it wouldn't change anything).

Note also that we can check out answers using Question 12.3.1.1. In that question, we found \(\hT\) was \(\hi\) when \(t=s=0\text{;}\) this fits with the vectors we just found.

As in Question 12.3.1.1, \(\ka=\frac13\text{.}\) So, using Theorem 12.3.3 Part (b):

\begin{align*} \diff{\hT}{s}(s) &= \ka(s)\,\hN(s)\\ \left(-\frac13\sin(s/3) , -\frac13\cos(s/3) \right)&=\frac13\hN(s)\\ \left(-\sin(s/3) , -\cos(s/3) \right)&=\hN(s) \end{align*}

Remember \(s=3t\text{.}\) Using Theorem 12.3.3 Part (c):

\begin{align*} \diff{\hT}{t} &= \ka \diff{s}{t} \hN(t)\\ (-\sin t ,-\cos t) &= \frac13 (3) \hN(t)\\ (-\sin t ,-\cos t) &= \hN(t) \end{align*}
