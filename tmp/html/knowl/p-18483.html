The behaviour of Example 15.6.1 is typical of \(\cV\)'s that are symmetric matrices, i.e. that obey 2  \(\cV_{i,j}=\cV_{j,i}\) for all \(i,j\text{.}\) Any \(d\times d\) symmetric matrix 3  (with real entries)

  • has \(d\) real eigenvalues
  • has \(d\) mutually orthogonal real unit eigenvectors.

Denote by \(\la_k,\ 1\le k\le d\text{,}\) the eigenvalues of \(\cV\) and choose \(d\) mutually perpendicular real unit vectors, \(\ha{k}\text{,}\) that obey \(\cV\ha{k}=\la_k\ha{k}\) for all \(1\le k\le d\text{.}\) Then

\begin{equation*} \vb^{(k)}(t)=e^{\la_k (t-t_0)}\ \ha{k} \end{equation*}

obeys

\begin{equation*} \diff{\vb^{(k)}}{t}(t)=\la_ke^{\la_k (t-t_0)}\ \ha{k}=e^{\la_k (t-t_0)}\ \cV\ha{k} =\cV\vb^{(k)}(t)\quad\text{and}\quad \vb^{(k)}(t_0)= \ha{k} \end{equation*}

So \(\vb^{(k)}(t)=e^{\la_k (t-t_0)}\ \ha{k}\) satisfies (IVP) for all \(t\) and \(1\le k\le d\text{.}\)
