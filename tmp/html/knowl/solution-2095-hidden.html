(a) Since \(-1\le \sin(4\theta)\le 1\text{,}\) the coordinate \(r=2+\sin(4\theta)\) oscillates between \(r=1\) and \(r=3\) as \(\theta\) runs from \(0\) to \(2\pi\text{.}\) The maximum value \(r=3\) is achieved when \(\sin(4\theta)=1\text{,}\) i.e when \(4\theta=\frac{\pi}{2} +2n\pi\text{,}\) i.e. when \(\theta=\frac{\pi}{8}+\frac{n\pi}{2}\text{.}\) That matches figure (E).

(b) Since \(-1\le \sin(4\theta)\le 1\text{,}\) the coordinate \(r=1+2\sin(4\theta)\) takes its maximum value \(r=3\) when \(\sin(4\theta)=1\text{,}\) i.e. when \(\theta=\frac{\pi}{8}+\frac{n\pi}{2}\text{,}\) just as the case with \((a)\text{.}\) But now \(r\) can also take the value \(0\text{.}\) That matches figure (B).

(c) \(r=1\) is completely indepedent of \(\theta\text{.}\) All points on the curve \(r=1\) are a distance \(1\) from the origin. That is, \(r=1\) is the circle of radius \(1\) centred on the origin. That's figure (F).

(d) In this case, \(\theta\) is subject to the restriction \(-\frac{\pi}{2}\le\theta\le\frac{\pi}{2}\text{,}\) like figure (C). Figure (C) looks like a circle. We can verify that \(r=2\cos(\theta)\) is indeed a circle by converting to Cartesian coordinates. We can convert the right hand side to exactly \(2x=2r\cos(\theta)\) by multiplying the whole equation by \(r\text{.}\)

\begin{align*} r=2\cos(\theta) &\iff r^2 =2r\cos(\theta) \iff x^2+y^2=2x\\ &\iff (x-1)^2+y^2=1 \end{align*}

So \(r=2\cos(\theta)\) is the circle of radius \(1\) centred on \(x=1\text{,}\) \(y=0\text{,}\) which indeed matches figure (C).

(e) When \(\theta=0\text{,}\) \(r=e^{\theta/10}+e^{-\theta/10}=2\text{.}\) As

\begin{equation*} \diff{}{\theta}\big(e^{\theta/10}+e^{-\theta/10}\big) =\frac{1}{10}\big(e^{\theta/10}-e^{-\theta/10}\big) \gt 0\qquad\text{for all }\theta \gt 0 \end{equation*}

\(r=e^{\theta/10}+e^{-\theta/10}\) increases as \(\theta\) increases for all \(\theta\ge 0\text{.}\) Furthermore the rate of increase gets bigger and bigger as \(\theta\) gets bigger and bigger. So \(r\) starts at \(r=2\) when \(\theta=0\) and increases faster and faster as \(\theta\) increases. That matches figure (A).

(f) When \(\theta=0\text{,}\) \(r=\theta=0\text{.}\) As

\begin{equation*} \diff{}{\theta} \theta =1 \qquad\text{for all }\theta \end{equation*}

\(r=\theta\) increases as \(\theta\) increases for all \(\theta\ge 0\text{.}\) Furthermore the rate of increase is independent of \(\theta\text{.}\) So \(r\) starts at \(r=0\) when \(\theta=0\) and increases at a constant rate as \(\theta\) increases. That matches figure (D).
