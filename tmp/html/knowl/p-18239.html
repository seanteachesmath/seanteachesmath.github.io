(b) Let \(S\) be the surface \(z=f(x,y)\) with \(f(x,y)=y^2\) and \(x^2+y^2\le 1\text{.}\) Since \(C\) is oriented counter clockwise when viewed from high on the \(z\)-axis, Stokes' theorem requires that we use the normal \(\hn\) to \(S\) with positive \(z\) component. Hence

\begin{align*} \hn\,dS&=\Big[-\pdiff{f}{x}\,\hi -\pdiff{f}{y}\,\hj+\hk\Big]\,\dee{x}\,\dee{y} =\Big[-2y\,\hj+\hk\Big]\,\dee{x}\,\dee{y}\\ \vnabla\times\vF&=\det\left[\begin{matrix}\hi & \hj & \hk \\ \pdiff{}{x} & \pdiff{}{y} & \pdiff{}{z} \\ x^2-y & y^2+x & 1 \end{matrix}\right] =2\hk\\ \vnabla\times\vF\cdot\hn\,dS&=2\,\dee{x}\,\dee{y}\\ \oint_C\vF\cdot \dee{\vr} &=\dblInt_S\vnabla\times\vF\cdot\hn\,\dee{S} =2\dblInt_{x^2+y^2\le 1}\,\dee{x}\,\dee{y}\\ &=2\pi \end{align*}
