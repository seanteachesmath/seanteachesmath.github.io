Solution 8.2.2.12.1.

(a) The table below is a record of our account, with black entries representing the money your friend gives you, and red entries representing the money you give them (which is why the red entries are negative).

\begin{equation*} \begin{array}{c||c|c||c} \mathbf{d}&\color{red}{\mathbf{-\frac{1}{d+1}}}&\mathbf{\frac1d}&\text{}\\ \hline 1 & \color{red}{-\frac{1}{2}} & 1 & \frac{1}{2}\\ 2&\color{red}{-\frac{1}{3}} & \frac{1}{2}&\frac{2}{3}\\ 3&\color{red}{-\frac{1}{4}} & \frac{1}{3}&\frac{3}{4}\\ 4&\color{red}{-\frac{1}{5}} & \frac{1}{4}&\frac{4}{5}\\ 5&\color{red}{-\frac{1}{6}} & \frac{1}{5}&\frac{5}{6}\\ 6&\color{red}{-\frac{1}{7}} & \frac{1}{6}&\frac{6}{7} \end{array} \end{equation*}

After the exchange of day \(n\text{,}\) the amount you're left with is \(\left(1-\frac{1}{n+1}\right)\text{.}\) We see this by the cancellation in the table: the $\(\frac12\) you gave your friend on day 1 was returned on day 2; the $\(\frac13\) you gave your friend on day 2 was returned on day 3, etc.

So, after a long time, you'll have gained close to (but always slightly less than) one dollar.

(b) The series \(\displaystyle\sum_{d=1}^\infty \left(\frac{1}{d}-\frac{1}{(d+1)}\right)\) describes the scenario in (a), so by our reasoning there,

\begin{equation*} \displaystyle\sum_{d=1}^\infty \left(\frac{1}{d}-\frac{1}{(d+1)}\right) = \lim_{n \to \infty}\left(1-\frac{1}{n+1}\right)=1 \end{equation*}

(c) Again, let's set up an account book.

\begin{equation*} \begin{array}{c||c|c||c} \mathbf{d}&\mathbf{d+1}&\color{red}{\mathbf{-(d+2)}}&\text{}\\ \hline 1 & 2& \color{red}{ -3} & -1\\ 2 & 3& \color{red}{ -4} & -2\\ 3 & 4& \color{red}{ -5} & -3\\ 4 & 5& \color{red}{ -6} & -4\\ 5 & 6& \color{red}{ -7} & -5\\ 6 & 7& \color{red}{ -8} & -6\\ \end{array} \end{equation*}

By day \(d\text{,}\) you've lost $ \(d\) to your so-called friend. As time goes on, you lose more and more.

(d) The series \(\displaystyle\sum_{d=1}^\infty ((d+1)-(d+2))\) exactly describes the scenario in part (c), so it diverges to \(-\infty\text{.}\) You can also see this by writing \(\displaystyle\sum_{d=1}^\infty ((d+1)-(d+2)) =\sum_{d=1}^\infty (-1)=-1-1-1-1-1-\cdots\text{.}\)

Be careful to avoid a common mistake with telescoping series: if we look back at our account book, we see that every negative term will cancel with a positive term, with the initial \(+2\) as the only term that never cancels. Your friend takes $3, which they return the next day; then they take $4, which they return the next day; then they take $5, which they return the next day, and so on. It's extremely tempting to say that the series adds up to $2, since every other term cancels out eventually. This is where we lean on Definition 8.2.3: we evaluate the partial sums, which always leave your friend's last withdrawal unreturned. This definition makes sense: saying “I gained two bucks from this exchange” doesn't really capture the reality of your increasing debt.
