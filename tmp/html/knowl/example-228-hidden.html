Evaluate the integral

\begin{gather*} \int 3x^2 \cos(x^3) \dee{x} \end{gather*}

Solution: Again we are going to use the substitution rule and helpfully our integrand is a product of two factors

\begin{gather*} \underbrace{3x^2}_\text{first factor} \cdot \underbrace{\cos(x^3)}_\text{second factor} \end{gather*}

The second factor, \(\cos\big(x^3\big)\) is a function, namely \(\cos\text{,}\) with a complicated argument, namely \(x^3\text{.}\) So we try \(u(x)= x^3\text{.}\) Then \(u'(x) = 3x^2\text{,}\) which is the other factor in the integrand. So the integral becomes

\begin{align*} \int 3x^2 \cos(x^3) \dee{x} &= \int u'(x) \cos\big(u(x)\big) \dee{x} &\text{just swap order of factors}\\ &= \int \cos\big(u(x)\big) u'(x) \dee{x} & \text{by the substitution rule}\\ &= \int \cos(u) \dee{u}\bigg|_{u=x^3}\\ &= \left( \sin(u) + C\right)\bigg|_{u=x^3} & \text{using Theorem }\knowl{./knowl/thm_imp_indef_int.html}{\text{6.3.17}})\\ &= \sin(x^3)+C \end{align*}
