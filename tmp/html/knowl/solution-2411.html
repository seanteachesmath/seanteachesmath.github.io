Solution 16.2.5.1.

(a) False. We could have, for example, \(\vnabla\cdot\vF\) zero at one point and strictly positive elsewhere. One example would be \(\vF = x^3\,\hi+y^3\,\hj +z^3\,\hk\text{,}\) with \(S_1\) and \(S_2\) being the upward oriented top and bottom hemispheres, respectively, of the unit sphere \(x^2+y^2+z^2=1\text{.}\)

(b) False. The conditions that (1) \(\vnabla\times\vF\) = 0 and (2) the domain of \(\vF\) is simply-connected, are sufficient, but not necessary, to imply that \(\vF\) is conservative. For example the vector field \(\vF=\vZero\text{,}\) with any domain at all, is conservative with potential \(0\text{.}\) Another example (which does not depend on choosing a domain that is smaller than the largest possible domain) is \(\vF=\vnabla\frac{1}{x^2+y^2}\) with domain \(\Set{(x,y,z)}{ (x,y)\ne (0,0)}\text{.}\) That is, the domain is \(\bbbr^3\) with the \(z\)-axis removed.

(c) That's true. Consider any point \(\vr(t_0)\) on a parametrized curve \(\vr(t)\text{.}\) That's the blue point in the figure below.

The centre of curvature for the curve at \(\vr(t_0)\) is \(\vc = \vr(t_0)+\rho(t_0)\hat\vN(t_0)\text{.}\) It is the red dot in the figure.

  • The radius of the osculating circle is the distance from its centre, \(\vc\text{,}\) to any point of the circle, like \(\vr(t_0)\text{.}\) That's \(|\vr(t_0) -\vc| = |\rho(t_0)\hat\vN(t_0)| = \rho(t_0)\text{.}\) The curvature of the osculating circle is one over its radius. So its curvature is \(\frac{1}{\rho(t_0)}=\ka(t_0)\text{.}\)
  • The unit normal to the osculating circle at \(\vr(t_0)\) is a unit vector in the opposite direction to the radius vector from the centre \(\vc\) to \(\vr(t_0)\text{.}\) The radius vector is \(\vr(t_0)-\vc_0=-\rho(t_0)\hat\vN(t_0)\text{,}\) so the unit normal is \(\hat\vN(t_0)\text{.}\)
  • The osculating circle lies in the plane that best fits the curve near \(\vr(t_0)\text{.}\) (See the beginning of ยง12.4.) So the unit tangents to the osculating circle at \(\vr(t_0)\) are perpendicular to both \(\hat\vN(t_0)\) and \(\hat\vB(t_0)\) and so are either \(\hat\vT(t_0)\) or \(-\hat\vT(t_0)\text{,}\) depending on how we orient the osculating circle.

(d) False. Kepler's third law is that a planet orbiting a sun has the square of the period proportional to the cube of the major axis of the orbit.

(e) True. That's part (a) of Theorem 15.1.7.

(f) True. Every domain contains closed surfaces. This has nothing to do with vector fields.

(g) True. We saw this in Example 13.3.4.

(h) False. Let \(\vF\) be an everywhere defined conservative vector field with potential \(\varphi\text{.}\) Then \(\vnabla\times\vF=\vZero\) everywhere. If \(P\) and \(Q\) are two points and if \(\varphi(P)-\varphi(Q)=3\) and if \(C\) is a curve from \(Q\) to \(P\text{,}\) then \(\int_C \vF \cdot \dee{\vr} = 3\text{.}\) One example would be \(\varphi(x,y,z) = x\text{,}\) \(\vF=\hi\text{,}\) \(P=(3,0,0)\text{,}\) \(Q=(0,0,0)\text{.}\)

(i) False. The normal component of acceleration depends on speed, as well as curvature.

(j) False. The curve \(\vr_1\) contains only points in the \(xy\)-plane. Every \(\vr_2(t)\) with \(t\ne 0\) has a nonzero \(z\)-coordinate.
