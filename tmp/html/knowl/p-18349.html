Method I â€” double Stokes': Let \(\cD\) denote the plane disk \(x^2+y^2\le 4\text{,}\) \(z=0\text{.}\) Using Stokes' theorem twice gives

\begin{align*} \dblInt_\cS \vG\cdot \hn\,\dee{S} &= \dblInt_\cS \nabla\times\vF\cdot \hn\,\dee{S} = \oint_\cC \vF\cdot \dee{\vr} = \dblInt_\cD \nabla\times\vF\cdot \hn\,\dee{S}\\ &= \dblInt_\cD \vG\cdot \hn\,\dee{S} \end{align*}

Now in \(\cD\) we have \(\hn=\hk\) and \(z=0\text{,}\) so on this surface,

\begin{align*} \vG\cdot\hn = (\nabla\times\vF)\cdot\hk &= \det\left[\begin{matrix} 0 & 0 & 1 \\ \frac{\partial }{\partial x} & \frac{\partial }{\partial y} & \frac{\partial }{\partial z} \\ (xz - y^3\cos z) & x^3 e^z & xyze^{x^2+y^2+z^2} \end{matrix}\right]_{z=0}\\ &= \big[3x^2 e^z + 3y^2\cos z\big]_{z=0} = 3(x^2+y^2) \end{align*}

Hence, using polar coordinates,

\begin{align*} \dblInt_\cS \vG\cdot \hn\,\dee{S} &= \dblInt_\cD 3(x^2+y^2)\,\dee{x}\dee{y} = 3\int_{\theta=0}^{2\pi} \int_{r=0}^2 (r^2)\,r\,\dee{r}\,\dee{\theta}\\ &= 3(2\pi)(4)= 24\pi \end{align*}
