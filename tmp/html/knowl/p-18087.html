Call the region enclosed by the curve \(R\text{.}\) By Green's theorem, Theorem 15.3.2,

\begin{align*} \frac{1}{2}\oint_C x\,\dee{y}-y\,\dee{x} &=\frac{1}{2}\dblInt_R\Big(\pdiff{}{x}x -\pdiff{}{y}(-y)\Big)\ \dee{x}\dee{y} =\frac{1}{2}\dblInt_R 2\ \dee{x}\dee{y}\\ &=A \end{align*}

as desired. The curve \(x^{2/3}+y^{2/3}=1\) may be parametrized in the counterclockwise orientation by \(x(\theta)=\cos^3\theta\text{,}\) \(y(\theta)=\sin^3\theta\text{,}\) \(0\le\theta\le 2\pi\text{.}\) Then

\begin{align*} A&=\frac{1}{2}\oint_C x\,\dee{y}-y\,\dee{x}\\ &=\frac{1}{2}\int_0^{2\pi}\big( x(\theta)y'(\theta)-y(\theta)x'(\theta)\big)\ \dee{\theta}\\ &=\frac{1}{2}\int_0^{2\pi}\big( 3\cos^4\theta\sin^2\theta+3\sin^4\theta\cos^2\theta\big)\ \dee{\theta}\\ &=\frac{3}{2}\int_0^{2\pi}\sin^2\theta\cos^2\theta\ \dee{\theta} =\frac{3}{8}\int_0^{2\pi}\sin^2(2\theta)\ \dee{\theta}\\ &=\frac{3}{16}\int_0^{2\pi}\big(1-\cos(4\theta)\big)\ \dee{\theta} =\frac{3}{16}\Big[\theta-\frac{1}{4}\sin(4\theta)\Big]_0^{2\pi} =\frac{3\pi}{8} \end{align*}
