Both \(V_1\) and \(V_2\) are spherical balls centred on \((0,2,0)\text{.}\) The difference between them is that \(V_1\) has radius \(3\) while \(V_2\) has radius \(1\text{.}\) In particular \((0,0,0)\) is not in \(V_2\text{.}\) So \(\vnabla\cdot\vF\) is well-defined and zero throughout \(V_2\) and, by the divergence theorem (Theorem 15.2.2),

\begin{gather*} \dblInt_{S_2} \vF\cdot\hn\,\dee{S} =\tripInt_{V_2} \vnabla\cdot\vF\,\dee{V} =0 \end{gather*}

On the other hand, \((0,0,0)\) is in \(V_1\text{.}\) We cannot blindly apply the divergence theorem to \(V_1\) â€” \(\vnabla\cdot\vF(x,y,z)\) is not defined at the point \((x,y,z)=(0,0,0)\) in \(V_1\text{.}\) We can work around this obstruction by

  • choosing a number \(\rho \gt 0\) that is small enough that the sphere
    \begin{equation*} S_\rho = \Set{(x,y,z)}{x^2+y^2+z^2=\rho^2} \end{equation*}
    is completely contained inside \(V_1\) (for example, \(\rho=\frac{1}{2}\) is fine)
  • and then removing the interior of \(S_\rho\) from \(V_1\text{.}\)

This produces

\begin{equation*} V_3 = \Set{(x,y)}{x^2 + (y - 2)^2 + z^2 \le 9,\ x^2+y^2+z^2\ge\rho^2} \end{equation*}
