Solution 8.3.11.18.1.

We usually check the divergence test first, to look for low-hanging fruit. The limit of the terms being added is zero:

\begin{equation*} \lim_{n \to \infty}\frac{1}{n+\frac{1}{2}}=0 \end{equation*}

so the divergence test is inconclusive. That is, we need to look harder.

Next, we might consider a comparison test â€” these can also provide us (if we're lucky) with an easy path. The terms we're adding look somewhat like \(\frac{1}{n}\text{,}\) but our terms are smaller than these terms, which form the terms of the divergent harmonic series. So, a direct comparison seems unlikely. Now we search for more exotic tests.

Let \(\displaystyle f(x) =\frac{1}{x+\frac{1}{2}}\text{.}\) Note \(f(x)\) is positive and decreases as \(x\) increases. So, by the integral test, which is Theorem 8.3.5, the given series converges if and only if the integral \(\int_0^\infty\frac{1}{x+\frac{1}{2}} \,\dee{x}\) converges. Since

\begin{align*} \int_0^\infty \frac{1}{x+\frac{1}{2}} \,\dee{x} = \lim_{R\rightarrow\infty} \int_0^R\frac{1}{x+\frac{1}{2}} \,\dee{x} &=\lim_{R\rightarrow\infty} \bigg[ \log\Big(x+\frac{1}{2}\Big)\bigg]^{x=R}_{x=0}\\ &=\lim_{R\to\infty} \bigg[ \log\Big(R+\frac{1}{2}\Big) - \log\frac12 \bigg] \end{align*}

diverges, the series diverges.
