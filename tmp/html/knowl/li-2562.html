We can simplify this further using a double-angle identity. Recall that \(u = \arcsin x\) and that \(x=\sin u\text{.}\) Then
\begin{align*} \sin 2u &= 2 \sin u \cos u\\ \\ \end{align*}

We can replace \(\cos u\) using \(\cos^2u = 1 - \sin^2u\text{.}\) Taking a square-root of this formula gives \(\cos u= \pm \sqrt{1-\sin^2u}\text{.}\) We need the positive branch here since \(\cos u \geq 0\) when \(-\frac\pi2 \leq u \leq \frac\pi2\) (which is exactly the range of \(\arcsin x\)). Continuing along:

\begin{align*} \sin2u &= 2 \sin u \cdot \sqrt{1-\sin^2 u}\\ &= 2 x \sqrt{1-x^2} \end{align*}
Thus our solution is
\begin{align*} \int \frac{x^2}{\sqrt{1-x^2}}\dee{x} &= \frac{1}{2}\arcsin x - \frac{1}{4} \sin(2\arcsin x) +C\\ &= \frac{1}{2}\arcsin x - \frac{1}{2} x \sqrt{1-x^2} +C \end{align*}
