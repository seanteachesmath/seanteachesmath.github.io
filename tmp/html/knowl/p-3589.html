As \(t\) moves from \(a\) to \(x\text{,}\) the instantaneous slope \(f'(t)\) keeps changing. Sometimes \(f'(t)\) might be larger than the average slope \(\tfrac{f(x)-f(a)}{x-a}\text{,}\) and sometimes \(f'(t)\) might be smaller than the average slope \(\tfrac{f(x)-f(a)}{x-a}\text{.}\) However, by the Mean-Value Theorem (TheoremÂ 3.13.5), there must be some number \(c\text{,}\) strictly between \(a\) and \(x\text{,}\) for which \(f'(c)=\dfrac{f(x)-f(a)}{x-a}\) exactly.
