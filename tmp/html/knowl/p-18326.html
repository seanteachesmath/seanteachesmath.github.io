The integral looks messy. Let's compute the curl of

\begin{equation*} \vF = (z + \sin z) \,\hi + (x^3 - x^2 y) \,\hj + (x \cos z - y) \,\hk \end{equation*}

to help gauge if Stokes' theorem would be easier.

\begin{align*} \vnabla\times\vF &=\det\left[\begin{matrix} \hi &\hj &\hk\\ \tfrac{\partial }{\partial x} & \tfrac{\partial }{\partial y} & \tfrac{\partial }{\partial z}\\ z + \sin z & x^3 - x^2 y & x \cos z - y \end{matrix} \right]\\ &=-\hi +\hj +(3x^2-2xy)\,\hk \end{align*}

That's a lot simpler than \(\vF\text{.}\) For the surface \(z=f(x,y)=xy^2\text{,}\) with downward pointing normal (since \(C\) is traversed clockwise)

\begin{equation*} \hn\,\dee{S} = -\big(-f_x,-f_y,1\big)\,\dee{x}\dee{y} =\big(y^2,2xy,-1\big)\,\dee{x}\dee{y} \end{equation*}

by (14.3.2), So, writing

\begin{align*} S&=\Set{(x,y,z)}{z=xy^2,\ x^2+y^2\le 1}\\ D&=\Set{(x,y)}{x^2+y^2\le 1} \end{align*}

Stoke's theorem gives

\begin{align*} \int_C\vF\cdot\dee{\vr} &=\dblInt_S \vnabla\times\vF\cdot\hn\,\dee{S} =\dblInt_D\big\{-y^2+2xy -3x^2+2xy\big\}\,\dee{x}\dee{y}\\ &=-\dblInt_{x^2+y^2\le 1}\big\{3x^2+y^2-4xy\big\}\,\dee{x}\dee{y} \end{align*}

To evaluate this integral, switch to polar coordinates.

\begin{align*} \int_C\vF\cdot\dee{\vr} &=-\int_0^1\dee{r}\ r\int_0^{2\pi}\dee{\theta}\ \big\{3r^2\cos^2\theta+r^2\sin^2\theta -4r^2\sin\theta\cos\theta\big\}\\ &=-4\pi \int_0^1\dee{r}\ r^3 =-\pi \end{align*}

since \(\int_0^{2\pi}\sin\theta\cos\theta\,\dee{\theta} =\frac{1}{2}\int_0^{2\pi}\sin(2\theta)\,\dee{\theta}=0\) and \(\int_0^{2\pi}\sin^2\theta\,\dee{\theta} =\int_0^{2\pi}\cos^2\theta\,\dee{\theta} =\pi\text{.}\) (See Example 13.4.4.)
