Example 15.5.2.
  1. The open rectangle \(\cO=\big\{\ (x,y)\in\bbbr^2\ \big|\ 0 \lt x \lt 1,\ 0 \lt y \lt 1\ \big\}\) is an open subset of \(\bbbr^2\) because any point \(\va=(x_0,y_0)\in\cO\) is a nonzero distance, namely \(d=\min\big\{x_0,1-x_0,y_0,1-y_0\big\}\) away from the boundary of \(\cO\text{.}\) So the open ball \(B_{d/2}(\va)\) is contained in \(\cO\text{.}\)

  2. The closed rectangle \(\cC=\big\{\ (x,y)\in\bbbr^2\ \big|\ 0\le x \le 1,\ 0 \le y \le 1\ \big\}\) is not an open subset of \(\bbbr^2\text{.}\) For example, \(\vZero=(0,0)\) is a point in \(\cC\text{.}\) No matter what \(\veps \gt 0\) we pick, the open ball \(B_\veps(\vZero)\) is not contained in \(\cC\) because \(B_\veps(\vZero)\) contains the point \((-\frac{\veps}{2},0)\text{,}\) which is not in \(\cC\text{.}\)

  3. The \(x\)-axis, \(\cX=\big\{\ (x,y)\in\bbbr^2\ \big|\ y=0\ \big\}\text{,}\) in \(\bbbr^2\) is not an open subset of \(\bbbr^2\) because for any point \((x_0,0)\in\cX\) and any \(\veps \gt 0\text{,}\) the ball \(B_\veps\big((x_0,0)\big)\) contains points with nonzero \(y\)-coordinates and so is not contained in \(\cX\text{.}\)
  4. The union of open balls
    \begin{align*} &B_1\big((0,0)\big)\cup B_1\big((2,0)\big)\\ &\hskip1in=\Set{(x,y)\in\bbbr^2}{x^2+y^2 \lt 1\text{ or }(x\!-\!2)^2+y^2 \lt 1} \end{align*}
    is not connected, since any continuous path from, for example, \((2,0)\) to \((0,0)\) must leave the union. In the figure on the left below, an “empty disk” has been sketched at \((1,0)\) just to emphasise that the point \((1,0)\) is not in the union.

  5. On the other hand the union of “closed balls”

    \begin{equation*} \Set{(x,y)\in\bbbr^2}{x^2+y^2\le 1\text{ or }(x-2)^2+y^2\le 1} \end{equation*}

    is connected. For example, the straight line segment from \((2,0)\) to \((0,0)\) remains in the the union.
