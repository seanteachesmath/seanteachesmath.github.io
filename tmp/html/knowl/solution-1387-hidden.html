The definition of a Taylor series tells us we will be computing the coefficients in the series

\begin{equation*} \sum_{n=0}^\infty \frac{h^{(n)}(a)}{n!}(x-a)^n \end{equation*}

That is, we need a general description of \(h^{(n)}(a)\text{.}\) To find this, we take a few derivatives, and look for a pattern.

\begin{align*} h(x)&=e^{3x} & h(a)&=e^{3a}\\ h'(x)&=3e^{3x} & h'(a)&=3e^{3a}\\ h''(x)&=3^2e^{3x} & h''(a)&=3^2e^{3a}\\ h'''(x)&=3^3e^{3x} & h'''(a)&=3^3e^{3a}\\ &\vdots&&\vdots\\ h^{(n)}(x)&=3^{n}e^{3x} & h^{(n)}(a)&=3^{n}e^{3a} \end{align*}

The pattern for \(h^{(n)}(a)\) holds for all (whole numbers) \(n \ge 0\text{.}\) So, our Taylor series for \(h(x)\) is

\begin{equation*} \sum_{n=0}^\infty \frac{3^ne^{3a} }{n!}(x-a)^n \end{equation*}

To find its radius of convergence, we use the ratio test.

\begin{align*} \left|\frac{a_{n+1}}{a_n}\right|&=\left|\frac{3^{n+1}e^{3a} (x-a)^{n+1}}{(n+1)!}\cdot {\frac{n!}{3^ne^{3a}(x-a)^n }}\right|\\ &=\left|\frac{3^{n+1}}{3^n}\cdot\frac{e^{3a}}{e^{3a}}\cdot \frac{n!}{(n+1)!}\cdot\frac{(x-a)^{n+1}}{(x-a)^n}\right|\\ &=3\cdot\frac{1}{n+1}\cdot |x-a|\\ \lim_{n \to \infty }\left|\frac{a_{n+1}}{a_n}\right|&=\lim_{n \to \infty}\left[\frac{3}{n+1}\cdot |x-a|\right]=0 \end{align*}

Our series converges for every value of \(x\text{,}\) so its radius of convergence is \(\infty\text{.}\)
