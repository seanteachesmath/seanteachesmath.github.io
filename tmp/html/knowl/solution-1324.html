Solution 8.3.11.40.1.

(a) For very large \(k\text{,}\) \(k\ll k^2\) so that

\begin{equation*} a_n=\frac{\root{3}\of{k}}{k^2-k}\approx \frac{\root{3}\of{k}}{k^2} =\frac{1}{k^{5/3}}. \end{equation*}

We apply the limit comparison test with \(b_k=\frac{1}{k^{5/3}}\text{.}\) Since

\begin{gather*} \lim_{k\rightarrow\infty}\frac{a_k}{b_k} =\lim_{k\rightarrow\infty}\frac{\root{3}\of{k}/(k^2-k)}{1/k^{5/3}} =\lim_{k\rightarrow\infty}\frac{k^2}{k^2-k} =\lim_{k\rightarrow\infty}\frac{1}{1-1/k} =1 \end{gather*}

exists and is nonzero, and \(\sum\limits_{k=1}^\infty\frac{1}{k^{5/3}}\) converges (by the \(p\)-test with \(p=\frac{5}{3} \gt 1\)), the given series converges by the limit comparison test.

(b) The \(k^{\rm th}\) term in this series is \(a_k= \frac{k^{10}10^k(k!)^2}{(2k)!}\text{.}\) Factorials often work well with the ratio test, because they simplify so nicely in quotients.

\begin{align*} \frac{a_{k+1}}{a_k} &=\frac{(k+1)^{10}10^{k+1}((k+1)!)^2}{(2k+2)!}\cdot\frac{(2k)!}{k^{10}10^k(k!)^2}\\ \amp=10\Big(\frac{k+1}{k}\Big)^{10}\frac{(k+1)^2}{(2k+2)(2k+1)}\\ &=10\Big(1+\frac{1}{k}\Big)^{10}\frac{(1+1/k)^2}{(2+2/k)(2+1/k)} \end{align*}

As \(k\) tends to \(\infty\text{,}\) this converges to \(10\times 1\times\frac{1}{2\times 2} \gt 1\text{.}\) So the series diverges by the ratio test.

(c) We'll use the integal test. The \(k^{\rm th}\) term in the series is \(a_k=\frac{1}{k(\log k) (\log\log k)}=f(k)\) with \(f(x)=\frac{1}{x(\log x) (\log\log x)}\text{,}\) which is continuous, positive and decreasing for \(x\ge 3\text{.}\)

\begin{align*} \int_3^\infty f(x)\ \dee{x} &= \int_3^\infty \frac{\dee{x}}{x(\log x) (\log\log x)} =\lim_{R\rightarrow\infty} \int_3^R \frac{\dee{x}}{x(\log x) (\log\log x)}\\ &=\lim_{R\rightarrow\infty} \int_{\log 3}^{\log R} \frac{\dee{y}}{y\log y} \qquad\text{with }y=\log x,\ \dee{y}=\frac{\dee{x}}{x}\\ &= \lim_{R\rightarrow\infty}\int_{\log \log 3}^{\log \log R} \frac{\dee{t}}{t} \qquad\text{with }t=\log y,\ \dee{t}=\frac{\dee{y}}{y}\\ &=\lim_{R \to \infty}\Big[\log t\Big]_{\log \log 3}^{\log\log R}=\infty \end{align*}

Since the integral is divergent, the series is divergent as well by the integral test.
