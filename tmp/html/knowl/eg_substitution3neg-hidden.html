This integral looks a lot like that of Example 6.4.7. It makes sense to try \(u(x)=1-x^3\) since it is the argument of \(\sin(1-x^3)\text{.}\) We

  • substitute \(u=1-x^3\) and
  • replace \(\dee{x}\) with \(\frac{1}{u'(x)}\dee{u} = \frac{1}{-3x^2}\dee{u}\text{,}\)
  • when \(x=0\text{,}\) we have \(u=1-0^3=1\) and
  • when \(x=1\text{,}\) we have \(u=1-1^3=0\text{.}\)

So

\begin{align*} \int_0^1 x^2 \sin\big(1-x^3\big)\cdot \dee{x} &=\int_1^0 x^2 \sin(u) \cdot \frac{1}{-3x^2}\dee{u}\\ &= \int_1^0 -\frac{1}{3}\sin(u)\dee{u}.\\ \end{align*}

Note that the lower limit of the \(u\)-integral, namely \(1\text{,}\) is larger than the upper limit, which is \(0\text{.}\) There is absolutely nothing wrong with that. We can simply evaluate the \(u\)-integral in the normal way. Since \(-\cos(u)\) is an antiderivative of \(\sin(u)\text{:}\)

\begin{align*} &= \left[\frac{\cos(u)}{3} \right]_1^0\\ &= \frac{\cos(0)-\cos(1)}{3}\\ &= \frac{1-\cos(1)}{3}. \end{align*}
