Solution 16.2.19.1.

(a) \(\int_C \vnabla f\cdot \dee{\vr}=0\) is the work done along the curve using the conservative force \(\vnabla f\text{.}\) That work is difference between the potential \(f\) at the final point minus the potential \(f\) at the initial point. If the final and initial points are both on the level surface \(f(x,y,z)=0\text{,}\) that difference is zero.

(b) The rate of change of the specified vector is

\begin{equation*} \diff{ }{t} \vv(t)\times\vr(t) =\vv'(t)\times\vr(t)+\vv(t)\times\vv(t) \end{equation*}

The first term vanishes because \(\vv'(t)=\va(t)=f(t)\vr(t)\) is parallel to \(\vr(t)\text{.}\) The second term vanishes because \(\vv(t)=\vv(t)\text{.}\)

(c) Call the constant vector \(\vv\times\vr\) of part (b) \(\vN\text{.}\) This vector is a constant and is perpendicular to both \(\vv(t)\) and \(\vr(t)\text{.}\) In particular

\begin{equation*} \vN\cdot\vr(t)=0 \end{equation*}

Assuming that \(\vN\) is nonzero, this is the equation of the plane through the origin with normal vector \(\vN\text{.}\)

(d) Yes, as long as \(\hat\vT\text{,}\) \(\hat\vN\text{,}\) and \(\hat\vB\) are well-defined, since \(\hat\vB = \hat\vT\times\hat\vN\text{.}\)

(e) No. When the maximum speed occurs \(\difftwo{s}{t}=0\) so that \(\va = \ka(t)\,\big(\diff{s}{t}(t)\big)^2\,\hat\vN(t)\text{.}\) If the speed and (constant) curvature are nonzero, the acceleration is nonzero.
