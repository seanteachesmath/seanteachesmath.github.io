Of course, any finite sum can be computed exactly — just sum together the terms. What we mean by “computed exactly” in this context, is that we can rewrite the sum as a simple, and easily evaluated, formula involving the terminals of the sum. For example
\begin{equation*} \sum_{k=m}^n r^k = \frac{r^{n+1}-r^m}{r-1} \qquad \qquad \text{provided $r\neq1$} \end{equation*}
No matter what finite integers we choose for \(m\) and \(n\text{,}\) we can quickly compute the sum in just a few arithmetic operations. On the other hand, the sums,
\begin{align*} \sum_{k=m}^n \frac{1}{k} && \sum_{k=m}^n \frac{1}{k^2} \end{align*}
cannot be expressed in such clean formulas (though you can rewrite them quite cleanly using integrals). To explain more clearly we would need to go into a more detailed and careful discussion that is beyond the scope of this course.
