Solution 8.5.3.24.1.

First, we differentiate.

\begin{align*} f(x)&=\displaystyle\sum_{n=0}^{\infty}A_n(x-c)^n\\ f'(x)&=\sum_{n=0}^{\infty}nA_n(x-c)^{n-1}\\ &=\sum_{n=1}^{\infty}nA_n(x-c)^{n-1}\\ f'(c)&=\sum_{n=1}^\infty nA_n\cdot 0^{n-1}\\ &=A_1\cdot 1 + 2A_2\cdot0+3A_3\cdot0+\cdots\\ &=A_1 \end{align*}

So, if \(A_1=0\text{,}\) then \(f'(c)=0\text{.}\) That is, \(f(x)\) has a critical point at \(x=c\text{.}\)

To determine the behaviour of this critical point, we use the second derivative test.

\begin{align*} f'(x)&=\sum_{n=1}^{\infty}nA_n(x-c)^{n-1}\\ f''(x)&=\sum_{n=1}^{\infty}n(n-1)A_n(x-c)^{n-2}\\ &=\sum_{n=2}^{\infty}n(n-1)A_n(x-c)^{n-2}\\ f''(c)&=\sum_{n=2}^{\infty}n(n-1)A_n\cdot 0^{n-2}\\ &=2(1)A_2\cdot 0^0+3(2)A_3\cdot 0^1+4(3)A_4\cdot 0^2+\cdots\\ &=2A_2 \end{align*}

Following the second derivative test, \(x=c\) is the location of a local maximum if \(A_2 \lt 0\text{,}\) and it is the location of a local minimum if \(A_2 \gt 0\text{.}\) (If \(A_2=0\text{,}\) the critical point may or may not be a local extremum.)
