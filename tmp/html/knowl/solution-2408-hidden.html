(a) False. For any constant vector \(\va=(a_1,a_2,a_3)\text{,}\)

\begin{align*} \va\times\vr &=\det\left[\begin{matrix} \hi &\hj &\hk\\ a_1 & a_2 & a_3\\ x & y & z \end{matrix} \right] = (a_2 z - a_3 y)\hi -(a_1 z - a_3 x)\hj +(a_1 y - a_2 x)\hj \end{align*}

So

\begin{align*} \vnabla\times(\va\times\vr) &=\det\left[\begin{matrix} \hi &\hj &\hk\\ \tfrac{\partial }{\partial x} & \tfrac{\partial }{\partial y} & \tfrac{\partial }{\partial z}\\ a_2 z - a_3 y & -a_1 z + a_3 x & a_1 y - a_2 x \end{matrix} \right]\\ &=2 a_1\hi + 2 a_2\hj + 2 a_3\hk \end{align*}

is nonzero, unless the constant vector \(\va=\vZero\text{.}\)

(b) False. For example, if \(f(x) = x^2\text{,}\) then

\begin{gather*} \vnabla\cdot(\vnabla f) =\vnabla\cdot(\vnabla x^2) =\vnabla\cdot(2x\hi) =2 \end{gather*}

(c) False. For example, if \(\vF = x^2\hi\text{,}\) then

\begin{gather*} \vnabla(\vnabla\cdot\vF) =\vnabla\big(\vnabla\cdot(x^2\hi)\big) =\vnabla(2x) =2\hi \end{gather*}

(d) False. The trap here is that \(\vF\) need not be defined at the origin. We saw, in Example 14.4.2, that the point source \(\vF=\frac{m\vr}{|\vr|^3}\) had flux \(4\pi m\) through every sphere centred on the origin. We also saw, in Example 15.2.7, that the divergence \(\vnabla\cdot\vF=0\) everywhere except at the origin (where it is not defined).

(e) True. Any simple, smooth, closed curve in \(\bbbr^3\) that avoids the origin is the boundary of a surface \(S\) that also avoids the origin. Then, by Stokes' theorem,

\begin{equation*} \oint_C\vF\cdot\dee{\vr} =\dblInt_S \vnabla\times\vF\cdot\hn\,\dee{S} =0 \end{equation*}

(f) True. Let \(S=\Set{\vr}{|\vr-\vc|=R}\) be a sphere. Denote by \(V=\Set{\vr}{|\vr-\vc|\le R}\) the ball whose boundary is \(S\text{.}\) Let \(H\) be one hemisphere of \(S\) with outward pointing normal and let \(H'\) be the other hemisphere of \(S\) with inward point normal. Then the boundary of \(V\text{,}\) with outward pointing normal, can be viewed as consisting of two parts, namely \(H\) and \(-H'\text{,}\) where by \(-H'\) we mean \(H'\) but with outward pointing normal. Then, by the divergence theorem

\begin{align*} \dblInt_H \vF\cdot\hn\,\dee{S} - \dblInt_{H'} \vF\cdot\hn\,\dee{S} &=\dblInt_{\partial V} \vF\cdot\hn\,\dee{S}\\ &=\tripInt_V \vnabla\cdot\vF\,\dee{V} \gt 0 \end{align*}

which implies that \(\dblInt_H \vF\cdot\hn\,\dee{S} \gt \dblInt_{H'} \vF\cdot\hn\,\dee{S}\text{.}\)

(g) False. The trap here is that the curve is in \(\bbbr^3\text{,}\) not \(\bbbr^2\text{.}\) As we saw in Example 12.4.4, a helix has constant curvature, but does not lie in a plane and so is not part of a circle.

(h) False. Even if we restrict \(\vF\) to the \(xy\)-plane (i.e. to \(z=0\)), this vector field is not conservative. We saw that in Examples 13.3.14 and 15.3.8.

(i) False. For example, the vector field \(\vF = x\,\hk\) is always parallel to the \(z\)-axis. So its flow lines are also all parallel to the \(z\)-axis. But if the closed curve \(C\) consists of the line segments

  • \(L_1\) from \((0,0,0)\) to \((1,0,0)\text{,}\) followed by
  • \(L_2\) from \((1,0,0)\) to \((1,0,1)\text{,}\) followed by
  • \(L_3\) from \((1,0,1)\) to \((0,0,1)\text{,}\) followed by

  • \(L_4\) from \((0,0,1)\) back to \((0,0,0)\text{.}\)

then

  • \(\int_{L_1} \vF\cdot\dee{\vr} = \int_0^1 (x\hk)\cdot\hi\,\dee{x} = 0\) since \(\hk\perp\hi\text{,}\) \(\dee{\vr}=\hi\,\dee{x}\) on \(L_1\) and
  • \(\int_{L_2} \vF\cdot\dee{\vr} = \int_0^1 (1\hk)\cdot\hk\,\dee{z} = 1\) since \(x=1\) and \(\dee{\vr}=\hk\,\dee{z}\) on \(L_2\) and
  • \(\int_{L_3} \vF\cdot\dee{\vr} = -\int_0^1 (x\hk)\cdot\hi\,\dee{x} = 0\) since \(\hk\perp\hi\) and
  • \(\int_{L_4} \vF\cdot\dee{\vr} = -\int_0^1 (0\hk)\cdot\hk\,\dee{z} = 0\) since \(x=0\) on \(L_4\text{.}\)

All together

\begin{equation*} \int_{C} \vF\cdot\dee{\vr} =\int_{L_1} \vF\cdot\dee{\vr} +\int_{L_2} \vF\cdot\dee{\vr} +\int_{L_3} \vF\cdot\dee{\vr} +\int_{L_4} \vF\cdot\dee{\vr} =1 \end{equation*}

(j) True. If the speed \(|\vv|\) is constant then

\begin{gather*} 0= \diff{ }{t} |\vv|^2 = \diff{ }{t} (\vv\cdot\vv) = 2\vv\cdot\va \end{gather*}
