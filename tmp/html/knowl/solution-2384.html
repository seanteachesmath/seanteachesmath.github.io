Solution 15.4.3.19.1.

We are to evaluate the line integral of a complicated vector field around a relatively complicated closed curve. That certainly suggests that we should not try to evaluate the integral directly. To see if Stokes' theorem looks promising, let's compute the curl

\begin{align*} \vnabla\times\vF &=\det\left[\begin{matrix} \hi &\hj &\hk\\ \tfrac{\partial }{\partial x} & \tfrac{\partial }{\partial y} & \tfrac{\partial }{\partial z} \\ -y+e^x\sin x & y^4 & \sqrt{z}\tan z \end{matrix} \right] =\hk \end{align*}

That's suggestive. Next we need to find a surface whose boundary is \(C\text{.}\) First, here is a sketch of \(C\text{.}\)

We can choose the surface \(S\) to be the union of two flat parts:

  • the quadralateral \(Q\) in the \(yz\)-plane with vertices \((0,0,0)\text{,}\) \((0,1,1)\text{,}\) \((0,1,2)\) and \((0,2,0)\) and
  • the triangle \(T\) in the \(xy\)-plane with vertices \((0,0,0)\text{,}\) \((0,2,0)\) and \((2,2,0)\text{.}\)

The normal to \(Q\) is \(-\hi\) and the normal to \(T\) is \(-\hk\text{.}\) Then Stokes' theorem gives

\begin{align*} \int_C\vF\cdot\dee{\vr} &=\dblInt_S \vnabla\times\vF\cdot\hn\,\dee{S}\\ &=\dblInt_Q \hk\cdot(-\hi)\,\dee{S} + \dblInt_T \hk\cdot(-\hk)\,\dee{S}\\ &= - \dblInt_T \dee{S}\\ &=-\text{Area}(T)\\ &= -\half\overbrace{2}^{\text{base}}\overbrace{2}^{\text{height}}\\ &=-2 \end{align*}
