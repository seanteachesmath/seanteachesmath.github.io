(a) Since

  • \(\frac{y}{x}\) is defined when \(x\ne 0\) and
  • \(x^{1+x^2}=e^{(1+x^2)\ln x}\) is defined when \(\ln x\) is defined, which is when \(x \gt 0\) (assuming that we are not allowed to use complex numbers) and
  • \(y^{1+y^2}=e^{(1+y^2)\ln y}\) is defined when \(\ln y\) is defined, which is when \(y \gt 0\) and
  • \(\cos^5(\ln z)\) is defined when \(\ln z\) is defined, which when \(z \gt 0\)

the domain of \(\vF\) is

\begin{equation*} D=\Set{(x,y,z)}{x \gt 0,\ y \gt 0,\ z \gt 0} \end{equation*}

(b) The domain \(D\) is both connected (any two points in \(D\) can be joined by a curve that lies completely in \(D\)) and simply connected (any simple closed curve in \(D\) can be shrunk to a point continuously in \(D\)).

(c) The curl of \(\vF\) is

\begin{align*} \vnabla\times\vF &=\det\left[\begin{matrix} \hi &\hj &\hk\\ \tfrac{\partial }{\partial x} & \tfrac{\partial }{\partial y} & \tfrac{\partial }{\partial z}\\ \frac{y}{x} +x^{1+x^2} & x^2-y^{1+y^2} & \cos^5(\ln z) \end{matrix} \right] =\big(2x-\frac{1}{x}\big)\hk \end{align*}

(d) The integrand for direct evaluation looks very complicated. On the other hand \(\vnabla\times\vF\) is quite simple. So let's try Stokes' thoerem. Denote

\begin{equation*} S=\Set{(x,y,z)}{2\le x\le 4,\ 2\le y\le 4,\ z=2} \end{equation*}

The boundary of \(S\) is \(C\text{.}\) Because of the clockwise orientation of \(C\text{,}\) we assign the normal vector \(-\hk\) to \(S\text{.}\) See the sketch below

Then, by Stokes' theorem,

\begin{align*} \oint_C\vF\cdot\dee{\vr} &=\dblInt_S \vnabla\times\vF\cdot\hn\,\dee{S} = \dblInt_S \vnabla\times\vF\cdot(-\hk)\,\dee{S}\\ &= -\dblInt_S \big(2x-\tfrac{1}{x}\big)\,\dee{S}\\ &= -\int_2^4\dee{x}\int_2^4\dee{y}\ \big(2x-\tfrac{1}{x}\big) =-\int_2^4\dee{x}\ 2\big(2x-\tfrac{1}{x}\big)\\ & = - 2\Big[x^2-\ln x\Big]_2^4 =-2\big[12-\ln 2\big] \\ &=2\ln 2-24 \end{align*}

(e) Since \(\vnabla\times\vF\) is not \(\vZero\text{,}\) \(\vF\) cannot be conservative.
