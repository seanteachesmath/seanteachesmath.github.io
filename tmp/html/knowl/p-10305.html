This is close to being in the form of a geometric series. First, we should have our powers be \(k\text{,}\) not \(k+2\text{,}\) but we notice \(3^{k+2}=3^k3^2=9\cdot3^2\text{,}\) so:

\begin{align*} \sum_{k=0}^\infty\frac{2^k}{3^{k+2}}&=\sum_{k=0}^\infty\frac{2^k}{9\cdot3^k} =\frac{1}{9}\sum_{k=0}^\infty\frac{2^k}{3^k}=\frac{1}{9}\sum_{k=0}^\infty\left(\frac{2}{3}\right)^k\\ \end{align*}

Now it looks like a geometric series with \(r=\frac{2}{3}\)

\begin{align*} &=\frac{1}{9}\left(\frac{1}{1-(2/3)}\right)=\frac{1}{3} \end{align*}

In conclusion: this (geometric) series is convergent, and its sum is \(\dfrac{1}{3}\text{.}\)
