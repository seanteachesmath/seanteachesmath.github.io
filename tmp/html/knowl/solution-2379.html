Solution 15.4.3.14.1.

(a) The boundary, \(\partial S_1\text{,}\) of \(S_1\) as specified in Stokes' theorem (Theorem 15.4.1) is the circle \(\sqrt{x^2+y^2}=4\text{,}\) \(z=4\) oriented clockwise when viewed from high on the \(z\)-axis. That is, we can parametrize \(\partial S_1\) by

\begin{gather*} \vr(t) = 4\cos t\,\hi -4\sin t\,\hj +4\,\hk,\qquad 0\le t\le 2\pi \end{gather*}

So

\begin{align*} \vF\big(\vr(t)\big)\cdot\dee{\vr} &= \big(16\sin t \,,\, 16\cos t \,,\, -16\sin t\cos t \cos(-16\sin t) \big)\\ &\hskip2in \cdot \big(-4\sin t \,,\, -4\cos t \,,\, 0\big)\,\dee{t}\\ &= -64\,\dee{t} \end{align*}

and, by Stokes' theorem,

\begin{align*} \dblInt_{S_1} \vnabla\times\vF \cdot \hn\,\dee{S} &= \oint_{\partial S_1} \vF\big(\vr(t)\big)\cdot\dee{\vr} = -64 \int_0^{2\pi} \dee{t} =-128\pi \end{align*}

(b) The boundary, \(\partial S_2\text{,}\) of \(S_2\) consists of two parts, a circle in the plane \(z=4\) and a circle in the plane \(z=1\text{.}\) We'll call the first part \(\partial S_{2a}\text{.}\) It is the same as \(\partial S_1\text{.}\) We'll call the second part \(\partial S_{2b}\text{.}\) It is the circle \(\sqrt{x^2+y^2}=1\text{,}\) \(z=1\) oriented counterclockwise when viewed from high on the \(z\)-axis. We can parametrize it

\begin{gather*} \vr(t) = \cos t\,\hi +\sin t\,\hj + \hk,\qquad 0\le t\le 2\pi \end{gather*}

So, on \(\partial S_{2b}\text{,}\)

\begin{align*} \vF\big(\vr(t)\big)\cdot\dee{\vr} &= \big(-\sin t \,,\, \cos t \,,\, \sin t\cos t \cos(\sin t) \big) \cdot \big(-\sin t \,,\, \cos t \,,\, 0\big)\,\dee{t}\\ &= \dee{t} \end{align*}

and, by Stokes' theorem,

\begin{align*} \dblInt_{S_2} \vnabla\times\vF \cdot \hn\,\dee{S} &= \oint_{\partial S_{2a}} \vF\big(\vr(t)\big)\cdot\dee{\vr} +\oint_{\partial S_{2b}} \vF\big(\vr(t)\big)\cdot\dee{\vr}\\ &= -128\pi + \int_0^{2\pi} \dee{t}\\ &=-126\pi \end{align*}
