Solution 12.4.1.9.1.

First, some preliminaries:

\begin{align*} \vr(t)&=(t^3,t,e^{ct}) & \vr(5)&=(5^3,5,e^{5c})\\ \vv(t)&=(3t^2,1,ce^{ct}) & \vv(5)&=(3\cdot 5^2,1,ce^{5c})\\ \va(t)&=(6t,0,c^2e^{ct}) & \va(5)&=(6\cdot 5,0,c^2e^{5c})\\ \diff{\va}{t}(t)&= (6,0,c^3e^{ct})& \diff{\va}{t}(5)&= (6,0,c^3e^{5c})\\ && \vv(5) \times \va(5)&=(c^2e^{5c},15ce^{5c}(2-5c),-30) \end{align*}

Second, we figure out what value of \(c\) makes \(\tau(5)=0\text{.}\)

\begin{align*} 0&=\tau(5)=\dfrac{(\vv(5) \times \va(5))\cdot\diff{\va}{t}(5)}{|\vv(5) \times \va(5)|^2}\\ 0&=(\vv(5)\times\va(5))\cdot \diff{\va}{t}(5)\\ &=(c^2e^{5c},15ce^{5c}(2-5c),-30)\cdot (6,0,c^3e^{5c})\\ &=6c^2e^{5c}(1-5c)\\ c&=0 \text{ or } c=\frac15 \end{align*}

If \(c=0\text{,}\) then \(\vr(t)=(t^3,t,1)\text{,}\) and so the entire curve is contained inside the plane \(z=1\text{.}\) (Its torsion is zero everywhere â€” not just at \(t=5\text{.}\))

Consider the case \(c=\frac15\text{.}\) When \(t=5\text{,}\) our curve (and its osculating circle) passes through the point \(\vr(5)=(5^3,5,e)\text{.}\) The normal vector to the plane of the osculating curve is the binormal vector \(\hB(5)=\frac{\vv(5)\times \va(5)}{|\vv(5)\times \va(5)|}\text{.}\) Since we don't need the normal vector to the plane to be a unit vector, we can take as the normal vector to the plane simply \(\vv(5) \times \va(5)\text{,}\) or \((e/25,3e,-30)\text{.}\) Then, an equation of the plane containing the osculating circle is \((e/25)x+(3e)y-30z=-10e\text{.}\) An equivalent equation for this plane is \((1/25)x+3y-(30/e)z=-10\text{.}\)
