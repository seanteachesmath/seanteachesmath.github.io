(a) True. That \(\vnabla\cdot(\vnabla\times\vF)=0\) is the vector identity of Theorem 15.1.7.a. That identity is the basis of the vector potential screening test.

(b) False. If \(\vF\) is not conservative, then \(\int_C \vF\cdot\dee{\vr}\) will depend on the endpoints of \(C\text{.}\)

(c) True. If \(\vnabla f = 0\text{,}\) then

\begin{alignat*}{5} \frac{\partial f}{\partial x}(x,y,z)&=0 &&\quad\implies\quad & f(x,y,z)&=g(y,z)\\ \frac{\partial f}{\partial y}(x,y,z)&=0 &&\quad\implies\quad & \frac{\partial g}{\partial y}(y,z)&=0 &&\quad\implies\quad & g(y,z)&=h(z)\\ \frac{\partial f}{\partial z}(x,y,z)&=0 &&\quad\implies\quad & h'(z)&=0 &&\quad\implies\quad & h(z)&=C \end{alignat*}

for some functions \(g(y,z)\text{,}\) \(h(z)\) and constant \(C\text{.}\)

(d) False. The curl \(\vnabla\times\vF\) is zero for every conservative vector fields \(\vF\text{.}\) There are many nonconstant conservative vector fields, like \(\vF(x,y,z) = x\,\hi\text{.}\)

(e) True. As \(S\) is closed, it is the boundary of a solid region \(V\text{.}\) Then, by the divergence theorem,

\begin{equation*} \dblInt_S\vF\cdot\hn\, \dee{S} =\tripInt_V\vnabla\cdot\vF\,\dee{V} =0 \end{equation*}

(f) True. If \(\int_C \vF\cdot \dee{\vr} = 0\) for every closed curve \(C\text{,}\) then \(\vF\) is conservative by Theorem 13.4.7. Consequently, \(\vnabla \times\vF = 0\) by Theorem 13.3.9.

(g) True. If the speed \(|\vv|\) is constant then

\begin{gather*} 0= \diff{ }{t} |\vv|^2 = \diff{ }{t} (\vv\cdot\vv) = 2\vv\cdot\va \end{gather*}

Since \(\hat\vT=\frac{\vv}{|\vv|}\text{,}\) \(\hat\vT\cdot\va=0\) too. Here, we have assumed that the constant \(|\vv|\) is not zero. If the constant \(|\vv|\) is zero, then \(\hat\vT\) is not defined at all (and \(\va=0\)).

(h) False. The trap here is that the curve is in \(\bbbr^3\text{,}\) not \(\bbbr^2\text{.}\) As we saw in Example 12.4.4, a helix has constant curvature, but does not lie in a plane and so is not part of a circle.

(i) False. The trap here is that we are told nothing about \(\vnabla\cdot\vF\text{.}\) As an example, let \(S_1\) be the hemisphere

\begin{equation*} S_1=\Set{(x,y,z)}{x^2+y^2+z^2=1,\ z\ge 0} \end{equation*}

with upward pointing normal and \(S_2\) be the disk

\begin{equation*} S_2=\Set{(x,y,0)}{x^2+y^2\le 1} \end{equation*}

also with upward pointing normal.

Set

\begin{equation*} V=\Set{(x,y,z)}{0\le z\le \sqrt{x^2+y^2},\ x^2+y^2\le 1} \end{equation*}

Then the boundary, \(\partial V\text{,}\) of \(V\) consists of two parts, namely \(S_1\) (with normal pointing upwards) and \(S_2\) (but with normal pointing downwards). The divergence theorem (Theorem 15.2.2) gives

\begin{align*} \dblInt_{S_1} \vF \cdot \hn\,\dee{S} -\dblInt_{S_2} \vF \cdot \hn\,\dee{S} &=\tripInt_V \vnabla\cdot\vF\,\dee{V} \end{align*}

If \(\vnabla\cdot\vF \gt 0\) (as is the case, for example, if \(\vF=x\,\hi\)) then \(\dblInt_{S_1} \vF \cdot \hn\,\dee{S} -\dblInt_{S_2} \vF \cdot \hn\,\dee{S}\) is definitely nonzero.

(j) True. This is one of Kepler's laws. See §12.9.
