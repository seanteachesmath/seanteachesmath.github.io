(b) We are to evaluate the line integral of a complicated vector field around a relatively complicated closed curve. That certainly suggests that we should not try to evaluate the integral directly. Let's try Stokes' theorem. First, we compute the curl

\begin{align*} \vnabla\times\vF &=\det\left[\begin{matrix} \hi &\hj &\hk\\ \tfrac{\partial }{\partial x} & \tfrac{\partial }{\partial y} & \tfrac{\partial }{\partial z}\\ \frac{2z}{1+y}+\sin(x^2) & \frac{3z}{1+x}+\sin(y^2) & 5(x+1)(y+2) \end{matrix} \right]\\ &= \Big(5(x+1)-\frac{3}{1+x}\Big)\,\hi -\Big(5(y+2)-\frac{2}{1+y}\Big)\,\hj\\ &\hskip1in +\Big(-\frac{3z}{(1+x)^2}+\frac{2z}{(1+y)^2}\Big)\,\hk \end{align*}

Next we need to find a surface \(S\) whose boundary is \(C\text{.}\) We can choose the surface \(S\) to be the union of two flat parts:

  • the triangle \(S_x\) in the \(xz\)-plane with vertices \((0,0,0)\text{,}\) \((2,0,0)\text{,}\) and \((0,0,2)\) and
  • the triangle \(S_y\) in the \(yz\)-plane with vertices \((0,0,0)\text{,}\) \((0,0,2)\text{,}\) and \((0,3,0)\)

Note that

  • The normal to \(S_x\) specified by Stokes' theorem is \(-\hj\text{.}\) On \(S_x\) we have \(y=0\text{,}\) so that \(\vnabla\times\vF\cdot\hj\) simplifies to \(-\big(5(0+2)-\frac{2}{1+0}\big)=-8\text{.}\)
  • The normal to \(S_y\) specified by Stokes' theorem is \(-\hi\text{.}\) On \(S_y\) we have \(x=0\text{,}\) so that \(\vnabla\times\vF\cdot\hi\) simplifies to \(\big(5(0+1)-\frac{3}{1+0}\big)=2\text{.}\)

So Stokes' theorem gives

\begin{align*} \int_C\vF\cdot\dee{\vr} &=\dblInt_S \vnabla\times\vF\cdot\hn\,\dee{S}\\ &=\dblInt_{S_x} \overbrace{\vnabla\times\vF\cdot(-\hj)}^{8}\,\dee{S} + \dblInt_{S_y} \overbrace{\vnabla\times\vF\cdot(-\hi)}^{-2}\,\dee{S}\\ &=8\,\text{Area}(S_x) -2\,\text{Area}(S_y) =8\frac{1}{2}(2)(2) -2\frac{1}{2}(3)(2)\\ &=10 \end{align*}
