Since we started with a function of \(x\) we need to finish with one. We know that \(\sec u = x\) and then we can use trig identities
\begin{align*} \tan^2 u &= \sec^2 u - 1 = x^2-1 & \text{so } \tan u &= \pm \sqrt{x^2-1}\\ \end{align*}

but we know

\begin{align*} \tan u & \geq 0 & \text{so } \tan u &= \sqrt{x^2-1} \end{align*}
Thus
\begin{align*} \int \frac{x^2}{\sqrt{x^2-1}} \dee{x} &= \frac{1}{2}x\sqrt{x^2-1} + \frac{1}{2}\log| x +\sqrt{x^2-1}| +C \end{align*}
