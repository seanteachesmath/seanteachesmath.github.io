In part (b) we will need to evaluate a line integral that clearly cannot be computed directly â€” we will need to use Stokes' theorem. So let's use Stokes's theorem in part (a) too. First, we find

\begin{align*} \vnabla\times\vF &=\det\left[\begin{matrix} \hi &\hj &\hk\\ \tfrac{\partial }{\partial x} & \tfrac{\partial }{\partial y} & \tfrac{\partial }{\partial z}\\ z^2 & x^2 & y^2 \end{matrix} \right] =2y\,\hi + 2z\,\hj + 2x\,\hi \end{align*}

Let \(S\) be the triangular surface that is contained in the plane \(x+y+z=2\) and is bounded by \(L_1\text{,}\) \(L_2\) and \(L_3\text{.}\) Orient \(S\) by the normal vector \(\hn = \frac{1}{\sqrt{3}}(\hi+\hj+\hk)\text{.}\) Then,

\begin{equation*} \vnabla\times\vF\cdot\hn =\frac{1}{\sqrt{3}}\big(2y\,\hi + 2z\,\hj + 2x\,\hi\big)\cdot(\hi+\hj+\hk) =\frac{2}{\sqrt{3}}(x+y+z) \end{equation*}

and, by Stokes' theorem,

\begin{align*} \oint_C\vF\cdot \dee{\vr} &=\dblInt_S\vnabla\times\vF\cdot\hn\,\dee{S} =\frac{2}{\sqrt{3}}\dblInt_S(x+y+z)\,\dee{S} =\frac{4}{\sqrt{3}}\dblInt_S\dee{S}\\ &=\frac{4}{\sqrt{3}}\text{Area}(S) \end{align*}

The triangle \(S\) is half of the prallelogram with sides \((0,2,0)-(2,0,0) =(-2,2,0)\) and \((0,0,2)-(2,0,0)=(-2,0,2)\text{.}\) The area of the parallelogram is

\begin{gather*} \big|(-2,2,0)\times (-2,0,2)\big| =\big|(4,4,4)\big| =4\sqrt{3} \end{gather*}

So

\begin{gather*} \oint_C\vF\cdot \dee{\vr} = \frac{4}{\sqrt{3}} 2\sqrt{3} =8 \end{gather*}
