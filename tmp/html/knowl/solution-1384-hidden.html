The definition of a Taylor series tells us we will be computing the coefficients in the series

\begin{equation*} \sum_{n=0}^\infty \frac{f^{(n)}(1)}{n!}(x-1)^n \end{equation*}

That is, we need a general description of \(f^{(n)}(1)\text{.}\) To find this, we take a few derivatives, and look for a pattern.

\begin{align*} f(x)&=\log(x) & f(1)&=0\\ f'(x)&=x^{-1} & f'(1)&=1\\ f''(x)&=(-1)x^{-2} & f''(1)&=-1\\ f^{(3)}(x)&=(-2)(-1)x^{-3}& f^{(3)}(1)&=2!\\ f^{(4)}(x)&=(-3)(-2)(-1)x^{-4}& f^{(4)}(1)&=-3!\\ f^{(5)}(x)&=(-4)(-3)(-2)(-1)x^{-5}& f^{(5)}(1)&=4!\\ f^{(6)}(x)&=(-5)(-4)(-3)(-2)(-1)x^{-6}& f^{(6)}(1)&=-5!\\ &\vdots&&\vdots\\ f^{(n)}(x)&=(-1)^{n-1}(n-1)!\,x^{-n} & f^{(n)}(1)&=(-1)^{n-1}(n-1)! \end{align*}

Using the convention \(0!=1\text{,}\) our pattern for \(f^{(n)}(1)\) begins when \(n=1\text{.}\)

\begin{align*} \sum_{n=0}^\infty \frac{f^{(n)}(1)}{n!}(x-1)^n \amp=0+\sum_{n=1}^\infty \frac{(-1)^{n-1}(n-1)! }{n!}(x-1)^n\\ \amp=\sum_{n=1}^\infty \frac{(-1)^{n-1}}{n}(x-1)^n \end{align*}
