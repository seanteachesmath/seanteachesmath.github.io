Solution 16.2.4.1.

(a) The helix is approximately a bunch of circles stacked one on top of each other. The radius of the circles increase as \(z\) increases. So the curvature decreases as \(z\) increases.

(b) Here are two arguments both of which conclude that \(f(x)\) is \(D\text{.}\)

  • If \(C\) were the graph \(y=f(x)\text{,}\) then \(f'(x)\) would have two points of discontinuity. The curvature \(\ka(x)\) would not the defined at those two points. The function whose graph is \(D\) is defined everywhere and so cannot be the curvature of the function whose graph is \(C\text{.}\)
  • The function whose graph is \(D\) has two inflection points. So its curvature is zero at two points. The function whose graph is \(C\) is indeed zero at two points (that in fact correspond to the inflection points of \(D\)). So \(D\) is the graph of \(f(x)\) and \(C\) is the graph of \(\kappa(x)\text{.}\)

(c) For any fixed \(y\text{,}\) \(x^2+z^2=1\) is a circle of radius \(1\text{.}\) So we can parametrize it by \(x(\theta)=\cos\theta\text{,}\) \(z(\theta)=\sin\theta\text{,}\) \(0\le\theta \lt 2\pi\text{.}\) The \(y\)-coordinate of any point on the intersection is determined by \(y=xz\text{.}\) So we can use

\begin{equation*} \vr(\theta) = \cos\theta\,\hi +\sin\theta\,\hk + \sin\theta\,\cos\theta\,\hj \qquad 0\le\theta \lt 2\pi \end{equation*}

(d) We are told that the helical ramp starts starts with the \(y\)-axis when \(z=0\text{.}\)

  • In the cases of parametrisations (a) and (c), \(z=0\) forces \(u=0\) and \(u=0\) forces \(x=y=0\text{.}\) That is only the origin, not the \(y\)-axis. So we can rule out (a) and(c).
  • In the case of parametrisation (b), \(z=0\) forces \(v=0\) and \(v=0\) forces \(y=0\) and \(x=u\text{.}\) As \(u\) varies that sweeps out the \(x\)-axis, not the \(y\)-axis. So we can rule out (b).
  • In the case of parametrisation (d), \(z=0\) forces \(v=0\) and \(v=0\) forces \(x=0\) and \(y=u\text{.}\) As \(u\) varies that sweeps out the \(y\)-axis, which is what we want.

Furthermore

  • we are told that \(z=v\) runs from \(0\) to \(5\) and that
  • \(\displaystyle x^2+y^2=u^2\ge 4\)

So we want parametrisation (d) with domain \(|u|\ge 2\text{,}\) \(0\le v\le 5\text{.}\)

(e) Straight lines have curvature \(0\text{.}\) So one acceptable parametrized curve is \(\vr(t) = t\,\hi\text{,}\) \(0\le t\le 1\text{.}\)

(f) The cube \(S\) has six sides. So the outward flux through \(\partial S\) is \(6\) and, by the divergence theorem,

\begin{align*} 6 &= \dblInt_{\partial S}\vF\cdot\hn\,\dee{S} =\tripInt_S \vnabla\cdot\vF\,\dee{V} = \tripInt_S C\,\dee{V} = C \end{align*}

since \(S\) has volume one. So \(C=6\text{.}\)

(g) For the vector field \(\vF\) to be conservative, we need

\begin{alignat*}{2} & & \frac{\partial\vF_1}{\partial y} &= \frac{\partial \vF_2}{\partial x}\\ &\iff\qquad & \frac{\partial }{\partial y}(ax+by) &= \frac{\partial }{\partial x}(cx+dy)\\ &\iff\qquad & b&=c \end{alignat*}

When \(b=c\text{,}\) an allowed potential is \(\frac{a}{2}x^2 +bxy + \frac{d}{2}y^2\text{.}\) The specified set is

\begin{gather*} \Set{(a,b,c,d)}{a,b,c,d\text{ all real and }b=c} \end{gather*}

(h) By the definition of arclength parametrisation, the arclength along the curve between \(\vr(0)\) and \(\vr(s)\) is \(s\text{.}\) In particular, the arclength between \(\vr(0)\) and \(\vr(3)\) is \(3\) and the arclength between \(\vr(0)\) and \(\vr(5)\text{,}\) which is the same as the arclength between \(\vr(0)\) and \(\vr(3)\) plus the arclength between \(\vr(3)\) and \(\vr(5)\text{,}\) is \(5\text{.}\) So the arclength between \(\vr(3)\) and \(\vr(5)\) is \(5-3=2\text{.}\)

(i) In this solution, we'll use, for example \(-T\) to refer to the curve \(T\text{,}\) but with the arrow pointing in the opposite direction to that of the arrow on \(T\text{.}\)

In parts (2), (3) and (4) we will choose \(\vF\) to be the vector field

\begin{equation*} \vG(x,y) = -\frac{y}{x^2+y^2}\hi + \frac{x}{x^2+y^2}\hj \end{equation*}

We saw, in Example 13.3.14, that \(\vnabla\times\vG=\vZero\) except at the origin where it is not defined. We also saw, in Example 15.3.8, that \(\oint_{C}\vG\cdot\dee{\vr}=2\pi\) for any counterclockwise oriented circle centred on the origin.

  • (1): Let \(\cR_1\) be the region between \(S\) and \(T\text{.}\) It is the shaded region in the figure on the left below. Note that \(\cR_1\) is contained in the domain of \(\vF\text{,}\) so that \(\vnabla\times\vF=\vZero\) on all of \(\cR_1\text{.}\) The boundary of \(\cR_1\) is \(S-T\text{,}\) meaning that the boundary consists of two parts, with one part being \(S\) and the other part being \(-T\text{.}\) So, by Stokes' theorem

    \begin{equation*} \int_S \vF\cdot\dee{\vr} - \int_T \vF\cdot\dee{\vr} =\int_{\partial \cR_1} \vF\cdot\dee{\vr} =\dblInt_{\cR_1} \vnabla\times\vF\cdot\hk\, \dee{S} =0 \end{equation*}

    and (1) is true.

  • (2): False. Choose a coordinate system so that \(Q\) is at the origin and choose \(\vF=\vG\text{.}\) We saw, in Examples 13.3.14 and 15.3.8, that the curl of \(\vG\) vanished everywhere except at the origin, where it was not defined, but that \(\int_R \vG\cdot\dee{\vr} \ne 0\text{.}\)

  • (3),(4): False. Here is a counterxample that shows that both (3) and (4) are false. Choose a coordinate system so that \(Q\) is at the origin and choose \(\vF=\vG\text{.}\) By Stokes' theorem

    \begin{equation*} \int_S \vG\cdot\dee{\vr} = \int_T \vG\cdot\dee{\vr} = 0 \end{equation*}

    because \(\vnabla\times\vG=\vZero\) everywhere inside \(S\text{,}\) including at \(P\text{.}\) So now both parts (3) and (4) reduce to the claim that \(\int_U \vG\cdot\dee{\vr} = \int_R \vG\cdot\dee{\vr}\text{.}\)

    We saw, in Example 15.3.8, that \(\int_R \vG\cdot\dee{\vr} = 2\pi\text{.}\)

    To finish off the counterexample, we'll now show that \(\int_U \vG\cdot\dee{\vr} = -2\pi\text{.}\) Let \(\cR_2\) be the region between \(U\) and \(R\text{.}\) It is the shaded region in the figure on the right above. Note that \(\vnabla\times\vG=\vZero\) on all of \(\cR_2\text{.}\) including at \(P\text{.}\) The boundary of \(\cR_2\) is \(-U-R\text{,}\) meaning that the boundary consists of two parts, with one part being \(-U\) and the other part being \(-R\text{.}\) So, by Stokes' theorem

    \begin{equation*} -\int_U \vG\cdot\dee{\vr} - \int_R \vG\cdot\dee{\vr} =\int_{\partial \cR_2} \vG\cdot\dee{\vr} =\dblInt_{\cR_2} \vnabla\times\vG\cdot\hk\, \dee{S} =0 \end{equation*}

    and \(\int_U \vG\cdot\dee{\vr} =-\int_R \vG\cdot\dee{\vr} = -2\pi\)

  • (5): False. For any conservative vector field \(\vF\text{,}\) with potential \(f\text{,}\) \(\int_V \vF\cdot\dee{\vr}\) is just the difference of the values of \(f\) at the two end points of \(V\text{.}\) It is easy to choose an \(f\) for which those two values are different. For example \(f(x,y)=x\) does the job.

(j) Let \(S\) be any closed surface and denote by \(V\) the volume that it encloses. Presumably the question assumes that \(S\) is oriented so that \(S=\partial V\text{.}\) Then by the divergence theorem

\begin{equation*} \dblInt_S \vF \cdot \hn\,\dee{S} =\dblInt_{\partial V} \vF \cdot \hn\,\dee{S} =\tripInt_V \vnabla\cdot\vF\ \dee{V} \end{equation*}

This is exactly the volume of \(V\) if \(\vnabla\cdot\vF=1\) everywhere. One vector field \(\vF\) with \(\vnabla\cdot\vF=1\) everywhere is \(\vF=x\,\hi\text{.}\)

(k) Let \(\cC\) be the counterclockwise boundary of a small square centred on \(P\text{,}\) like the blue curve in the figure below, but much smaller. Call the square (the inside of \(\cC\)) \(S\text{.}\)

By Stokes' theorem

\begin{equation*} \dblInt_S \vnabla\times\vF\cdot\hk\ \dee{S} =\oint_\cC \vF\cdot\dee{\vr} \end{equation*}
  • The contribution to \(\oint_\cC \vF\cdot\dee{\vr}\) coming from the left and right sides of \(\cC\) will be zero, because \(\vF\) is perpendicular to \(\dee{\vr}\) there.
  • The contribution to \(\oint_\cC \vF\cdot\dee{\vr}\) coming from the top of \(\cC\) will be negative, because there \(\vF\) is a positive number times \(\hi\) and \(\dee{\vr}\) is a negative number times \(\hi\text{.}\)
  • The contribution to \(\oint_\cC \vF\cdot\dee{\vr}\) coming from the bottom of \(\cC\) will be positive, because there \(\vF\) is a positive number times \(\hi\) and \(\dee{\vr}\) is a positive number times \(\hi\text{.}\)
  • The magnitude of the contribution from the top of \(\cC\) will be larger than the magnitude of the contribution from the bottom of \(\cC\text{,}\) because \(|\vF|\) is larger on the top than on the bottom.

So, all together, \(\oint_\cC \vF\cdot\dee{\vr} \lt 0\text{,}\) and consequently (taking a limit as the square size tends to zero) \(\vnabla\times\vF\cdot\hk\) is negative at \(P\text{.}\)
