(a) If \((x,y,z)\ne\vZero\text{,}\)

\begin{align*} &\vnabla\cdot\vF(x,y,z) =\pdiff{}{x}\frac{x}{{\big[x^2+y^2+z^2\big]}^{3/2}} +\pdiff{}{y}\frac{y}{{\big[x^2+y^2+z^2\big]}^{3/2}}\\ &\hskip1.25in +\pdiff{}{z}\frac{z}{{\big[x^2+y^2+z^2\big]}^{3/2}}\\ &\hskip0.25in =\frac{\big[x^2+y^2+z^2\big]-x\frac{3}{2}(2x)}{{\big[x^2+y^2+z^2\big]}^{5/2}} +\frac{\big[x^2+y^2+z^2\big]-y\frac{3}{2}(2y)}{{\big[x^2+y^2+z^2\big]}^{5/2}}\\ &\hskip1.25in +\frac{\big[x^2+y^2+z^2\big]-z\frac{3}{2}(2z)}{{\big[x^2+y^2+z^2\big]}^{5/2}}\\ &\hskip0.25in =\frac{3\big[x^2+y^2+z^2\big]-3x^2-3y^2-3z^2}{{\big[x^2+y^2+z^2\big]}^{5/2}}\\ &\hskip0.25in=0 \end{align*}

If \((x,y,z)=\vZero\text{,}\) \(\vF(x,y,z)\) is not defined and hence \(\vnabla\cdot\vF(x,y,z)\) is also not defined.

(b) Let \(a \gt 0\text{.}\) Write \(\sigma_a = \Set{(x,y,z)}{x^2+y^2+z^2 = a^2}\text{.}\) The outward unit normal to \(\sigma_a\) is \(\hn = \frac{\vr}{|\vr|}\) so that

\begin{align*} \int_{\sigma_a}\vF\cdot\hn\, \dee{S} &=\int_{|\vr|=a} \frac{\vr}{|\vr|^3}\cdot \frac{\vr}{|\vr|}\ \dee{S} =\int_{|\vr|=a} \frac{1}{|\vr|^2}\ \dee{S} =\frac{1}{a^2}\int_{|\vr|=a}\dee{S}\\ &=\frac{1}{a^2}\big(4\pi a^2\big) =4\pi\ne 0 \end{align*}

(c) No, the results of (a) and (b) do not contradict the divergence theorem. One hypothesis of the divergence theorem is that \(\vnabla\cdot\vF\) (in fact all first order derivatives of \(\vF\)) be defined and continuous throughout the solid that \(\vnabla\cdot\vF\) is to be integrated over. That hypothesis is violated in this case.

(d) Let's first figure out what the surface \(z^2 - x^2 - y^2 + 1 = 0\text{,}\) i.e. the surface \(x^2 + y^2 = 1 +z^2\text{,}\) looks like. For each \(z_0\text{,}\) the \(z=z_0\) cross-section of this surface is the circle \(x^2+y^2=1+z_0^2\text{.}\) The radius of this circle is \(1\) when \(z_0=0\) and grows as \(|z_0|\) increases. So the solid region \(E\) looks like an hourglass drum, as sketched in the figure on the left below.

We are going to use the divergence theorem to compute the flux of \(\vF\) out through the surface \(\sigma\) of \(E\text{.}\) However we cannot apply the divergence theorem using \(E\) as the solid, because \(\vF\) is not defined at the origin, \((0,0,0)\text{,}\) which is a point in \(E\text{.}\) So we pick any \(0 \lt a \lt 1\text{,}\) and define the auxiliary solid

\begin{equation*} E_a = \Set{(x,y,z)}{ x^2+y^2+z^2\ge a^2,\ x^2+y^2\le 1+z^2,\ -1\le z\le 1} \end{equation*}

The solid \(E_a\) is constructed from the solid \(E\) by removing the ball \(x^2+y^2+z^2\le a^2\) from it. A side view of \(E_a\) is sketched in the figure on the right above. As in part (b), denote by \(\sigma_a\) the surface \(x^2+y^2+z^2=a^2\) with outward pointing normal. Then the boundary of \(E_a\) is \(\partial E_a=\sigma-\sigma_a\text{,}\) meaning that it consists of two parts. One part is the boundary, \(\sigma\text{,}\) of \(E\text{,}\) with outward pointing normal. The other part is the surface \(x^2+y^2+z^2=a^2\text{,}\) but with normal pointing into the sphere, opposite to the normals for \(\sigma_a\text{.}\) Consequently the divergence theorem gives

\begin{gather*} 0=\tripInt_{E_a} \vnabla \cdot \vF\,\dee{V} =\dblInt_{\partial E_a} \vF\cdot\hn\,\dee{S} = \dblInt_{\sigma} \vF\cdot\hn\,\dee{S} -\dblInt_{\sigma_a} \vF\cdot\hn\,\dee{S} \end{gather*}

so that, by part (b)

\begin{gather*} \dblInt_{\sigma} \vF\cdot\hn\,\dee{S} =\dblInt_{\sigma_a} \vF\cdot\hn\,\dee{S} =4\pi \end{gather*}

(e) The equation \(z^2 - x^2 - y^2 + 4y - 3 = 0\) can be rewritten as

\begin{equation*} x^2 + (y-2)^2 = 1+z^2 \end{equation*}

As is part (d), for each \(z_0\text{,}\) the \(z=z_0\) cross-section of this surface is a circle \(x^2+(y-2)^2=1+z_0^2\) of radius \(\sqrt{1+z_0^2}\text{.}\) But this circle is centred at \((0,2,z_0)\text{,}\) whereas the corresponding circle in part (d) was centred at \((0,0,z_0)\text{.}\) The solid \(R\) again has the shape of an hourglass drum. But while the origin \((0,0,0)\) was in \(E\text{,}\) it is not in

\begin{equation*} R=\Set{(x,y,z)}{x^2+(y-2)^2\le 1+z^2,\ -1\le z\le 1} \end{equation*}

So \(\vnabla \cdot \vF=0\) throughout all of \(R\) and the divergence theorem gives

\begin{gather*} \dblInt_{\Sigma} \vF\cdot\hn\,\dee{S} =\dblInt_{\partial R} \vF\cdot\hn\,\dee{S} =\tripInt_{R} \vnabla \cdot \vF\,\dee{V} =0 \end{gather*}
