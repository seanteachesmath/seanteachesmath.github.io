Solution 13.4.2.1.1.

The square has four sides, each of which is a line segment.

  • On the first side, \(y=0\) and \(\dee{y}=0\text{.}\) That is, we may parametrize the first side by \(\vr(x)=x\,\hi\) with \(0\le x\le 1\text{.}\)
  • On the second side, \(x=1\) and \(\dee{x}=0\text{.}\) We may parametrize the second side by \(\vr(y)=\hi+y\,\hj\) with \(0\le y\le 1\text{.}\)
  • On the third side, \(y=1\) and \(\dee{y}=0\text{.}\) We may parametrize the third side by \(\vr(x)=x\,\hi+\hj\) with \(x\) running from \(1\) to \(0\text{.}\)
  • On the final side, \(x=0\) and \(\dee{x}=0\text{.}\) We may parametrize the fourth side by \(\vr(y)=y\,\hj\) with \(y\) running from \(1\) to \(0\text{.}\)

So

\begin{align*} \int_\cC x^2y^2\,\dee{x}+x^3y\,\dee{y} &=\int_0^1 x^2\times 0^2\,\dee{x} +\int_0^1 1^3\times y\,\dee{y} +\int_1^0 x^2\times 1^2\,\dee{x}\\ &\hskip2in+\int_1^0 0^3\times y\,\dee{y}\\ &=\frac{1}{2}-\frac{1}{3} =\frac{1}{6} \end{align*}
