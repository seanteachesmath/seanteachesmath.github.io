Our limit has the indeterminate form \(1^{\infty}\text{;}\) as with l'HÃ´pital's rule, we can change it to a friendlier form using the natural logarithm.

\begin{align*} f(x)&=\left(1+x+x^2\right)^{2/x}\\ \log(f(x))&=\log\left[\left(1+x+x^2\right)^{2/x}\right]=\frac{2}{x}\log\left(1+x+x^2\right) \end{align*}

Recall \(\log(1+y)=\displaystyle\sum_{n=1}^\infty \frac{(-1)^{n+1}y^n}{n}\text{,}\) and set \(y=x+x^2\text{.}\) The series converges when \(|y| \lt 1\text{,}\) and since we only consider values of \(x\) that are very close to 0, we can assume \(|x+x^2| \lt 1\text{.}\)

\begin{align*} \log(f(x))&=\frac{2}{x}\log\left(1+(x+x^2)\right)=\frac{2}{x}\sum_{n=1}^\infty\frac{(-1)^{n+1}(x+x^2)^n}{n}\\ &=\frac{2}{x}\left[(x+x^2)-\frac{(x+x^2)^2}{2}+\frac{(x+x^2)^3}{3}-\cdots\right]\\ &=2+2x-\frac{(x+x^2)^2}{2x}+\frac{(x+x^2)^3}{3x}-\cdots\\ &=2+2x-\frac{(x^2+x)(1+x)}{2}+\frac{(x^2+x)^2(1+x)}{3}-\cdots \end{align*}

so that

\begin{align*} \amp\lim_{x \to 0}\log(f(x))\\ \amp\hskip0.25in=\lim_{x \to 0}\left[2+2x-\frac{(x^2+x)(1+x)}{2}+\frac{(x^2+x)^2(1+x)}{3}-\cdots\right]\\ &\hskip0.25in=2+0+0\cdots=2 \end{align*}

and

\begin{align*} \lim_{x \to 0} f(x)&=\lim_{x \to 0}e^{\log f(x)}=e^2 \end{align*}
