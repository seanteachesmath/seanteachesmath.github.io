We can choose the surface \(S\) to be the union of two flat parts:

  • the rectangle \(S_x\) in the \(xz\)-plane with vertices \((0,0,0)\text{,}\) \((\frac{\pi}{2},0,0)\text{,}\) \((\frac{\pi}{2},0,1)\) and \((0,0,1)\) and
  • the rectangle \(S_y\) in the \(yz\)-plane with vertices \((0,0,0)\text{,}\) \((0,0,1)\text{,}\) \((0,\frac{\pi}{2},1)\) and \((0,\frac{\pi}{2},0)\)

The normal to \(S_x\) is \(-\hj\) and the normal to \(S_y\) is \(-\hi\text{.}\) Then Stokes' theorem gives

\begin{align*} \int_C\vF\cdot\dee{\vr} &=\dblInt_S \vnabla\times\vF\cdot\hn\,\dee{S}\\ &=\dblInt_{S_x} \vnabla\times\vF\cdot(-\hj)\,\dee{S} + \dblInt_{S_y} \vnabla\times\vF\cdot(-\hi)\,\dee{S}\\ &=\int_0^{\frac{\pi}{2}} \dee{x}\int_0^1\dee{z}\ z^2 + \int_0^{\frac{\pi}{2}} \dee{y}\int_0^1\dee{z}\ z^2\\ &=\int_0^{\frac{\pi}{2}} \dee{x}\ \frac{1}{3} + \int_0^{\frac{\pi}{2}} \dee{y}\ \frac{1}{3}\\ &=\frac{\pi}{3} \end{align*}
