\begin{align*} \int \left(\ddiff{2}{y}{x}\right)\,\dee{x}&=\int\left(\frac{2}{y^3}\cdot\diff{y}{x}\right)\,\dee{x}\\ \end{align*}

The right integral is in exactly the form we would use for a change of variables (substitution) to \(y\text{.}\)

\begin{align*} \diff{y}{x}&=\int\left(\frac{2}{y^3}\right)\,\dee{y} = -\frac{1}{y^2}+C\\ \end{align*}

When \(y=1\text{,}\) \(\diff{y}{x}=3\text{.}\)

\begin{align*} 3& =-\frac{1}{1}+C\\ C&=4\\ \end{align*}

So,

\begin{align*} \diff{y}{x}&=-\frac{1}{y^2}+4\\ \end{align*}

This is a separable differential equation.

\begin{align*} \diff{y}{x}&=\frac{4y^2-1}{y^2}\\ \frac{y^2}{4y^2-1}\,\dee{y}&=\dee{x}\\ \int \frac{y^2}{4y^2-1}\,\dee{y}&=\int \dee{x}\tag{$*$} \end{align*}
