Solution 16.2.3.1.

(a) False. \(\vr''(t)\) is the full acceleration. So \(|\vr''(t)|\) is the magnitude of the full acceleration, not just the tangential component of acceleration. For example, if \(\vr(t) =\cos t\,\hi+\sin t\,\hj\) (i.e. the particle is just going around in circles), the acceleration \(\vr''(t) = -\cos t\,\hi-\sin t\,\hj\) is perpendicular to the direction of motion. So the tangential component of acceleration is zero, while \(|\vr''(t)|=1\text{.}\)

(b) \(\hat\vT(t)\) is the tangent vector to the curve at \(\vr(t)\text{.}\) \(\hat\vN(t)\) and \(\hat\vB(t)\) are both perpendicular to \(\hat\vT(t)\) (and to each other) and so span the plane normal to the curve at \(\vr(t)\text{.}\)

(c) True. This is (half of) Theorem 13.4.8.

(d) False. The statement \(\vnabla\times(\vnabla\cdot F) = 0\) is just plain gibberish, because \(\vnabla\cdot F\) is a scalar valued function and there is no such thing as the curl of a scalar valued function.

(e) False. For example if \(\vF=\hi\text{,}\) then, by the divergence theorem,

\begin{equation*} \dblInt_S \vF \cdot\hn\,\dee{S} =\tripInt_V\vnabla\cdot\vF\,\dee{V}=0 \end{equation*}

Here \(V=\Set{x,y,z}{x^2+y^2+z^2\le 1}\) is the inside of the sphere.

(f) True. If \(S\) is the boundary of the solid region \(E\text{,}\) then we can orient \(S\) by always choosing the normal vector that points into \(E\text{.}\)
