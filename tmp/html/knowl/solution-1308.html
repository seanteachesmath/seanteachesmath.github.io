Solution 8.3.11.24.1.

Factorials grow super fast. Like, wow, really fast. Even faster than exponentials. So the terms are going to zero, and the divergence test won't help us. Let's use ratio â€” it's a good go-to test with factorials.

\begin{align*} \frac{a_{k+1}}{a_k}&=\frac{\frac{e^{k+1}}{(k+1)!}}{\frac{e^k}{k!}}=\frac{e^{k+1}}{e^k}\cdot\frac{k!}{(k+1)!}\\ \amp= e\cdot\frac{k(k-1)\cdots(1)}{(k+1)(k)(k-1)\cdots(1)}=e\cdot\frac{1}{k+1}=\frac{e}{k+1}\\ \end{align*}

Since \(e\) is a constant,

\begin{align*} \lim_{k \rightarrow \infty}\frac{a_{k+1}}{a_k}&=\lim_{k \rightarrow \infty}\frac{e}{k+1}=0 \end{align*}

Since \(0 \lt 1\text{,}\) by the ratio test, the series converges.
