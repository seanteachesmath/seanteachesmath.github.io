(a) True. By the vector identity of Theorem 15.1.5.c,

\begin{gather*} \vnabla\times(f \vnabla f ) =(\vnabla f) \times (\vnabla f) +f\,\vnabla\times (\vnabla f) =\vZero \end{gather*}

The second term vanished because of the screening test vector identity of Theorem 15.1.7.b.

(b) True. That's the vector identity of Theorem 15.1.4.c.

(c) True. To have constant curvature \(0\) the curve must have unit tangent vector \(\hat\vT(s)\) obeying

\begin{equation*} \diff{\hat\vT}{s}(s)=\vZero \end{equation*}

(See §12.5.) So \(\vr'(s) = \hat\vT(s)\) must be a constant vector. Call it \(\hat\vT_0\text{.}\) Integrating gives

\begin{equation*} \vr(s) = s\hat\vT_0 +\vr_0 \end{equation*}

for some constant vector \(\vr_0\text{.}\) So \(\vr(s)\) lies on the same straight line for all \(s\text{.}\)

(d) False. The trap here is that the curve is in \(\bbbr^3\text{,}\) not \(\bbbr^2\text{.}\) As we saw in Example 12.4.4, a helix has constant curvature, but does not lie in a plane and so is not part of a circle.

(e) True. The vector field \(\vF=\vnabla f\) is conservative. So, by Theorem 13.4.7.b, the work integral

\begin{equation*} \int_C\vnabla f\cdot\dee{\vr} =\int_C\vF\cdot\dee{\vr} =0 \end{equation*}

for any closed curve \(C\text{,}\) and, in particular, for any circle \(C\text{.}\)

(f) True. The statement that “the flux out of one hemisphere is equal to the flux into the opposite hemisphere” is equivalent to the statement that “the flux out of the sphere is equal to zero”. Since \(\vnabla\cdot\vF=0\) everywhere, that is true by the divergence theorem.

(g) True. Let \(S\) be the boundary of the solid region \(V\text{.}\) Then, by the divergence theorem (Theorem 15.2.9),

\begin{gather*} \dblInt_S\vnabla\times\vF\cdot\hn\,\dee{S} =\tripInt_V \vnabla\cdot\big(\vnabla\times\vF\big)\,\dee{V} \end{gather*}

But \(\vnabla\cdot\big(\vnabla\times\vF\big)\) is identically zero, by the screening test vector identity of Theorem 15.1.7.a. So the integral is zero.
