(a) No. \(z=\sin\phi\sin \theta\) is negative when \(0 \lt \phi\le\frac{\pi}{4}\text{,}\) \(\pi \lt \theta \lt 2\pi\text{.}\)

(b) Yes. Note that \(x^2+\big(-y\big)^2+\big(\sqrt{2-x^2-y^2}\big)^2=2\) and that, for \(x^2+y^2\le 1\text{,}\) we have both \(x^2+(-y)^2\le 1\) and \(\sqrt{2-x^2-y^2}\ge 0\text{.}\)

(c) No. \((u\sin\theta)^2+(u\cos\theta)^2=u^2 \gt 1\) for \(1 \lt u\le 2\text{.}\) Also \(\sqrt{2-u^2}\) is not defined for \(\sqrt{2} \lt u\le 2\text{.}\)

(d) Yes. Note that

  • \(\displaystyle \big(\sqrt{2}\sin \phi\cos\theta\big)^2 +\big(\sqrt{2}\sin \phi\sin\theta\big)^2 +\big(\sqrt{2}\cos \phi\big)^2=2\)
  • For \(0\le\phi\le\frac{\pi}{4}\text{,}\) we have \(z=\sqrt{2}\cos \phi \gt 0\text{.}\)
  • As \(\phi\) runs from \(0\) to \(\frac{\pi}{4}\text{,}\) \(r(\phi)=\sqrt{2}\sin\phi\) runs from \(0\) to \(1\text{,}\) so that \(\big(x=r(\phi)\cos\theta\,,\,y=r(\phi)\sin\theta\big)\) covers all of \(x^2+y^2\le1\) as \(\phi\) runs from \(0\) to \(\frac{\pi}{4}\) and \(\theta\) runs from \(0\) to \(2\pi\text{.}\)

(e) Yes. Note that

  • \(\displaystyle \big(-\sqrt{2-z^2}\sin \phi\big)^2 +\big(\sqrt{2-z^2}\cos \phi\big)^2 +\big(z\big)^2=2\)
  • For \(1\le z\le\sqrt{2}\text{,}\) we have obviously have \(z \gt 0\text{.}\)
  • As \(z\) runs from \(1\) to \(\sqrt{2}\text{,}\) \(r(z)=\sqrt{2-z^2}\) runs from \(1\) to \(0\text{,}\) so that \(\big(x=-r(z)\sin\phi\,,\,y=r(z)\cos\phi\big)\) covers all of \(x^2+y^2\le1\) as \(z\) runs from \(1\) to \(\sqrt{2}\) and \(\phi\) runs from \(0\) to \(2\pi\text{.}\)
