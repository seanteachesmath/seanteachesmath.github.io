Step 2: factor \(\mathbf{x^4+1}\). For any real \(x\text{,}\) note \(x^4+1 \gt 0\text{.}\) Since it has no roots, it has no linear factors. That means it factors as the product of two irreducible quadratics. That is,

\begin{align*} x^4+1&=(ax^2+bx+c)(dx^2+ex+f)\\ \end{align*}

Since the coefficient of \(x^4\) on the left-hand is 1, we may assume \(a=d=1\text{.}\)

\begin{align*} x^4+1&=(x^2+bx+c)(x^2+ex+f)\\ \end{align*}

Since the constant term is 1, \(cf=1\text{.}\) That is, \(f = \frac{1}{c}\text{.}\)

\begin{align*} x^4+1&=(x^2+bx+c)(x^2+ex+1/c)\\ &=x^4 + \underbrace{\vphantom{\bigg(}(b+e)}_{(1)}x^3+\underbrace{\left(\frac{1}{c}+be+c\right)}_{(3)}x^2+\underbrace{\left(\frac{b}{c}+ec\right)}_{(2)}x+1 \end{align*}
  1. The coefficient of \(x^3\) tells us \(e=-b\text{.}\)
  2. Then the coefficient of \(x\) tells us \(0=\frac{b}{c}+ec = \frac{b}{c}-bc\text{.}\) So, \(c = \frac{1}{c}\text{,}\) hence \(c=\pm 1\text{.}\)
  3. Finally, the coefficient of \(x^2\) tells us \(0=\frac{1}{c}+be + c = \frac{1}{c}-b^2+c\text{.}\) Since \(-b^2\) is negative (or zero), \(\frac{1}{c}+c\) is positive, so \(c=1\text{.}\) That is, \(0=1-b^2+1\text{.}\) So, \(b = \sqrt{2}\text{.}\)

All together,

\begin{equation*} x^4+1 = (x^2+\sqrt{2}x+1)(x^2-\sqrt{2}x+1) \end{equation*}
