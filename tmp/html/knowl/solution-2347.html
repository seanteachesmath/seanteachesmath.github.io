Solution 15.3.1.17.1.

(a) The given integral is of the form \(\int_C F_1(x,y)\,\dee{x}+F_2(x,y)\,\dee{y}\) with

\begin{equation*} F_1(x,y) = \sqrt{1+x^3}\qquad F_2(x,y) = 2xy^2 + y^2\qquad \frac{\partial F_2}{\partial x} - \frac{\partial F_1}{\partial y} = 2y^2 \end{equation*}

As \(C\) is \(\partial R\) with

\begin{equation*} R = \Set{(x,y)}{ x^2+y^2\le 1} \end{equation*}

Green's theorem (Theorem 15.3.2) gives

\begin{align*} \int_C \sqrt{1+x^3}\,\dee{x} +\big(2xy^2 + y^2\big)\,\dee{y} & = \int_C F_1(x,y)\,\dee{x}+F_2(x,y)\,\dee{y}\\ & = \dblInt_R \Big(\frac{\partial F_2}{\partial x} - \frac{\partial F_1}{\partial y}\Big)\ \dee{x}\dee{y}\\ &= 2 \dblInt_R y^2\ \dee{x}\dee{y} \end{align*}

Switching to polar coordinates

\begin{align*} \int_C \sqrt{1+x^3}\,\dee{x} +\big(2xy^2 + y^2\big)\,\dee{y} &= 2\int_0^{2\pi}\dee{\theta} \int_0^1\dee{r}\ r\big(r\sin\theta\big)^2\\ &= 2\left[\int_0^{2\pi}\dee{\theta}\ \sin^2\theta\right] \left[\int_0^1\dee{r}\ r^3\right]\\ &=2\ \pi\ \frac{1}{4} =\frac{\pi}{2} \end{align*}

To do the \(\theta\) integral, we have used

\begin{gather*} \int_0^{2\pi} \sin^2 \theta\ \dee{\theta} =\int_0^{2\pi} \Big(\frac{1-\cos(2\theta)}{2}\Big)\,\dee{\theta} =\Big[\frac{\theta-\sin(2\theta)/2}{2} \Big]_0^{2\pi} = \pi \end{gather*}

For an efficient, sneaky, way to evaluate \(\int_0^{2\pi} \sin^2 \theta\ \dee{\theta}\text{,}\) see Example 13.4.4.

(b) It is again natural to use Green's theorem. But Green's theorem must be applied to a curve that is closed, so that it is the boundary of a region in \(\bbbr^2\text{.}\) The given curve \(C\) is not closed. But it is part of the boundary of

\begin{equation*} R = \Set{(x,y)}{x^2+y^2\le 1,\ x\ge 0} \end{equation*}

Here is a sketch of \(R\text{.}\)

The boundary of \(R\) consists of two parts â€” \(C\) on the right and the line segment \(L\) from \((0,1)\) to \((0,-1)\) on the left. Note that \(\vF=F_1\,\hi +F_2\,\hj\) is well-defined on all of \(R\) and that we still have, from part (a),

\begin{align*} \frac{\partial F_2}{\partial x} - \frac{\partial F_1}{\partial y} &=2y^2 \end{align*}

on all of \(R\text{.}\) So, by Green's theorem (Theorem 15.3.2),

\begin{align*} \int_C \sqrt{1+x^3}\,\dee{x} +\big(2xy^2 + y^2\big)\,\dee{y} &=\dblInt_R \Big(\frac{\partial F_2}{\partial x} - \frac{\partial F_2}{\partial y} \Big)\dee{x}\dee{y} - \int_L\vF\cdot\dee{\vr}\\ &=\dblInt_{\Atop{x^2+y^2\le 1}{x\ge 0}} 2y^2\ \dee{x}\dee{y} + \int_{-L} F_2\,\dee{y}\\ &=\dblInt_{x^2+y^2\le 1} y^2\ \dee{x}\dee{y} + \int_{-1}^1 y^2\,\dee{y}\\ &\hskip0.25in\text{by symmetry for the first integral and}\\ &\hskip0.25in\text{since $x=0$ and $\dee{x}=0$ in the}\\ &\hskip0.25in\text{second integral}\\ &=\frac{\pi}{4} + \frac{2}{3} \end{align*}

In the second line, we used the notation \(-L\) for the line segment from \((0,-1)\) to \((0,1)\text{.}\)
