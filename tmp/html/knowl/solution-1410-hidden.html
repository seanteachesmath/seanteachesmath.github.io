We'll use TheoremÂ 8.6.3 to bound the error in a partial-sum approximation. The error in the partial-sum approximation \(S_N\) is

\begin{equation*} E_N=\frac{f^{(N+1)}(c)}{(N+1)!}(x-a)^{N+1} \end{equation*}

for some \(c\) strictly between \(a\) and \(x\text{.}\) In our case, \(a=\dfrac12\text{,}\) \(x=-\dfrac1{3}\text{,}\) and we are given the \(n\)th derivative of \(f(x)\text{:}\)

\begin{align*} E_6&=\frac{\textcolor{blue}{f^{(7)}(c)}}{7!}\left(-\frac13-\frac12 \right)^{7}\\ &=\frac{1}{7!}\cdot\textcolor{blue}{\frac{6!}{2}\left[\left(1-c\right)^{-7}+(-1)^{6}\left(1+c\right)^{-7} \right]}\left(-\frac56 \right)^7\\ &=\frac{-5^7}{14\cdot 6^7}\cdot\left[\left(1-c\right)^{-7}+\left(1+c\right)^{-7} \right] \end{align*}

for some \(c\) in \((-\frac13,\frac1{2})\text{.}\)

We want to provide actual numeric bounds for this expression. That is, we want to find the absolute max and min of

\begin{equation*} E(c)=\frac{-5^7}{14\cdot 6^7}\cdot\left[\left(1-c\right)^{-7}+\left(1+c\right)^{-7} \right] \end{equation*}

over the interval \(\left( -\frac{1}{3},\frac{1}{2}\right)\text{.}\) Absolute extrema occur at endpoints and critical points. So, we'll start by differentiating \(E(c)\text{,}\) and finding its critical points (if any) in the interval \(\left( -\frac{1}{3},\frac{1}{2}\right)\text{.}\)

\begin{align*} E(c)&=\frac{-5^7}{14\cdot 6^7}\cdot\left[\left(1-c\right)^{-7}+\left(1+c\right)^{-7} \right]\\ E'(c)&=\frac{-5^7}{14\cdot 6^7}\cdot\left[7\left(1-c\right)^{-8}-7\left(1+c\right)^{-8} \right]=0\\ \left(1-c\right)^{-8}&=\left(1+c\right)^{-8}\\ 1-c&=1+c\\ c&=0 \end{align*}

Since \(E'(c)\) is defined over our entire interval, its only critical point is \(c=0\text{.}\)

  • \(\displaystyle E(0)=\dfrac{-5^7}{14\cdot 6^7}[2]\)
  • \(\displaystyle E\left(-\frac{1}{3}\right)=\dfrac{-5^7}{14\cdot 6^7}\left[\left(\frac43 \right)^{-7}+\left(\frac23 \right)^{-7} \right]=\dfrac{-5^7}{14\cdot 6^7}\left[\left(\frac34 \right)^{7}+\left(\frac32 \right)^{7} \right]\)
  • \(\displaystyle E\left(\frac{1}{2}\right)=\dfrac{-5^7}{14\cdot 6^7}\left[\left(\frac12 \right)^{-7}+\left(\frac32 \right)^{-7} \right]=\dfrac{-5^7}{14\cdot 6^7}\left[2^7+\left(\frac23 \right)^{7} \right]\)

We want to decide which of these numbers is biggest, and which smallest. Note that \(2^7\) is much, much bigger than \((3/2)^7\text{,}\) and both \((3/4)^7\) and \((2/3)^7\) are less than one. Furthermore, \((3/2)^7\) is much larger than 2. So: \(\left[2^7+(2/3)^7\right] \gt \left[(3/2)^7+(3/4)^7\right] \gt 2.\) Therefore,

\begin{equation*} \dfrac{-5^7}{14\cdot 6^7}\left[2^7+\left(\frac23 \right)^{7} \right] \lt \dfrac{-5^7}{14\cdot 6^7}\left[\left(\frac34 \right)^{7}+\left(\frac32 \right)^{7} \right] \lt \dfrac{-5^7}{14\cdot 6^7}[2] \end{equation*}

We conclude that the error \(E_6\) is in the interval

\begin{equation*} \left(\dfrac{-5^7}{14\cdot 6^7}\left[2^7+\left(\frac23 \right)^{7} \right]\quad,\quad \dfrac{-5^7}{14\cdot 6^7} [2]\right) \end{equation*}

or, equivalently,

\begin{equation*} \left(\dfrac{-5^7}{14\cdot 3^7}\left[1+\frac{1}{3^7} \right]\quad,\quad \dfrac{-5^7}{7\cdot 6^7} \right) \end{equation*}

which is approximately \(\left(-0.199, -0.040\right)\text{.}\)
