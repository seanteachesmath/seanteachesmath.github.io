Fortunately we can use the basic properties of integrals (Theorem 6.2.3(b) and (c)) to split \(\int_x^{x^2} e^{-t^2}\dee{t}\) into pieces whose derivatives we already know.

\begin{align*} \int_x^{x^2} e^{-t^2}\dee{t} &=\int_x^0 e^{-t^2}\dee{t}+\int_0^{x^2} e^{-t^2}\dee{t} &\text{by Theorem }\knowl{./knowl/thm_Intdomain.html}{\text{6.2.3}}\text{(c)}\\ &=-\int^x_0 e^{-t^2}\dee{t}+\int_0^{x^2} e^{-t^2}\dee{t} &\text{by Theorem }\knowl{./knowl/thm_Intdomain.html}{\text{6.2.3}}\text{(b)} \end{align*}

With this pre-processing, both integrals are of the right form. Using what we have learned in the the previous two examples,

\begin{align*} \diff{}{x}\int_x^{x^2} e^{-t^2}\dee{t} &=\diff{}{x}\left( -\int_0^{x} e^{-t^2}\dee{t} + \int_0^{x^2} e^{-t^2}\dee{t} \right)\\ &=-\diff{}{x}\int^x_0 e^{-t^2}\dee{t} +\diff{}{x}\int_0^{x^2} e^{-t^2}\dee{t}\\ &=- e^{-x^2} +2x e^{-x^4} \end{align*}
