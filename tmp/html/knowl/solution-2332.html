Solution 15.3.1.4.1.

The two partial derivatives

\begin{alignat*}{2} \pdiff{}{x}\Big( \frac{x}{x^2+y^2}\Big) &=\ \ \frac{(x^2+y^2)-x(2x)}{{(x^2+y^2)}^2} &&=\frac{y^2-x^2}{{(x^2+y^2)}^2}\\ \pdiff{}{y}\Big( \frac{-y}{x^2+y^2}\Big) &=\frac{-(x^2+y^2)-(-y)(2y)}{{(x^2+y^2)}^2} &&=\frac{y^2-x^2}{{(x^2+y^2)}^2} \end{alignat*}

are well-defined and equal everywhere except at the origin \((0,0)\text{.}\)

Short discussion: Were it not for the singularity at \((0,0)\text{,}\) the vector field of the last problem would be conservative and the integral \(\int\vF\cdot \dee{\vr}\) around any closed curve would be zero. But as we saw in parts (a) and (b) of Q[15.3.1.3], this is not the case. On the other hand, by Green's theorem (Theorem 15.3.2), the integral around the boundary of any region that does not contain \((0,0)\) is zero, as happened in part (c) of Q[15.3.1.3].

Long discussion: First consider part (c) of Q[15.3.1.3]. The curve \(C\) is the boundary of the region

\begin{equation*} R = \Set{(x,y)}{1\le x^2+y^2\le 2,\ y\ge 0} \end{equation*}

The partial derivatives \(\pdiff{}{x}\Big( \frac{x}{x^2+y^2}\Big)\) and \(\pdiff{}{y}\Big( \frac{-y}{x^2+y^2}\Big)\) are well-defined and equal everywhere in \(R\text{.}\) So by Green's theorem

\begin{align*} \frac{1}{2\pi}\oint_C \frac{x\,\dee{y}-y\,\dee{x}}{x^2+y^2} &=\frac{1}{2\pi}\dblInt_R\left[ \pdiff{}{x}\Big( \frac{x}{x^2+y^2}\Big) -\pdiff{}{y}\Big( \frac{-y}{x^2+y^2}\Big)\right]\dee{x}\, \dee{y}\\ &=0 \end{align*}

which is the answer we got before.

We cannot apply Green's theorem in this way for parts (a) and (b) of Q[15.3.1.3] because the singularity at \((0,0)\) is inside the curve \(C\) for both parts (a) and (b). On the other hand suppose, for simplicity, that \(0 \lt a \lt 1\text{.}\) Denote by \(C_a\text{,}\) \(C_b\) the curves of parts (a) and (b), respectively. Define \(R\) to be the set of points that are inside \(C_b\) and outside \(C_a\text{.}\) That is,

\begin{equation*} R=\Set{(x,y)}{-1\le x \le 1,\ -1\le y\le 1,\ x^2+y^2\ge a^2 } \end{equation*}

Then the boundary, \(\partial R\text{,}\) of \(R\) consists of two parts. One part is \(C_b\text{.}\) The other part is \(C_a\text{,}\) but oriented clockwise rather than counterclockwise. We'll call it \(-C_a\text{.}\)

Again the partial derivatives \(\pdiff{}{x}\Big( \frac{x}{x^2+y^2}\Big)\) and \(\pdiff{}{y}\Big( \frac{-y}{x^2+y^2}\Big)\) are well-defined and equal everywhere in \(R\text{.}\) So by Green's theorem

\begin{align*} \frac{1}{2\pi}\oint_{\partial R} \frac{x\,\dee{y}-y\,\dee{x}}{x^2+y^2} &=\frac{1}{2\pi}\dblInt_R\left[ \pdiff{}{x}\Big( \frac{x}{x^2+y^2}\Big) -\pdiff{}{y}\Big( \frac{-y}{x^2+y^2}\Big)\right]\dee{x}\, \dee{y}\\ &=0 \end{align*}

Consequently

\begin{align*} 0 &= \frac{1}{2\pi}\oint_{\partial R} \frac{x\,\dee{y}-y\,\dee{x}}{x^2+y^2} = \frac{1}{2\pi}\oint_{C_b} \frac{x\,\dee{y}-y\,\dee{x}}{x^2+y^2} + \frac{1}{2\pi}\oint_{-C_a} \frac{x\,\dee{y}-y\,\dee{x}}{x^2+y^2}\\ &=\frac{1}{2\pi}\oint_{C_b} \frac{x\,\dee{y}-y\,\dee{x}}{x^2+y^2} - \frac{1}{2\pi}\oint_{C_a} \frac{x\,\dee{y}-y\,\dee{x}}{x^2+y^2} \end{align*}

and we conclude that the answers to parts (a) and (b) should be the same.We did indeed see that in Q[15.3.1.3].
