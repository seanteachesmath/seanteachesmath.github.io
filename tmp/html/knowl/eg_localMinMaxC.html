Example 4.5.10. Local max and min of \(|x|\).

In this example, we'll look for local maxima and minima of the function

\begin{align*} f(x) = |x| = \begin{cases} x & \text{if }x \ge 0\\ -x & \text{if }x \lt 0 \end{cases} \end{align*}

on the interval \(-1\le x\le 1\text{.}\)

  • Again, start by computing the derivative (reread Example 3.2.10):
    \begin{align*} f'(x) = \begin{cases} 1 & \text{if }x \gt 0\\ \text{undefined} & \text{if }x = 0\\ -1 & \text{if }x \lt 0 \end{cases} \end{align*}
  • This derivative never takes the value \(0\text{,}\) so the function does not have any critical points. However the derivative does not exist at the point \(x=0\text{,}\) so that point is a singular point.

  • Here is a sketch of the graph of \(f(x)\text{.}\)

    From the figure we see that \(f(x)\) has a local (and in fact global) minimum at \(x=0\) despite the fact that \(f'(0)\) is not a critical point.

  • Reread Theorem 4.5.4 yet again. It says that, “if \(f(x)\) has a local maximum/minimum at \(x=c\) and if \(f\) is differentiable at \(x=c\), then \(f'(c)=0\)”. It says nothing about what happens at points where the derivative does not exist. Indeed that is why we have to consider both critical points and singular points when we look for maxima and minima.
