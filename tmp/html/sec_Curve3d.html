Section 12.4 Curves in Three Dimensions

So far, we have developed formulae for the curvature, unit tangent vector, etc., at a point \(\vr(t)\) on a curve that lies in the \(xy\)-plane. We now extend our discussion to curves in \(\bbbr^3\text{.}\) Fix any \(t\text{.}\) For \(t'\) very close to \(t\text{,}\) \(\vr(t')\text{,}\) will, by the Taylor expansion to second order, be very close to \(\vr(t) + \vr'(t)\,(t'-t) +\frac{1}{2}\vr'(t)\,(t'-t)^2\text{,}\) so that \(\vr(t')\) almost lies in the plane through \(\vr(t)\) that is determined by the two vectors \(\vr'(t)\) and \(\vr'(t)\text{.}\) Thus, if we restrict our attention to a very small part of the curve near the point of interest \(\vr(t)\text{,}\) the curve will, to a very good approximation lie in some plane. So we can still define, for example, the osculating circle to the curve at \(\vr(t)\) to be the circle in that plane that fits the curve best near \(\vr(t)\text{.}\) And we still have the formulae 1 

\begin{align*} \vv&=\diff{\vr}{t}=\diff{s}{t}\,\hat\vT\\ \diff{\hat\vT}{s} &= \ka\hat\vN\\ \diff{\hat\vT}{t} &= \ka\diff{s}{t}\hat\vN\\ \va&=\difftwo{\vr}{t}=\difftwo{s}{t}\,\hat\vT +\ka\Big(\diff{s}{t}\Big)^2\hat\vN\\ \vv\times\va &= \ka \Big(\diff{s}{t}\Big)^3\hat\vT\times\hat\vN \end{align*}

The only  2  difference is that \(\vv, \va, \hat\vT\) and \(\hat\vN\) are now three component vectors rather than two component vectors.

The arguments in the proof of Theorem 12.3.3 that we used to verify these formulae work in any plane, not just the \(xy\)-plane. Just choose \(\hi\) and \(\hj\) to be any two mutually perpendicular unit vectors in the plane.
However this can be a significant difference.

If we are lucky and our curve happens to lie completely in a single plane, the vectors \(\hat\vT(s)\) and \(\hat\vN(s)\) are mutually perpendicular unit vectors that lie in the same plane, so that their cross product \(\hat\vB(s) = \hat\vT(s)\times\hat\vN(s)\) is a unit vector that is perpendicular to the plane. By continuity, \(\hat\vB(s)\) has to be a constant vector, i.e. be independent of \(s\text{.}\)

If, on the other hand, \(\hat\vB(s)\) is not constant, then our curve doesn't lie in a single plane, and we can use the derivative

\begin{align*} \diff{\hat\vB}{s} &=\diff{}{s}\big(\hat\vT\times\hat\vN\big) =\diff{\hat\vT}{s}\times\hat\vN + \hat\vT\times \diff{\hat\vN}{s}\\ &=\hat\vT\times \diff{\hat\vN}{s}\qquad \Big( \text{since } \diff{\hat\vT}{s} \text{ is parallel to } \hat\vN \Big) \end{align*}

as a measure

  • of how badly the curve fails to lie in a plane,
  • i.e. how much the plane that fits the curve best near \(\vr(s)\) twists as \(s\) increases,

The cross product in \(\diff{\hat\vB}{s}=\hat\vT\times \diff{\hat\vN}{s}\) implies that \(\diff{\hat\vB}{s}\) is perpendicular to \(\hat\vT\text{.}\) In addition, \(\diff{\hat\vB}{s}\) must be perpendicular to \(\hat\vB\) because

\begin{equation*} |\hat\vB|=1 \implies 1=\hat\vB\cdot\hat\vB \implies 0 = \diff{}{s}\left[\hat\vB\cdot\hat\vB\right] = 2 \hat\vB\cdot\diff{\hat\vB}{s} \end{equation*}

So \(\diff{\hat\vB}{s}(s)\) must be parallel to \(\hat\vN(s)\text{.}\)

Definition 12.4.1.
  1. The binormal vector at \(\vr(s)\) is \(\hat\vB(s) = \hat\vT(s)\times \hat\vN(s)\text{.}\) The normal vector \(\hat\vN(s)\) is sometimes called the unit principal normal vector to distinguish it from the binormal vector.
  2. We define the torsion \(\tau(s)\) by
    \begin{equation*} \diff{\hat\vB}{s}(s) = -\tau(s)\hat\vN(s) \end{equation*}
    The negative sign is included so that \(\tau(s) \gt 0\) indicates “right handed twisting”. There will be an explanation of what this means in Example 12.4.4 below.
  3. The osculating plane at \(\vr(s)\) (the plane that fits the curve best at \(\vr(s)\)) is the plane through \(\vr(s)\) with normal vector \(\hat\vB(s)\text{.}\) The equation of the plane is
    \begin{equation*} \hat\vB(s)\cdot\big\{(x,y,z)-\vr(s)\big\}=0 \end{equation*}

For each \(s\text{,}\) \(\hat\vT(s)\text{,}\) \(\hat\vN(s)\) and \(\hat\vB(s)\) are mutually perpendicular unit vectors. They form an orthonormal basis for \(\bbbr^3\text{,}\) just as \(\hi\text{,}\) \(\hj\) and \(\hk\) form an orthonormal basis for \(\bbbr^3\text{.}\) Furthermore both \((\hat\vT(s)\,,\,\hat\vN(s)\,,\,\hat\vB(s))\) and \((\hi\,,\,\hj\,,\,\hk)\) are “right handed triples” 3 , meaning that \(\hat\vB(s) = \hat\vT(s)\times\hat\vN(s)\) and \(\hk=\hi\times\hj\text{.}\)

We shall stick to “right handed triples” to make it easier to get various signs right.

We have already computed \(\diff{\hat\vT}{s}\) and \(\diff{\hat\vB}{s}\text{.}\) It is now an easy matter to compute

\begin{align*} \diff{\hat\vN}{s} &= \diff{}{s}\big(\hat\vB(s)\times\hat\vT(s)\big)\\ &= -\tau(s)\hat\vN(s)\times\hat\vT(s) +\hat\vB(s)\times\big(\ka(s)\hat\vN(s)\big)\\ &=\tau(s)\hat\vB(s)-\ka(s)\hat\vT(s) \end{align*}

To see that \(\hat\vN(s)\times\hat\vT(s)=-\hat\vB(s)\) and \(\hat\vB(s)\times\hat\vN(s)=-\hat\vT(s)\text{,}\) just look at the right hand figure above.

Now suppose that we have a curve that is parametrized by \(t\) rather than \(s\text{.}\) How do we find the torsion \(\tau\text{?}\) The most obvious method is to

  • recall that \(\vv\times\va = \ka \big(\diff{s}{t}\big)^3\hat\vT\times\hat\vN = \ka \big(\diff{s}{t}\big)^3\hat\vB\) and that \(\hat\vB(t)\) is a unit vector. So
    \begin{equation*} \hat\vB(t) = \frac{\vv(t)\times\va(t)}{|\vv(t)\times\va(t)|} \end{equation*}
  • Having found \(\vB(t)\) we can differentiate it and use \(\diff{\hat\vB}{s}(s) = -\tau(s)\hat\vN(s)\) and the chain rule to give
    \begin{equation*} \diff{\vB}{t} = \diff{\vB}{s}\diff{s}{t} = -\tau\diff{s}{t} \hat\vN \end{equation*}
    from which we can read off \(\tau\text{,}\) provided we know \(\diff{s}{t}\) and \(\hat\vN\text{.}\)

There is another, often more efficient, method to find the torsion \(\tau\) that uses

\begin{align*} \diff{\va}{t} &= \diff{}{t}\Big(\difftwo{s}{t}\,\hat\vT +\ka\Big(\diff{s}{t}\Big)^2\hat\vN\Big)\\ &= \ddiff{3}{s}{t}\,\hat\vT +\difftwo{s}{t}\,\diff{s}{t}\,\ka\hat\vN +\diff{}{t}\Big(\ka\Big(\diff{s}{t}\Big)^2\Big)\hat\vN +\ka\Big(\diff{s}{t}\Big)^3 \big(\tau\hat\vB-\ka\hat\vT\big) \end{align*}

While this looks a little complicated, notice that, with just one exception, namely \(\ka\big(\diff{s}{t}\big)^3\tau(s)\hat\vB(s)\text{,}\) every term on the right hand side is either in the direction \(\hat\vT\) or in the direction \(\hat\vN\) and so is perpendicular to \(\hat\vB\text{.}\) So, dotting with \(\vv\times\va = \ka \big(\diff{s}{t}\big)^3\hat\vB\) gives

\begin{gather*} \big(\vv\times\va\big)\cdot \diff{\va}{t} = \ka^2 \Big(\diff{s}{t}\Big)^6\,\tau = |\vv\times\va|^2\,\tau \end{gather*}

and hence

\begin{gather*} \tau = \frac{\big(\vv\times\va\big)\cdot \diff{\va}{t} }{|\vv\times\va|^2} \end{gather*}

If the curvature 4  \(\ka(s) \gt 0\) and the torsion \(\tau(s)\) are known, then the system of equations 5 

As in two dimensions, if \(\ka(s)=0\text{,}\) then \(\hat\vN(s)\) is not defined. This makes even more sense in three dimensions than in two dimensions: if the curve is a straight line, there are infinitely many unit vectors perpendicular to it and there is no way to distinguish between them.
The equations are named after the two French mathematicians who independently discovered them: Jean Frédéric Frenet (1816–1900, the son of a wig maker), in his thesis of 1847 (actually he only gave two of the three equations), and Joseph Alfred Serret (1819–1885) in 1851.
Equation 12.4.2. Frenet–Serret Formulae.
\begin{align*} \diff{\hat\vT}{s}(s)&=\phantom{-}\ka(s)\ \hat\vN(s)\cr \diff{\hat\vN}{s}(s)&=\phantom{-}\tau(s)\ \hat\vB(s)-\ka(s)\ \hat\vT(s)\cr \diff{\hat\vB}{s}(s)&=-\tau(s)\ \hat\vN(s)\cr \end{align*}

is a first order linear system of ordinary differential equations

\begin{align*} \diff{}{s} \left[ \begin{matrix}\hat\vT(s) \\ \hat\vN(s)\\ \hat\vB(s)\end{matrix} \right] =\left[\begin{matrix} 0 & \ka(s) & 0 \\ -\ka(s) & 0 &\tau(s) \\ 0 &-\tau(s) & 0 \end{matrix}\right] \left[\begin{matrix}\hat\vT(s) \\ \hat\vN(s)\\ \hat\vB(s)\end{matrix}\right] \end{align*}

for the \(9\) component vector valued function \((\hat\vT(s)\,,\,\hat\vN(s)\,,\,\hat\vB(s))\text{.}\)

Any first order linear initial value problem

\begin{equation*} \diff{}{s}\vx(s) = M(s) \vx(s)\qquad \vx(0)=\vx_0 \end{equation*}

where \(\vx\) is an \(n\)-component vector and \(M(s)\) is an \(n\times n\) matrix with continuous entries, has exactly one solution. If \(n=1\text{,}\) so that \(\vx(s)\) and \(M(s)\) are just functions, this is easy to see. Just let \(\cM(s)\) be the antiderivative of \(M(s)\) that obeys \(\cM(0)=0\text{.}\) Then

\begin{align*} \diff{}{s}\vx(s) = M(s) \vx(s) &\iff e^{-\cM(s)}\diff{}{s}\vx(s) - M(s) e^{-\cM(s)} \vx(s)=0\\ &\iff \diff{}{s}\Big(e^{-\cM(s)}\vx(s)\Big) = 0 \end{align*}

by the product rule. So \(e^{-\cM(s)}\vx(s)\) is a constant independent of \(s\text{.}\) In particular \(e^{-\cM(s)}\vx(s)=e^{-\cM(0)}\vx(0)= \vx_0\) so that \(\vx(s) = \vx_0 e^{\cM(s)}\text{.}\) This argument can be generalized to any natural number \(n\text{.}\) But that is beyond the scope of this book.

Since the Frenet-Serret formulae constitute a first order system of ordinary differential equations for the vector \((\hat\vT(s)\,,\,\hat\vN(s)\,,\,\hat\vB(s))\) and since any first order linear initial value problem has a exactly one solution,

  • the vector valued function \((\hat\vT(s)\,,\,\hat\vN(s)\,,\,\hat\vB(s))\) is determined by the functions \(\ka(s)\) and \(\tau(s)\) (assuming that they are continuous) together with the initial condition \((\hat\vT(0)\,,\,\hat\vN(0)\,,\,\hat\vB(0))\text{.}\)
  • Furthermore, once you know \(\hat\vT(s)\text{,}\) then \(\vr(s)\) is determined by \(\vr(0)\) and \(\diff{\vr}{s}(s)=\hat\vT(s)\text{.}\)
  • So any smooth curve \(\vr(s)\) is completely determined by \(\vr(0)\text{,}\) \((\hat\vT(0)\,,\,\hat\vN(0)\,,\,\hat\vB(0))\text{,}\) \(\ka(s)\) and \(\tau(s)\text{.}\)
  • That is, up to translations (you can move between any two possible choices of \(\vr(0)\) by a translation) and rotations (you can move between any two possible choices of \((\hat\vT(0)\,,\,\hat\vN(0)\,,\,\hat\vB(0))\) by a rotation) a curve is completely determined by the curvature \(\ka(s) \gt 0\) and the torsion \(\tau(s)\text{.}\) This result is called “The fundamental theorem of space curves”.
Theorem 12.4.3. The Fundamental Theorem of Space Curves.

Let \(\ka(s) \gt 0\) and \(\tau(s)\) be continuous. Then up to translations and rotations, there is a unique curve with curvature \(\ka(s)\) and torsion \(\tau(s)\text{.}\)

Example 12.4.4. Right circular helix.

The right circular helix is the curve

\begin{gather*} \vr(t)= a\cos t\,\hi +a\sin t\,\hj + bt\,\hk \end{gather*}

with \(a,b \gt 0\) as in the figure on the left below.

Here is why it is called a right helix rather than a left helix. If the helix is the thread of a bolt that you are screwing into a nut, and you turn the bolt in the direction of the (curled) fingers of your right hand (as in the figure 6  on the right above), then it moves in the direction of your thumb (as in the long straight arrow of the figure on the right above).

This figure is a variant of this picture.

To determine the curvature and torsion of this curve we compute

\begin{align*} \vv(t)&= -a\sin t\,\hi +a\cos t\,\hj + b\,\hk\\ \va(t)&= -a\cos t\,\hi -a\sin t\,\hj\\ \diff{\va}{t}(t)&= a\sin t\,\hi -a\cos t\,\hj \end{align*}

From \(\vv(t)\) we read off

\begin{align*} \diff{s}{t}&=\sqrt{a^2+b^2}\\ \hat\vT(t)&= -\frac{a}{\sqrt{a^2+b^2}}\sin t\,\hi +\frac{a}{\sqrt{a^2+b^2}} \cos t\,\hj +\frac{b}{\sqrt{a^2+b^2}}\,\hk \end{align*}

From \(\va=\difftwo{s}{t}\,\hat\vT+\ka\big(\diff{s}{t}\big)^2\hat\vN =\ka(a^2+b^2)\hat\vN\text{,}\) we read off that

\begin{gather*} \ka(t)=\frac{a}{a^2+b^2}\qquad \hat\vN(t) = -\cos t\,\hi-\sin t\,\hj \end{gather*}

From

\begin{align*} \vv(t)\times\va(t) &= \det\left[ \begin{matrix} \hi & \hj & \hk\\ -a\sin t & a\cos t & b\\ -a\cos t &-a\sin t & 0\end{matrix} \right] = ab\sin t\,\hi -ab\cos t\,\hj +a^2\,\hk\\ |\vv(t)\times\va(t)|^2 &=a^2b^2+a^4 = a^2(a^2+b^2) \end{align*}

we read off

\begin{align*} \hat\vB(t) &= \frac{\vv(t)\times\va(t)}{|\vv(t)\times\va(t)|} = \frac{b}{\sqrt{a^2+b^2}}\sin t\,\hi -\frac{b}{\sqrt{a^2+b^2}} \cos t\,\hj +\frac{a}{\sqrt{a^2+b^2}}\,\hk \end{align*}

and

\begin{align*} \tau(t) & = \frac{\big(\vv\times\va\big)\cdot \diff{\va}{t} }{|\vv\times\va|^2} =\frac{a^2b}{a^2(a^2+b^2)} =\frac{b}{a^2+b^2} \end{align*}

Note that, for the right handed helix, \(\tau \gt 0\text{.}\) Finally the centre of curvature is

\begin{align*} \vr(t) +\frac{1}{\ka(t)}\hat\vN(t) &=\Big(a-\frac{a^2+b^2}{a}\Big)\cos t\,\hi +\Big(a-\frac{a^2+b^2}{a}\Big)\sin t\,\hi +bt\,\hk\\ &=-\frac{b^2}{a}\cos t\,\hi -\frac{b^2}{a}\sin t\,\hi +bt\,\hk \end{align*}

which is another helix. In the figure below, the red curve is the original helix and the blue curve is the helix traced by the centre of curvature.
